# Xie Baotou's GM Experience

Xie Baotou's tabletop experience

The first great principle: a tabletop game is a social game. The socializing and the roleplay are the point — everyone gathering to have a good time, such that you can all play together again, comes first. Break this principle so that the others can no longer stand playing with you, and that is the true critical failure of the table. Following the rules, respecting others, being a friend, keeping the time, not playing the murder-hobo, not wrecking the whole table's experience — all of these flow from this one principle.

The second great principle: be someone the team needs. Understand clearly what this recruitment is asking for, what the Storyteller needs, and what your teammates need; know what style this ST usually runs, and weigh your force accordingly through character creation, roleplay, and teamwork. Part of the trouble with overtuned and extreme characters lies here. Yes, your character is mighty and sweeps every enemy in one stroke; yes, you are distinctive and unlike the others, deeply enigmatic, coolly aloof and speaking to no one — but none of it helps our experience of the game, and it may well harm it. Far from thanking you, we will likely look for the quickest excuse to distance ourselves, drop you onto the blacklist, and carry on with our happy game.

The third principle: understand the team's and the Storyteller's intent. Intent breeds needs; intent breeds action. Know what the other side is running this campaign to achieve, what effect they're after. Say we want a cheerful spring-outing romp, and you play it in your usual grave and earnest style — this isn't to say you did wrong, nor that you lack skill, but that we truly have no need of such a presence, so kindly leave the group. Which brings us to ST–PC compatibility: whether you've grasped each other's intent, tended each other's needs, and whether you're able to produce the interplay everyone wants (say, you type too slowly, or don't understand the riddles) — did your actions consider your teammates' intent?

Ⅰ. On joining a table, both sides are urged to hold these attitudes:

For a goal: "through this group game, both sides create a captivating experience."

For a style: "respect for others, fair rules, friendly play, harmonious talk, courtesy and punctuality."

For an ending: "a full measure of group-play enjoyment and narrative experience — everyone goes home loaded with it."

Ⅱ. Understanding the game

. The Storyteller is responsible for presenting the players with [the meaningful key points of the present scene that bear on this session]

√ yes ST: "Look! A beauty!" # gave a suitably alluring description

√ yes ST: "Look! Treasure!" # described the look of the treasure's mechanism and offered clues

√ yes ST: "Look! The sea!" # introduced the sea's important places and important objectives

× no ST: # the objects mentioned, or the description given, carry no intent, clue, connection, or story weight whatsoever.

× no ST: # kept everyone waiting twenty minutes to produce a thousand characters of beautiful, entirely unimportant scenery.

× no ST: # mentioned nothing of importance in the environment, and excessively asked the players to act freely.

. Memorize your character card; consult it often at the table

× no Player: "What's my character's name?"

. Memorize the basics in the rulebook; for questions the rules can answer, please do.

× no Player: "What does the Strength Stat do?"

. Once you've done your part, ask and communicate about whatever still eludes you

yes Player: "GM, I can't find the material on this part"

. For out-of-game discussion, wrap the text in brackets ( ) to mark it as table talk, or hold it in another channel.

. Tearing the card: the character has died.

Ⅲ. Roleplaying actions

. Roleplay means interacting with the world in your character's identity

. When you roleplay, always use the third person

√ yes Player: "Angie walks forward"

× no Player: "I walk forward" (who?)

× no Player: "Walk forward" (this is not a video game)

. Whatever can be settled through roleplay, settle through roleplay as far as possible

× no Player: "(GM, what's here?)"

Storyteller: "(You could make a move — try looking around)"

yes Player: "Angie surveys the surroundings"

"Roleplay means experiencing the world and acting from the character's identity and viewpoint; its opposite is out-of-game activity — table talk, out-of-character activity, OC (Out of Character). In general we hope players will roleplay and sink into the story as much as they can. Table-side fun is part of the experience, but it should never stop anyone from roleplaying."

. As far as possible, act out the character's motions in the character's identity and from the character's view.

× no Player: "Go to the desk and finish reading the book"

yes Player: "Angie walks to the desk, picks up the book, and begins to read"

. Once you make a move, it becomes the fact that "you have made the attempt"

× no Player: "Storyteller, may I reach a hand toward the treasure?" (of course — it's your hand)

yes Player: "Angie reaches a hand toward the treasure"

. But how that attempt runs, and how it ends, is decided by checks and the working of the rules

Storyteller and rulebook: "But Angie fails to overcome 5 Frozen, and her arm cannot touch a treasure three meters away"

. You should attempt, not declare, the result of an action — the rules' working decides it.

× no Player: "I got the treasure, and inside is a divine shield"

× no Player: "I'm way cool — one punch bursts the dragon's head"

Storyteller: "Out."

Ⅳ. Team communication

Choose a character and a style of play that others can accommodate over the long run and keep playing beside; meet the team's bar, as far as you can, for the needed combat, conversation, and roleplaying ability. And in the story, weigh your actions against how the rest of the team feels — forever clashing with teammates, forever hurting them for no reason, and we may not be able to go on. In short: talk it over together.

Communicate as a team, and often! It makes you feel like a professional squad; when trouble comes, the whole group's intent grows more gathered and focused, the team scatters less, and you spare yourself and your teammates the sour moments.

Cooperation: you're one team — help each other within proper bounds, watch your teammates' backs, mind the teamwork, communicate, plan, share the ideas you strike on when a hard problem comes, and push through it together.

Action intent: if the Storyteller keeps fumbling resolutions, or your teammates keep acting against the plan, you might try stating your intent out-of-game beforehand, even in a few short words — attack, move, lights on, mechanism, flee, circle around… — or talk the tactics through with them properly.

Use whatever parts prove useful, freely.
