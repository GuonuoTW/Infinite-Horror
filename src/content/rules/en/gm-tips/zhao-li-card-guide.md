# Zhao Li's Advanced Card-Building Guide

**Foreword:**

A quick introduction first. I'm Zhao Li, an old hand a year and a half in the group, combat strength respectable — at least fit to compete on the same stage as a few of the veteran munchkins… and the group's designated face and conscience.

Every newcomer touching Infinite for the first time has asked some version of these: "Boss Roma, how do I build this card so it has any combat strength?" "Boss Tuanzi, how on earth did you stack that much DP with only D and C resources?" "You old rascal Shenxu, how could this amount of resources possibly beat a monster that strong — what?! You can flatten it on half the resources?" And when the newcomers want to dig deeper, the answer they usually get is: "Read the book! Go read the book. Read it yourself. Read the book or I'll cut you down!" (that last one is Manzi.)

As a PC, I understand deeply the miserable experience of grinding through the rulebook for a week to build a card, only to have it thrashed at the table by a card some veteran threw together in five minutes. So there has long been a call for the group's elders to publish some strong builds or common card-building lines. But speaking as an old hand and a developer of such lines, I don't want a parade of overtuned builds tempting PCs to abandon the rulebook and copy cards — still less do I want fixed formulas fencing in PCs' imaginations.

So friends who came purely to learn the munchkins' overtuned formulas may be disappointed: I don't care for cards built too mercenarily. This guide mainly shares my way of thinking about builds, garnished with a few munchkin lines long since nerfed dead, nerfed lame, or worn threadbare. I hope you draw some inspiration from it.

This guide targets Starlight's Infinite Horror TRPG version 2.30; where resources from other versions are cited, the text notes it.

**Part One: What kind of character am I building?**

Before building the card, your very first job is to size up the campaign: what is its nature, what level are your ST and teammates at, and what does the table demand of combat strength? Is it a casual romp, a story campaign, or a cutthroat one? I have always held that a TRPG is, at bottom, the ST and PCs telling a good story together; deliberately hogging anyone's share of the spotlight is wrong and hurts both sides' experience. Your card should match your ST's skill at running and your teammates' skill at building — above all in online games. Next, pin down exactly what character you're building. Under the current rules, one card covering every base is close to impossible — especially with support builds dead in this version — so a team should divide its labor: you handle Tech crafting, I handle Fantasy spellcasting, he handles the frontal assault, and Su Can handles backstabbing the party, and so on.

With the role fixed, next weigh the campaign's length, whether the ST runs steadily, how generous the resource payouts are, how the earnings are divided, and so on. Why? Because these determine how large a build the campaign can carry, how much you must set aside to survive the weak early phase if you're playing a late-game build, whether to respec, and when. A quick example: if a campaign expects four films and a final haul of around BBCC, then planning an EVA-pilot card is plainly unwise; if you mean to hoard a B Tier to buy the Heroic Spirit Alternation outright, consider how much you'll still have for early-game survival and a modest showing, or you'll spend the campaign feeling invisible. Of course, this step mainly applies to campaigns that hew to canon, care about combat strength, and have a full plan. If you've joined a casual romp, skip it — play whatever you love.

The next step is the heart of card-building for most people: which resource do I want to play? A good share of Infinite's players come precisely for the cosplay — I just want to play a Duelist; I desperately want the Magma Fruit with Scorched Earth — and here my advice is: follow your heart… The current version has next to no balance, but everything is playable; resources run strong and weak, and the beloved ones number only a few. If you love it, that's enough. Finally, to spare the group's titanium dog-eyes, I strongly advise against combinations too painful to look at…

**Part Two: How do I construct my ideal character?**

Right — this is the substantive part, and I'll take it apart with a few examples. With your role and resource chosen, the question becomes how to do your job: how do I deal damage? How do I take it? How do I stack detection level and range? I'll discuss this in several sections.

**(1) The core idea**

Reading the resource book, flashes of insight will come — the Chapter of Gunfire with Projection might be strong; Cthulhu with a tentacle horror must be fun. Those flashes usually lay the foundation of a new line. To borrow from Hearthstone: "fatigue" as an idea begat Mill Rogue, "go face" evolved into Face Hunter, and Mysterious Challenger single-handedly built the entire Secret Paladin deck. A build usually picks its resources around one core idea — the soul of the whole construction.

By Starlight's theory, resources divide into quantitative and qualitative: quantitative resources merely pile up numbers, while qualitative resources are where a build's 1+1>2 essence lives. As I understand it, a build's core is nothing more than one or a few core traits, and the differing effects of their combinations decide the build's line of thought. For a build to do the job you assigned it, first find one idea that produces the effect you want, then construct around the core traits. Let's analyze concrete cases.

1. Case 1: Heroic Spirit Alternation (pre-2.29 versions)

_Noble Phantasm: Noble Phantasms are also called [precious fantasies] — the concepts a Heroic Spirit followed in life, the items and abilities they used, sublimated by human fantasy into a Noble Phantasm. After sealing the Heroic Spirit contract, for each Additional Success on your base Intelligence you may self-create one skill, at half the XP cost. The skill must be exchanged at the moment you become a Heroic Spirit and cannot be saved for later._

_This self-created skill may be any ability in the resources, including:_

_Skill: you may gain a skill._

_Spell: you may gain a spell; you cannot gain a spell above your own caster level, and its casting function counts as Heroic Spirit._

_Title: you may gain a Title, but must exchange it starting from the D rank._

_Item: gain an item at double its price — an item priced D+1000, if you want it, computes as DD+2000. Noble Phantasms cannot be lost or handed over._

_If a spell or skill permits ranking up, evolving, or researching new uses, treat each upgrade as a new skill._

_With enough slots you may even exchange an entire Title, or a Tome, through this ability — meaning you exchange every skill and rank under the spellbook or Title, each rank and skill computed separately._

Heroic Spirit Alternation is an old-guard munchkin resource, and its key trait is exactly this XP-discount clause. The core idea: use the XP discount to leap past your current resource level and seize high-Tier abilities, then let the resource-level advantage carry the build's strength. A bit of lore here: in Infinite's rules, the XP currency and the Tier currency don't grow in step. By the self-created-ability rules, one D Tier is worth 10 XP, one C Tier 28 XP, one B Tier 54 XP, one A Tier 88 XP. Self-creating one A Tier costs less XP than self-creating nine Ds — yet counted in Tiers, one A = 27 Ds. So the higher the resource, the better the XP deal. Around this trait many munchkin lines were developed; grabbing resources above your grade naturally plays out like bullying schoolchildren. The classic application is Putao's build in Infinite Universe season one — grimly hoarding 120+ XP, then using Heroic Spirit Alternation to take the S-Tier vehicle Imperial Palace…

Resources of this kind — a single trait of standout strength that can carry a whole build — are easy to spot; other examples include the Apostle's AT Field and the dragon's magic-immunity trait.

2. Case 2: any Strength/Size Bloodline + Mario Alternation (version 2.27)

_C+1000: Mushroom Alternation_

_Prerequisite: Plumber Mario_

_You ate a mushroom nearly your own height, and it wrenched you taller. Your height doubles and your Size doubles — and this trait doubles your current Size, not your base Size. Size gained later stacks on top of this._

_Legendary Strength_

_1 point: 1 Additional Success, 500 kg extra carry weight, base throwing distance doubled, +1 DP on Strength checks, and when attacking with Strength your damage cap +1._

_2 points: 2 Additional Successes, 1,000 kg extra carry weight, base throwing distance ×4, a further +2 DP on Strength checks, and a further +2 damage cap when attacking with Strength._

_3 points: 3 Additional Successes, 2,000 kg extra carry weight, base throwing distance ×8, a further +3 DP on Strength checks, and a further +3 damage cap when attacking with Strength._

_4 points: 4 Additional Successes, 4,000 kg extra carry weight, base throwing distance ×12, a further +4 DP on Strength checks, and a further +4 damage cap when attacking with Strength._

_5 points: 5 Additional Successes, 8,000 kg extra carry weight, base throwing distance ×16, a further +5 DP on Strength checks, and a further +5 damage cap when attacking with Strength._

_Size: an ordinary adult is Size 5; only a special Feat allows 6, and 4 seems fitting for the underage-looking._

_For every 2 Size above 5: +1 Strength, −1 to hit, +1 Born Defense, −1 Evade Defense (this can push Evade Defense below 0, but in no case does it push your Defense below 0)._

From those three traits we can see that under 2.27 rules, Size converts straight into Strength, and Strength converts straight into DP and damage cap. On that rule stood a whole crop of Strength/Size builds, and the brightest among them was Mario, the resource that flat-out doubles Size. Through this trait you could stack damage and DP, simply and brutally, to the deadest heights of overtuning. In this build Mario is the qualitative resource; the other Strength/Size resources are the quantitative ones. (In passing: every rule trait mentioned above is dead now.)

In this case the trait belongs to the kind that needs a numerical base underneath before its magic shows — resources of this type generally convert big numbers into fundamentals, or fetch key traits. Here the Titan-style Strength/Size Bloodlines supply the numbers, and Mario acts as the qualitative catalyst; the familiar "add stat X / value X onto Y" clauses belong to this class. Similar resources include Sword Qi, the Divine Power Staff, and so on.

3. Case 3: Requiem + Rakshasa Ghost Bone

_Requiem D+1000_

_Sleep is death's brother; in dreams one stands nearest death._

_You can sing a hymn to the wheel of life and death — a song that puts the living to sleep and lays the dead to rest._

_You spend a Whole-round Action on a Demeanor + Expression check; every living creature and every wronged spirit of the dead that hears it resists with a Willpower check. A living creature that fails falls asleep for 1 hour per success — a state a Standard Action can wake, and any damage breaks automatically. If a dead soul (wronged spirits and similar incorporeal or undead beings; mind-immunity based on such traits does not apply here) fails its Willpower check, it sinks into despondency, gaining despondency points equal to the Expression successes left after the Willpower check's offset; when the despondency produces its destroying effect, the dead find rest._

_Rakshasa Ghost Bone_

_Category: Fantasy Category_

_Price: D+1000_

_Classification: magical treasure_

_Prerequisite: dark creature_

_Size: 1_

_Description: Again he asked, "And the bewitching?" She said: "Those who grow familiar with me — I secretly prick their feet with an awl, and they fall dazed as if entranced, whereupon I draw their blood for the demon to drink. Or I tempt them with gold — not gold in truth, but a Rakshasa's ghost bone, which kept nearby can cut out a man's heart and liver. Both merely pander to the tastes of the age." Ning thanked her and asked when to be on guard; she answered: tomorrow night._

_Hidden form: the thing looks like an ingot of gold. This is illusion — treat as a D-Tier anti-detection effect; seeing through it requires a DC 20 Perception + Investigation (Detection) or Intelligence + Occult (Illusion) check._

_Sacrificial tempering: you must pour 1 point of maximum demon force, dao force, true qi, vitality, grudge force, or true essence into it to temper it; thereafter the treasure gains the 【owner-bonded】 trait toward you._

_Dark probing: once the treasure is 【owner-bonded】 to you, holding the Ghost Bone lets you feel the living state of creatures within your sensitivity range (see Qi Manipulation) — an A-Tier Fantasy-Category detection effect. You may not see the target, but you can feel that it is near. This grants no positioning ability, but you know with certainty whether other targets are around._

_Instant-death strike: if a creature within the Ghost Bone's observed range lies helpless — unconscious, asleep, and the like (petrification excepted) — you may pay 5 points of the tempering energy and spend a Whole-round Action to launch a killing strike, tearing out the target's heart and liver._

_The strike makes no check at all: treat it as an instant-death attack of Tier A, type curse — without immunity, the target simply dies._

_Against the immune, treat it as a lethal attack, the temperer rolling Intelligence + Occult (Necromancy) to attack._

_Special: against targets whose heart is not vital (spirits, certain undead, constructs, etc.), the instant-death strike does not function._

This line of thought leans on the chemistry between two or more resources to dodge harsh conditions or produce rather imbalanced effects. It's a build I developed fairly recently; in PVP there's a real chance of catching someone unguarded — never mind your high defense and deep HP, within the conditions it's simply instant death… Similar pairings include the Cloud-Fire Duelist and the Night Angel bomb dominator; develop your own.

**(2) Stacking the numbers**

Generally, apart from a minority of concepts, most builds still need numbers stacked to perform. In wuxia terms: the core traits are the techniques, and the stacked numbers are the inner force. The higher your numbers, the better the special effects land — and sometimes, with no dazzling trait combination at all, plain honest number-piling alone beats the trick players to death. This part is inner cultivation; there is no shortcut — read the book, accumulate, learn the lines. Take DP-stacking: the most direct way is adding straight to the final computed number — a classic example is _Multiple Brain-Domain Partition_ under other Arts. Next is stacking base data such as Stats directly; for a Strength attacker, _Muscle Dominator_ embodies the idea — raw added Strength. Boosting skills, weapon damage, Size, and the rest belongs here too. The third way is closer to the trait-pairing idea above: using certain resources to add some other number onto this one. Two builds analyzed below.

1. Case: Executor + Soldier Bloodline (dead)

_Your art with the Black Keys grows more practiced: when attacking with Black Keys as your weapon, you may wield three in one hand at once. Weapon damage counts only one Key, but the other two Keys' weapon damage becomes a DP Machinery Bonus added to the attack check, while all three Keys' other effects stack — yielding a weapon of 3L damage, +6 DP, +3 Breach, +3 DP Sacred Bonus against dark creatures, and 3 lingering damage per hit._

_Devil Training: the skill you chose at C rank gains 1 more level, and its checks become 9+ Dice. While you wear armor, your weapon gains damage equal to your Strength, up to your chosen skill's level; if you have Additional Successes on Strength, your attacks gain the same Additional Successes, up to the Additional Successes you hold on the chosen skill. Besides this, your HP cap rises 3, and you gain one level of the Diehard Feat._

From the Executor Title's effect we can see the build's core idea: use the Death Seal's effect and stack weapon damage into massive output — so weapon damage is the number we must stack. Toward that end, the Soldier Bloodline's B trait buffs weapon damage enormously; and that latter approach can itself serve as a core idea, pairing with Executor-type resources to construct the damage side.

The example shows that different lines can be combined — they just take primary and secondary places in the build. Still on weapon-damage stacking: a high-Power, deeply researched Magic Marksman can likewise multiply the Black Keys' weapon damage, but the high caster level and Power-stacking behind it will claim a share of resources. Whether that crowds your core idea is for each builder to weigh.

2. Case: Primordial Sword Classic (version 2.33)

_Mystic Penetration_

_Basic training: the Melee skill gains every Profession related to "sword" and "saber", and the Profession bonus becomes 3._

_Sword Qi: once per Round, you may spend 1 Free Action to pay up to the lower of your Agility or Perception in Sword Qi points, then gain an equal Environmental Bonus on melee attack checks or defense (your choice) for 1 Round; this use can run alongside Sword Qi's basic use._

Sword Qi is a staple DP-stacker for eastern-style Agility melee: it adds the lower of Agility/Perception straight onto DP, and as a rare Environmental Bonus no less — direct and violent, with no flaw except the steep energy cost.

However many lines you weave and swap along the way, number-stacking must in the end come home to the fundamentals — Stats, skills, Power, Size, caster level — and the numbers that add DP directly. Apportion your resources well in the build; never chase fancy lines so hard that you neglect the foundation.

**(3) Acquiring key traits**

With the idea and the number-stacking settled, your build should have a basic frame — perhaps you're brimming with confidence. Why not take it for a spin, and watch how easily the ST toys with you in the palm of one hand (laughs).

At this stage many players — Agility-melee players above all — will have discovered it: I built a card with DP in the hundreds or thousands, so why is my damage in single digits? Simple: you never solved the damage cap. In Infinite 2.xx, melee DP computes as _Strength + skill level + weapon Power + others_, while the damage cap computes as _Strength + skill level + weapon Power_. Plenty of resources let you roll damage with Agility/Demeanor/…, but leave the cap unsolved and your damage cap becomes _skill level + weapon Power_ — however high your DP climbs, that is all the damage you'll ever deal…

To solve the damage cap there are two main routes at present: one, brute-stack weapon damage; two, acquire a trait like this: _when making a melee attack, if you use Agility in place of Strength for the attack check, you may also use Agility in place of Strength for your damage cap. (White Tiger Star aside, this trait usually sits at each resource's B rank.)_ That is what we call a key trait.

By the same token: your melee output is unbeatable, but the ST fields flying ranged monsters; your marksmanship takes a devil's head from eight hundred li, but you can't see the invisible assassin at your side; your one sword sweeps in from the west like an immortal from beyond the sky, but you lack any AOE, and hack east and west as you may, that swarm of sacred scarabs is beyond you… There are many such traits; explore them at the table. My summary of the more critical ones, for reference: damage cap, AOE, detection, range, movement speed, flight, initiative, the three saves, and so on.

**Part Three: What is my role in the team?**

Having read the last section, the sharp among you will have noticed: resources are finite, and the key traits to cover are many — how can one card manage them all? Indeed, apart from mages and a rare few shameless resources like the Kidney-Deficient Demon, most builds cannot cover every base. Spend your resources on detection, flight, and the three saves, and your damage will likely trail the pure damage-stackers. Against everything a campaign can throw at you, one person can hardly manage alone — hence teammates. Here is my reading of the roles a team needs.

_1. The detector._ The classic psychic-force scan of the original Infinite Horror novel is exactly this. A team that doesn't run detection dies without ever learning how; other teams strike and you never know from where. Detection is also the team's chief source of intelligence when advancing the plot, with high story involvement and a decent play experience — indispensable. A good detector must solve three problems: detection range, detection level, and intelligence-sharing.

_2. The striker._ No explanation needed. In most campaigns everything else is optional, but no striker is unthinkable — 80% of PC cards are striker cards. A team can hardly have too many strikers, and other roles can moonlight as one. My own feeling is that 2–3 strikers is best, each specialized in one aspect, covering different attack surfaces between them — single-target, beyond-visual-range strikes, AOE, special attacks against Willpower or Fortitude, control abilities. Strikers generally have the best table experience and the better earnings too.

_3. The support._ Infinite's support is like the "crutch" of FGO: a good support can prop up any striker, and one support may multiply your damage severalfold. Generally supports serve as buff and debuff engines; some are themselves strong strikers. A pure support's table experience runs poorer; a support who doubles as a striker is a thigh — cling to it. The support must mind initiative, numbers, and survival; fail there and the support gets deleted on entry, and the whole team turns invalid…

_4. Logistics / utility._ To my mind the most fun role of all. Ordinarily, without a utility character the ST won't press you too hard — but with one, you can pull off no end of tricks: double the joy! Group teleportation, terrain reshaping, speaking with the dead, mind control, mass-produced troop swarms — given one wildly imaginative player and an ST willing to let PCs run riot, these can tear the script apart. This role's experience depends on the ST: if the ST won't let you make mischief, it will be poor. In PVP campaigns, though, STs are usually lenient — every dirty trick of yours can go on the enemy samsara team. _Note!_ One class of character in this role is special: the almighty _crafter!_ The crafter is father to most of the team, above all the Gundam and vehicle players; a crafter can also charge teammates extra on their commissions and climb in strength fast, so they rarely fall behind in combat power. Of course, if your teammates are all Fantasy players and you insist on Tech crafting, sales of your products may be hard to guarantee…

Some classmates will still ask: what about the frontline — the tank? Don't we need one? As I read Infinite 2.xx's mechanics, we genuinely don't need the tank as a profession. An HP-sponge does earn its keep walking into traps; but for one thing, Infinite's numbers inflate too easily — defense growth trails damage growth hopelessly, and Infinite's attack surface is too broad: attacks on defense, on the three saves, fixed damage, even attacks on lifespan and karma — no guarding against them all. For another, Infinite's taunts are too weak: apart from the Dashing Man, taunts are short-ranged and still must win a Resisting Check… if I can win the Resisting Check, why taunt you? Why not just control you outright? So in the usual PVP campaign, Infinite is a game of rock-paper-scissors plus mutual one-shots: you failed to block my line, you acted after me — fine, you're dead. One example, my original PVP line "Book of the Seven Nail-Head Arrows" (version 2.34), Double-One B + Blood-Curse Bearer A: the moment I sense you I know your birth characters, and knowing your birth characters I can curse you through a puppet… any build without karma-law immunity finds this line very hard to answer.

Survival remains necessary all the same — every role must weigh it, or die with ease to any passing plot. The most straightforward survival tool is stacking HP; the best is not getting hit. Damage absorption, temporary HP, leaving the attack's range, death-substitution tricks, hiding in imaginary space — develop the rest yourselves.

**Part Four: In closing**

Work keeps me from playing much; I mostly went into retirement around version 2.32. But in my experience most online campaigns never reach the late game — they generally end before A-Tier resources — and Infinite, being a rules-heavy, combat-heavy game, often sees the PCs slide into an arms race the longer it runs. That is bad. Overtuning ruins your teammates' experience and the ST's alike. My advice: everyone show restraint, hold your strength near your teammates' level, and keep the team in step — that is how the whole table plays better.
