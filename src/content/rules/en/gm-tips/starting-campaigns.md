# Campaign-Starting Experience

The Storyteller (generally shortened to ST) is the game's focal point: when the game delights, the Lord God takes the praise — and when it fails, whatever the cause, the Lord God takes the blame.

**What is an ST?**

The ST's work spans teaching, steering the plot, performing, driving the game forward, adjudicating checks, and more. Below we sketch the whole of it; you may find certain parts suit you best, and you're free to lean into what interests you — but note that a good ST treats the rest as no less important.

**Why call it an infinite-flow TRPG?**

This being an infinite-flow TRPG, the setup generally has the Storyteller send the PCs (players) on periodic adventures.

Play usually opens with the PCs summoned out of their own world into a brand-new one — a film's world, an anime's world, or one the ST invents outright. (And if the ST prefers, the PCs may be natives of that world instead.) Once summoned, the ST hands every PC a main quest; the moment it's completed, the PCs return to the ST's imaginary space, where the rewards of the adventure buy all manner of enhancements against the next challenge.

The ST then prepares the next challenge; after an interval of the ST's choosing (usually 10 days), the PCs are summoned into a new world for a new adventure. As the PCs grow stronger, so do the worlds, the enemies, and the monsters.

**Designing adventure quests**

Beyond the main quest just mentioned there are side quests. The main quest is the spine of the adventure, running through it from start to finish; side quests thread through in between, and the more of them completed, the greater the PCs' rewards.

Designing both eats time — many STs spend far longer preparing than actually running, and generally they find the creating itself great fun. Inventing quests, plots, challenges, and monsters, then pulling friends deep inside them, gives an ST's imagination full play.

Designing one good adventure matters enormously. An adventure generally means summoning the PCs into a world and running until the main quest sends them back to the imaginary space — we usually call this one horror film.

**What is the overall flow of the game?**

The world the PCs are first summoned into is not preceded by the imaginary space — meaning the PCs enter it on their starting character cards.

In the third summoned world — the third horror film — the Storyteller picks, by performance, one PC among those who have opened Potential Burst to become captain, granting captain authority; all the team's PCs then automatically form a squad, called an infinite squad.

Then in the fourth film, the sixth, the eighth, and so on, they meet other infinite squads. This is called team battle, and such a film a team-battle horror film.

The twentieth film is called the final battle: every infinite squad fights to the death until a last victor stands. It is also the final adventure — completing the twentieth film's story generally declares the campaign complete.

**Altering the original plot**

As said, the PCs land in film worlds, anime worlds, or others — each with its own established setting and story.

Remember: you are the one leading the whole adventure, which means you may change any of the original content. When importing a plot you'll often need small changes to make the whole flow perfectly, since most PCs have probably seen the film or show; touching up details for the sake of smoothness is entirely normal.

But if you do so, tell your PCs — it keeps them from being trapped in the story they remember. And when PC actions themselves bend the original plot, that is itself part of the side quests: reward them well for it.

**Teaching the game**

Sometimes teaching brand-new PCs how to play is part of your job too. It may feel like a burden — in truth it's the opposite. Everyone understands the game differently, and that difference traces largely to their first experience of it; so for an ST, onboarding a new PC is a golden opportunity. It doesn't just add a player — it shapes one who plays on your wavelength, the kind you'll genuinely enjoy playing with.

Some tips:

Read the "Building a Character Card" chapter carefully, then help new PCs create their characters. Have them describe the character they hope to play — looks, height, weight, gender, and where they want the character to grow.

The ST then teaches the PC how to shape that imagined character with these rules. If they don't know what to play, tell them this book holds endless possibility: let their imagination loose, offer examples from fantasy to realism, from past to future, from magic to technology, and guide them to a character they love.

_A PC might build a plain-faced, 1.9-meter woman of muscle, swinging a greatsword while hurling fireballs — or a scrawny 1.3-meter man charging headlong behind a shield._

_It rests entirely on what the PC wants from the card._

Once the new PC's character stands, the basic rules come next.

This part is very simple: early on, they only need the rules touching their own character.

The game opens on mortal cards, so new players will have almost nothing supernatural — attacking, defending, and using skills is enough, with the rest picked up in play.

Start with the basics: attack, defense, Speed, initiative, saves, and the rules of rolling and checks.

As long as the ST knows the rules and comes prepared, the game can begin. In play, each player minds only their own character and how to act when events arrive. You need only build a basic world that runs on logic; when PCs act, have them tell you what they intend, help translate the action into the rules, explain how those rules work — then resolve it. After a few rounds of counting dice and totting results on one matter, they'll have learned it.

_Say a player's character charges: have the player pick a target, check the straight line between for obstacles, and figure the character's movement speed. All satisfied, set the charge's DP change, run attack against defense, and roll damage last._

**Creating the world**

The ST is the game world's creator. Even when the ST borrows the setting and plot of an anime, a film, or anything else, the world remains the ST's own.

The game world holds all of that world's background — though mostly we don't encourage characters to explore all of it. With a main quest standing over them, players may have little room or time to roam, and in that case the ST need only plan the parts of the world that touch the players. In an infinite TRPG each PC stays in a world only briefly and soon departs, so weaving PCs deep into it is rarely needed. But if you mean to run several adventures in one world, set out everything in it: a well-designed, well-running world changes with the characters' actions, and only then will players find it interesting enough to pour real effort into engaging with what you built.

**Refereeing**

Note: in this game everything falls to the ST's word — which doesn't license bossing people around outside the game; it means only that at the table you are the rules' final arbiter. Within the session you hold absolute, supreme authority over the rules, resources, resolutions, worldview, setting, and mechanics in play — above even the rulebook and the original world's canon.

But a good ST knows not to change or overturn standing rules lightly without proper cause, nor to change or overturn established setting without advance word, lest the players sour. Whatever else, you all sat down together to have fun.

**Running the game**

Every player is part of the game, but the ST must push it forward, keep interest alive, and keep things fun. Bear in mind: keeping play flowing smoothly matters far more than digging frantically through the rulebook for some detail or burning time arguing a ruling. That said, this is no license for the ST to improvise rules at whim — in every circumstance, the rules are neutral.

Even a well-run game can bog down. Players may weary of repeated, formulaic, dull plotlines after a while — and tastes differ, so a scene one player loves may bore another stiff. No ST can prevent this entirely; but when you see the story sinking into such a mire, cut it or simplify it rather than let it drag and wreck the session's structure.

Beyond plot, a fearsome monster or a stubborn puzzle can also strand the players. When that happens, ease the difficulty or otherwise let them through — matching wits is genuinely hard, and your task is to give the players a good time, not to net them all with some impossible obstacle.

Small tricks can also deepen the experience: physical props, terrain cards, and the like.

Given this ruleset's nature, you can also pull the source work's terrain, scenes, images, and lines to recreate a moment — read them out or show them directly. It makes for a more thrilling, more vivid session.

Practically, you can't prep props or pictures for every monster, scene, item, and NPC — there you must lean on spoken description that fires the imagination.

_Say the players board an alien ship. "You enter an alien ship — this looks like its storeroom; there's a passage over there" conveys the scene. But try: "You enter an enormous alien vessel. Just inside, unidentifiable clutter is stacked beside you, and a deep passage stretches off to who knows where. The whole space presses down on you — you seem to catch a low sound from far off, wind or something else." The second, I promise, will put your players inside the scene._

Remember: in the game you are the players' eyes, nose, ears, and touch. You must give them every sense their characters can reach. It multiplies the fun of your game.

Of course the surest draw is a tight battle — no player forgets a storm-lashed fight on the Caribbean, or a death-struggle with the Ringwraiths at Mount Doom. But not every fight needs to hang by a thread while the sky falls: pace it like an action film or an adventure novel, tension and release, and it will hold attention fine.

If you like to set the atmosphere, background music helps — the original film's or show's score, or anything else — but never let the music break immersion. Some players dislike music entirely and find it distracting; accept the table's preference. Atmosphere that annoys the players defeats itself.

Sound effects can color particular scenes too — wind, a door opening — and lift the atmosphere further.

When playing an NPC, consider shifting pitch, changing tone, or adding verbal tics — players remember such NPCs vividly.

You can mimic simple movements too — a dwarf, a limp, a hunched back — or act out an in-game motion: a sword swing, a thrown challenge, a taunt, handing something over, eating and drinking. Players usually respond in kind to the performance. But note: it is only performance. Don't actually do these things — it could be genuinely dangerous.

**Game styles**

The ST supplies the adventure's plot and world and needs the players' cooperation for the game to run well. Note that the ST–player relationship is cooperation, not opposition or competition. So state your terms when the campaign opens: finding players aligned with you matters greatly, and chief among the alignments is play style — what we call the table's style.

**Kick-in-the-door**

Kick-in-the-door means the players storm the world with simple brutality, cut down monsters, sweep up rewards, and head home — then into the next world, more monsters, more loot, round and round.

The style is direct and thrilling — action-oriented. Plot is thin, NPC interaction sparse; players hardly need to develop personality or roleplay their characters, nor to mind non-combat scenes for roleplay or plot strategy. Bring your combat power and fight.

Under this style, set the players face to face with the monsters (the zombies of *Resident Evil*, the xenomorphs of *Alien*). The players need only think about rolling over (defeating) whatever stands in front of them — no tangles of plot and NPC feelings to manage. Main quest and motive stay dead simple: kill the target, take the reward.

In this form of game the rules matter greatly. If you run it, field a wide variety of enemies to challenge different players, and know the rules cold — that's what carries you through combat's surprises.

**Story-driven**

Under this style, player combat power fades in importance. The hallmarks are conversational roleplay, deep character development, and interaction — player with player, player with NPC. A whole session may pass without a single roll.

Here NPCs generally must be built as richly and intricately as the player characters, and the ST must know the world's setting, its background, and every appearing NPC's character (or, in an ST-original world, simply have them all set down).

Expect players to spend long stretches discussing what their characters will do and why. Striking a deal with an NPC weighs as much as going out to kill a monster together, and actions that shift an NPC's views or the story's course can earn rich rewards. Characters will sometimes do the wholly unexpected — the plot may end up nothing like the original film or show, which tests the ST's grip on the story all the more. If you choose this style, don't stop them: it may produce the better story, and your charge is keeping that story able to move forward.

In this style the rules recede. With combat no longer the core, the central machinery serves plot development and the rounding of character and background. You may simplify some combat rules to buy storytelling time, or skip fights entirely to speed characters into the next scene.

**The middle road**

Most campaigns actually sit between the two extremes — plenty of action scenes, but plot too, and character interplay. Players sharpen their combat power and deepen their characters' personalities and backstories alike; they want NPC scenes and they want a good rousing fight. Here you weave roleplay and combat together, advancing scenes with rhythm, giving the table tension and release — not one long chat, and not one long slaughter.

**Campaign-starting essentials — experience shared by a certain ST:**

1) Not too many NPCs. Infinite-flow being what it is, PCs simply lack the time to form real bonds with NPCs, and a crowd of them tends to hijack your plot and leave the PCs feeling like film spectators.

2) Keep the plot simple. Give PCs real freedom of play. Should a puzzle stall them, hand out clue after clue — set it right in front of their faces if need be. Never let the story dead-end.

3) Monsters should fight straight and honest. Unless it's a special boss-type creature, meet the PCs head on. Don't hammer Willpower because a PC's Willpower is low, blanket the field with AOE because Reflex is low, or roll out fog after fog of poison because Fortitude is low. Forever striking the weak spot grinds down the player behind the card.

4) Design the points and side quests well — let PCs see what they're playing for.

5) Control the pacing. The rhythm is always the ST's to hold: right after a great battle is no time for another. Cycle the PCs between combat and rest. Fights too frequent tire the characters, tire the players — and tire the ST.

6) Let PCs feel death's threat; let them know danger scales with the score. Keep pressure on, so that relieving it yields real accomplishment. But never hand them helplessness — that sours players on the game fast.

7) Following on: danger, yes, but don't let PCs die easily. Every player has feelings for their card. Governing death's tempo is part of the craft. A player showing up with 3–4 spare cards ready to be torn is a bad time for any ST. Once too many die, players stop sinking into their characters of their own accord.

8) Know your measure — don't attempt what you can't manage. If triple-S strength is your ceiling, then govern how freely you hand out points. When you don't know how deep the water runs, wade in a step at a time; don't try to swallow it all in one bite.

9) Serve the PCs; don't torment them. STs are scarce in this world — but "your campaign was great fun" and "your campaign was a pit" are very different things to hear. PCs finding your table fun is the true measure. Your excellence shows only in how you run the game; a brilliant character-build technique or brilliant prose proves nothing.
