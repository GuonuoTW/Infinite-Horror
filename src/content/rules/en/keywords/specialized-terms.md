# Specialized Terms

**Specialized terms explained:**

**Number ranges:**
"X and above": inclusive of the compared value — that rank and every rank higher. E.g. "A-rank and above" means A and S.
"X and below": inclusive of the compared value — that rank and every rank lower. E.g. "A-rank and below" means A, B, C, and D.
"Greater than" ("higher than"): exclusive — every rank above the compared value. E.g. "greater than B" means A and S.
"Less than" ("lower than"): exclusive — every rank below the compared value. E.g. "less than C" means D.

**Targets:**

Most area skills in fact also involve targets.

Terms like "hostile target", "enemy", "ally" and so on are generally read as follows:

"Hostile target" and "enemy": units regarded as hostile in nature — by faction, attitude, etc. — with the ST as final arbiter.

"Ally" and "companion": friendly units, not including yourself.

"Friendly target": friendly units, including yourself.

"One target": any target — yourself, enemies, and allies included. For resources that can select one additional target: unless stated otherwise, the additional target may be chosen as such, but may not be the originally chosen target.

"All targets" / "friend and foe alike": every target in the area, enemies and allies included, but not yourself.

"Including yourself": every target in the area — enemies, allies, and yourself.

"Within sight": within rough visual range, including yourself, and requiring a line of sight.

Owing to loose drafting, "target" and "unit" generally mean the same thing.

**Checks:**

Action check: a check aimed at some activity, such as climbing or swimming. Make the check normally per the rules — action Checks are generally Stat + Skill (with matching Profession).

Skill check: generally, when told simply to make a check of a skill, roll only that skill's level. For Expression or Crafts, choose the qualifying sub-skill with the highest level.

Example: Blade 5 — a Blade check means rolling 5 DP, gaining the skill level's bonus successes as normal.

Attack check: attack checks come in different kinds; see the various attack modes under "Battle Rules".

Resisting Check: a Resisting Check is the reactive, contested check you make when you are the target — including but not limited to the three Saves. When an ability hits you and you must resist with EDR + DTR, that check is a Resisting Check.

Full check: "full checks" include all of the checks above, but not those defined as non-checks — Initiative, Gene Lock, Opportunity Rolls, and the like. Note that defense is not a check either, so full checks do not include defense.

**Prerequisites:**

Non-item prerequisites: some non-item resources require prerequisites at exchange — Stats, skill levels, Energy Pools, or other conditions. You must meet them to buy, learn, and use the resource.

If, after acquiring a resource, you lose a prerequisite for other reasons, you temporarily cannot use the resource; once the prerequisite recovers, you may use it again.

Item prerequisites: most weapons have usage prerequisites, generally Stat, skill, and Profession:

For Stat and skill: each point you fall short imposes a 2 DP Machinery Penalty on use. Short by 3 or more and the weapon is unusable — STR 3 cannot use a weapon demanding STR 7; using it at STR 6 takes a 6 DP Machinery Penalty.

For Profession: lacking the matching Profession imposes a 3 DP Machinery Penalty. If a weapon uses more than one Profession, each missing one adds a 3 DP penalty; with none of them, the weapon is unusable.

**Areas:**

Some spells, skills, Techniques, and items provide persistent areas of effect. These come in two kinds:

The first targets a fixed point: the effect anchors there when released and never moves afterward. While active, targets leaving the area lose the effect and targets entering gain it. E.g. Myriad Leaves Meditation.

The second anchors to a target: the area moves with that target while active; targets leaving lose the effect, targets entering gain it. E.g. Chapter of the Knight-Errant.

**Range and reach:**

Attacks with range are ranged attacks — you may attack targets within your range. Attacks with reach are melee attacks — you may attack targets within your reach. If a skill lets you strike distant targets: if its wording changes *reach* to XX meters, it counts as melee; if it changes *range* to XX meters, it counts as ranged — this matters for certain triggered effects.

Reach is computed from a character's Size, which determines the Size of their natural weapons and hence their reach. Wielding a weapon, add the weapon's Size to derive the new reach.

**Tremorsense**: a creature with this trait, standing on a surface — the ground, a web, etc. — can precisely locate targets standing on that same surface. Its range is the creature's sensory range; the locating is of the Natural category. Against invisibility and similar effects it counts directly as a rough fix. Tremorsense has no Resisting Tier — it simply works on qualifying units.

**Blindsense**: some creatures sense what they cannot see through non-visual senses — keen smell or hearing. It usually needs no Spot or Listen checks to locate creatures within its blindsense range, provided a line of effect exists between it and the target. To a blindsensing creature, unseen creatures still have total concealment, and it still takes the rough-fix penalty when attacking concealed opponents. Visibility still affects a blindsensing creature's movement. Blindsense has no Resisting Tier — it simply works on qualifying units.

**Blindsight**: like blindsense, but far more precise. Using non-visual senses — vibration, keen smell, sharp hearing, echoes — a blindsighted creature maneuvers and fights as well as a sighted one. Invisibility, darkness, and most concealment are useless against it, though it needs a line of effect to detect anything. Its effective range is given in the creature's description. It usually needs no Spot or Listen checks to note creatures within range. Unless stated otherwise, blindsight is continuous and requires nothing to activate. Blindsight has no Resisting Tier — it simply works on qualifying units.

**Low-light vision**: a weaker darkvision. Sees unimpaired in poor light, but remains blind in total lightless darkness. Ignores the effects of dim environments.

**Darkvision**: the ability to see in darkness — any darkness, even utterly without light — accurately identifying objects and colors within it. Ignores the effects of dark and dim environments.

**Spirit sight**: a character with spirit sight sees incorporeal beings and spirits normally. A spirit's inherent invisibility simply fails before spirit sight.

**Keen scent (hearing)**: the bearer can use smell (hearing) to get a rough fix on targets within sensory range.

**True seeing**: sees everything that ought to be seen. Sees in darkness; sees spirits and incorporeal beings; sees through any "illusion" cast on a person or "phantasm" placed at a location; sees through invisibility. Its piercing ignores ability Tier and needs no Resisting Check. Unless stated otherwise, its range is sensory range.

**Amplify**: some spells, skills, and Techniques allow amplification. The amplification cap is a cap on energy spent. E.g. "Amplify: each point of energy spent grants 2 DP, cap 4" — you may spend 4 energy for 8 DP, because the cap of 4 refers to energy.

**Unavoidable (unmitigable)**: damage or a negative status of this kind, though it has its own damage type, ignores all your negation, absorption, conversion, and similar defensive abilities — it can even break through immunity — and cannot be transferred to another target. E.g.: I take 3 unmitigable Lethal damage; despite my 3 points of all-damage absorption, I take the 3 damage as normal.

**Unsaveable (no save)**: damage or a negative status of this kind merely denies the target a save — the target's negation, absorption, conversion, immunity, and similar defenses all work as normal.

**Action upgraded one step**: a negative effect. What took a swift action now takes a move action; what took a move action now takes a standard action. Unless the causing ability states otherwise, the action ladder is: free – swift – move – standard – full-round.

**Action downgraded one step**: a positive effect. What required a standard action can now be done as a move action; what required a move action now takes a swift action. Unless stated otherwise, the ladder is: free – swift – move – standard – full-round.

**Damage downgraded one grade**: a positive effect. An attack's Aggravated damage becomes Lethal; Lethal becomes Blunt. Unless the granting effect says otherwise, Blunt is not downgraded further.

**Damage upgraded one grade**: a positive effect. An attack's Blunt damage becomes Lethal; Lethal becomes Aggravated. Unless the granting effect says otherwise, Aggravated is not upgraded further.

**Margin of success**: in an opposed check, the winner's successes minus the loser's; or, in a check, the successes exceeding the DC.

**Margin of failure**: in an opposed check, how far the loser's successes fall below the winner's; or, in a check, how far the DC exceeds the successes.

**The three saves**: the three reactive checks — Will save, Reflex save, Fortitude save. These are your staple life-savers.

**Critical hit**: a critical ability lets your attack deal higher damage. When you trigger a critical, its effect resolves after the damage cap — critical damage can exceed the damage cap.
Only one critical ability can trigger per attack; a character with several must choose one.
A critical only boosts the attack's own damage — it cannot boost damage added onto the attack.

**Touch attack**: a touch attack is generally contained within one of your attack actions.

Touch-attack checks ignore the target's natural, armor, and shield defense.

This does not mean those defenses stop existing: for abilities that trigger on breakthrough — such as **[Bulletproof]** — compute your Breach as normal.

"Make a touch attack; on a hit, deal XXX" — the touch attack deals its damage as normal.
"Make a touch attack; on a hit, additionally deal XXX" — the touch attack deals its damage as normal.
"Make a touch attack; on a hit, the attack deals no damage and instead deals/causes XXX" — the touch attack deals no damage.

**Born Defense**: the defense bonus of a creature's naturally tough hide. It stacks with other defense bonuses, including but not limited to Armor and Shield Defense. Born Defense can be broken by an attack's Breach.

**Natural weapon**: a creature's built-in armament — fists, feet, claws, fangs, horns, tails, and the like; magical and legendary creatures may add wing-blades, arm-blades, tentacles, and more. Natural-weapon attacks are checked with **STR + Brawl (matching Profession)**. Natural weapons have no Technique compatibility; when computing Tier, use the higher of the natural weapon's Tier or an artificial barehand weapon (fist wraps).

For pseudo-blade weapons imitated with natural weapons (Blacklight's claw, blade, and whip; the Iron Dragon Sword of the iron Dragon-Slayer grimoire; Blade-Fruit limb conversion; the Survivor bloodline's hand-to-sword; the cantrip knife, etc.), such imitation weapons generally have their own "classification" — check them with Blades and parry with Blades accordingly, with the matching specialty bonus. If it has no classification of its own (e.g. "Shikigami dispatch"), it remains a natural weapon.

Unless stated otherwise, a creature's fist, foot, and claw natural weapons have Size equal to the creature's Size / 5; teeth, Size / 50; tail, Size / 3.

**Multiple attacks**: in combat, a character may attack once or several times against one or several targets within a single action. The usual cases:

Buffs or debuffs affecting "the next attack": if the multiple attacks count as one attack, all of them are affected. If they count as separate attacks, only the first is affected — or, if the ability allows, any single one of them.

Multiple attacks on one target with the same weapon (or effect), counting as one attack: they share a damage cap, and effects that could trigger repeatedly trigger only once (2 attacks with "+2 damage on hit" total +2). If total damage exceeds the cap, the attacker allocates how much each attack dealt, consistent with the earlier rolls.

Multiple attacks on one target with the same weapon (or effect), counting as one attack: the target's damage-lowering abilities (absorption, reduction, ignoring, negation) apply only once against the final damage.

Multiple attacks on one target with the same weapon (or effect), counting as one attack: effects keyed to final damage or to hitting compute once, using the final damage or final hit result.

Multiple attacks on one target with the same weapon (or effect), counting as separate attacks: each attack has its own damage cap, and repeatable effects trigger repeatedly (2 attacks with "+2 damage on hit" total +4).

Multiple attacks on one target with different weapons (or effects), counting as one attack: they share a damage cap; compute each attack's effects and the attacker picks one to apply (2 attacks granting "+2 damage" and "+2 charm points" on hit — choose one). If total damage exceeds the cap, the attacker allocates each attack's damage consistent with the rolls.

Multiple attacks on one target with different weapons (or effects), counting as separate attacks: separate damage caps, and each attack's repeatable effects compute individually.

Multiple attacks on one target with different weapons (or effects), counting as one attack: if the target moves out of your attack range mid-way, the remaining attacks still resolve, but the target's defense doubles for them.

Multiple attacks on one target with different weapons (or effects), counting as separate attacks: if the target moves out of range, the current attack faces doubled defense and subsequent attacks are simply canceled — unless some ability lets you move mid-sequence and bring the target back into range.

When attacking several targets with multiple attacks, treat each target as its own single-target case above.

**Multiple-attack penalty**: the penalty a target suffers for being attacked repeatedly in the same turn.

Each attack the target has already received this turn imposes 1 point of multiple-attack defense penalty on your Guard Defense. When Guard Defense reaches 0, Evade Defense is depleted next; when Evade is gone, Base Defense; at most all three are depleted. When Base Defense hits zero, the defense Additional Successes it grants all vanish.

An attack imposes the multiple-attack penalty whether or not it hits or deals damage.

If the target hasn't been attacked yet this turn and the attacker's multiple attacks count as separate attacks rather than one, the target takes the multiple-attack penalty as normal from the second attack onward.

**Stats Damage**: a milder, temporary effect — unless stated otherwise, all Stats Damage is temporary. Each point makes the relevant Stat count as 1 lower than its true value for the duration, immediately affecting everything tied to it. Unless stated otherwise, natural recovery is: each 24-hour Long Rest restores 1 point of Stats Damage to one Stat of your choice.

**Attribute drain**: a permanent injury to an Attribute. The Attribute permanently drops by the drained amount, immediately affecting everything tied to it, and cannot be recovered through rest or repair — treat it as though the player never had those points. (Skills the player took using it as a prerequisite become temporarily unusable but are not lost; regain the Attribute by other means and the disabled abilities return automatically.) When later raising the Attribute with XP or points (or similar), pay costs as from the reduced current base value.

**Adjacent (touching)**: two targets no more than 1 meter apart (1 meter inclusive).

**Invisibility**: invisibility hides your form; for details see the "Detection and Locating" rules.

An invisible creature gains a 4 DP environmental bonus on attack and 4 DP environmental bonus on defense. Only against targets able to see through your invisibility (able to pinpoint you) is this bonus void.

**Line of effect and line of sight**: line of effect determines whether some effect (an explosion, say) can reach a target.

Line of sight determines whether two creatures have direct sight of each other — at least one can see the other.

To judge either: between the space one creature occupies and the space the other occupies, from any start to any end, if a straight line exists that passes through no obstruction, the two have clear lines of effect and sight. An obstruction that doesn't block vision (a transparent wall) leaves line of sight but no line of effect. No obstruction, but no vision (the target is invisible, or you are blind) leaves line of effect but no line of sight.

The two lines decide mutual influence: unless stated otherwise, lacking either one means the two sides cannot affect each other.

There are exceptions. Suppose your target is an immobile wooden post and you hide behind a wall: you can still pinpoint the post despite having no line of sight, and if you have a curving strike that ignores lines of effect and sight, you can still hit it — but only because you can pinpoint the target and your ability needs neither line.

**Ray**: a beam produced by a skill. Firearm-class weapons with the **[Energy Weapon]** trait that are neither suppressive fire nor area damage are rays; some magics, spells, and skills are rays too.

**Annihilated**: unavoidable death, beyond any future revival.

**"X-category related checks"**: generally this wording covers the category's skill-related checks, not its Stat-related checks.

**Immune to X**: generally, immunity must name a specific effect — you are unaffected by that effect. If an immunity has a Resisting Tier, it only blocks matching effects of that Tier and below. If you gain an immunity while already under the matching effect, all of it is removed at once.

E.g., player A carries: 20 burn points (from a C-rank skill, stacking), 5 burn (from a B-rank item, stacking), 10 burn (from an A-rank spell, stacking).

If A is immune to B-rank burn points, A takes 10 burn points.

If A is immune to A-rank spells, A takes 25 burn points.

If A is immune to fire damage, A takes all 35 burn points but none of the fire damage they inflict.

**Unarmored target**: a target wearing nothing classified as light or heavy armor and having no armor defense.

**Spider climb**: the target moves along any fixed surface as though walking flat ground — no Climb checks, moving at full land speed.

**Self-contradiction**: when a character holds outright self-contradictory abilities, rule against the character.

E.g., a character who cannot die of old age but also must die at age 20: the latter prevails.

**Dismiss**: some abilities, beyond their normal duration, can be dismissed — only by the character who produced them. Dismissal ends the duration immediately; the dismissing action is "at any time".

**Revival**: various abilities can bring targets back from death, which invites confusion, so the definitions are standardized here.

One kind revives the original target: the unit returns to its original player's control — regardless of how long revival takes, it is a player-controlled unit, and in rules terms a true revival.

The other kind re-creates from the corpse: raising a skeleton soldier, or duplicating a unit identical to the body. The new unit belongs to its creator; the corpse is merely consumed as material, and unless stated otherwise it is annihilated (or no longer usable as a corpse) once used — the new unit (the skeleton) has no relation to the original body. Revival or other death-targeting abilities aimed at the corpse's original owner have nothing to do with the new unit.

If an ability uses or targets "a corpse", it cannot be used on a corpse already consumed. If it needs no corpse — targeting the soul, or reviving from nothing (Lord God revival) — it works as normal.

**Defense lowering**: armor piercing, magic piercing, and similar special effects lower defense. Three forms exist:

1. "The target suffers X defense penalty": subtract X from the target's defense directly; abilities the target derives from defense — such as **[Bulletproof]** — are unaffected. Unless stated otherwise, this cannot push defense negative.

2. "The attacker ignores X of the target's defense": the resource usually states whether this affects the target's defense-derived abilities; if not, handle as (1); if so, handle as (3). Unless stated otherwise, this cannot push defense negative.

3. The attacker has armor piercing or another specific defense-breaking ability: subtract X from the matching breakable defense; unless stated otherwise, once that defense reaches 0 the excess does nothing.

Unless stated otherwise, a target's defense cannot go negative; if it would, the attacker's attack check gains a perfect bonus equal to the negative amount.

**Mobility**
Every mode of movement has a Mobility rating, in five grades from high to low: perfect → good → average → clumsy → none.

**Perfect**
Turn at any angle; move forward or backward at will. For flight or swimming, ascend or descend vertically.
Characters moving normally generally have perfect Mobility.

**Good**
Turning radius at most (your Size / 2) meters (on the map: you must move about one of your own occupied squares between turns); switching from forward to reverse costs half your movement. For flight or swimming, vertical ascent moves at half speed.
Motorcycles, bicycles, and well-tuned compacts and rally cars at low speed are Good.

**Average**
Turning radius at most (your Size) meters (about two occupied squares between turns); cannot switch directly from forward to reverse. For flight or swimming, no vertical ascent; maximum climb angle 60°. A flying unit must also move at least half its speed each round or begin to fall.
Most land vehicles at normal speed are Average.

**Clumsy**
Turning radius at most (your Size × 2) meters (about four occupied squares between turns); cannot switch directly from forward to reverse. For flight or swimming, no vertical ascent; maximum climb 45°. A flying unit must move at least half its speed each round or begin to fall.
Speedboats at normal speed and small ships at low speed are Clumsy.
\*Some resources call Clumsy "poor" — they are the same thing.

**None**
Turning radius above (your Size × 2) meters (at least four occupied squares between turns); cannot switch directly from forward to reverse. For flight or swimming, no vertical ascent; maximum climb 45°. A flying unit must move at least half its speed each round or begin to fall.
Airliners and large ships at normal speed, and charging units, are None.
Note: Mobility math is fiddly and mostly matters for vehicle driving. In general the ST need only track what a Mobility grade *cannot* do — flight below Good cannot ascend vertically; movement below Perfect needs distance to turn; and so on.

**Abilities used together**
Movement arts: qinggong, Nanaya body technique, and the like — ways of governing bodily movement.
Protective auras: protecting the body with energy.
Techniques: most practical martial arts, Chinese and foreign. In general, "using a non-free action to perform some act" is using a Technique.
Inner methods: usually passive, sometimes prerequisites for other Arts.
Stacking: as long as action costs don't clash, a player may use any number of Arts. The usual test for "cannot be used together" is 【abilities activated by spending a given action】: if you spend a standard action on an **all-out attack**, you cannot also trigger Baji Quan's **Inch Force** (both cost a standard action). Whether an Art is a maneuver, movement art, protective aura, or inner method, they combine freely if they spend different actions — e.g. spend a move action on Sensory Combat for a dodge bonus, then a reaction on Nine-Luminary Ultraspeed to convert damage when attacked.

**Basic attack**
A basic attack is generally "an ordinary attack with no Technique attached" — an attack with no specified action cost (though, of course, attacking still usually takes a Standard Action).
For example, "make a Blade attack as a Standard Action" is a Technique-driven Blade attack, not a basic one; "make a melee attack with an artificial weapon" is a Blade attack without a Technique — a basic Blade attack.
Other kinds of basic attack follow the same pattern: basic Brawl attacks, basic casting attacks, and so on.

**Pulling your blow**
After making a check, you may choose to take fewer successes than you actually rolled. For example, John attacks Henry with a sword and rolls a total of 7 successes; he may choose to deal only 3 damage rather than the normal 7.
Most attacks can be pulled — the player striking with less force, or shooting a non-lethal body part, and so on.
Some situations make pulling difficult. These include:
- Without special enhancement, you cannot pull blows during long bursts or full-auto fire.
- If you cannot pinpoint a character, you cannot pull blows against them.
- If two characters share a square, are grappling, or one possesses the other — anything preventing you from striking that character precisely — you cannot pull blows against them.
- Without special enhancement, in an attack striking several targets at once you cannot pull blows against only some of them (you may still pull against all targets of the attack together).

**Multi-target attacks and distinguishing friend from foe**
A multi-target attack affects several targets in one action — a grenade blast, a raking machine gun, a summoned poison cloud, a piercing laser, one bow loosing four arrows at four people: all are "multi-target attacks".

**Distinguishing friend from foe in multi-target attacks**
Some multi-target attacks discriminate: they read "attack/damage all 【enemies】 in the area", or "【choose】 N targets in the area to attack/damage".

Others do not: they read "attack/damage all 【characters/units/targets】 in the area", or simply "deal damage to the area".
Even with a discriminating multi-target attack, you can only exempt characters you can pinpoint and could strike accurately.
Example 1: player Jiang Shi enhances an ability that "attacks all enemies within 1,000 meters", but his sensory range is only 140 meters, and teammate Ellis — enhanced to be an unseeable werewolf — lurks nearby. He can spare the friendly characters within his sensory range; the undetected Ellis and everyone beyond his sensory range are all swept into the attack.
Example 2: player Beer is tangled in a grapple with four crocodile-men. Player Murong uses "choose up to 10 units within 5 meters" for a ranged attack; unwilling to take the "attacking into a grapple" penalty, he cannot distinguish Beer from the crocodile-men and must attack all 5 units.

**Stacking multi-target attacks**
Multiple simultaneous multi-target effects, unless stated otherwise, do not stack with one another — the caster chooses which effect each affected character receives.
Example 1: ability 1 — a standard action makes one attack loosing up to 5 arrows at 5 targets; ability 2 — arrows explode, the attack instead dealing 22L area damage in a 5-meter-radius circle. Combined, they produce at most five such areas — and even if two struck targets stand within 2 meters of each other, each takes one area damage, not two.
Example 2: player Fishbone enhances "attack every character within 10 meters" and "when attacking a character, also attack at −5 DP two characters within 2 meters of them". Directly ahead stand four enemies in a line, at 8, 9, 10, and 11 meters. Attacking with both abilities, the first three enemies each take one attack — Fishbone naturally picks the first, penalty-free ability to apply — and the fourth also takes just 1 attack, to which only the second ability can apply.
