# Experience System

**Experience system**

Experience (XP) is the growth a character earns from what they live through — characters with extraordinary experiences naturally hold more seasoning, experience, and knowledge than ordinary folk. XP raises a character's skills, buys new tricks (Feats), and hard training can strengthen body and mind alike. Certain enhancements from the Lord God also demand extra experience to understand and practice before they are truly mastered.

Any XP-discount ability may only be applied where the ability in question is learned with XP —

Feats, self-taught skills, and the like.

In the Infinite Horror TRPG, each player's XP tracks the quality of their performance. Generally the ST should note each player's earned experience at the end of every session according to that session's showing, and again at each film's end for the film's overall performance. Most of the time the ST can hand all of it out together when a film closes; but if a player's character gains a long stretch inside the film (or in some chapter outside one) to rest and train, the ST may release the earned XP early so they can raise the skills they mean to practice.

Broadly, XP buys the following:

Buying Stats. Raising a Stat by 1 costs (current Stat × 2) XP.

Buying skills. Raising a skill by 1 level costs (current level − 1) × 2 XP; raising from 0 to 1 costs 3 XP.

Buying Professions. 1 XP each.

Buying Feats. A Feat costs (the purchased level × 3) XP; multi-level Feats must be bought level by level in order. Samsara-team Feats cost (Feat level × 6) XP.

Buying or raising certain special abilities — as each ability describes.

Self-teaching skills — see the "self-taught skills" rules.

For new STs unsure how to hand out XP, here are two distribution schemes as worked examples, for reference only:

Scheme one: fixed distribution — roughly the same every game.

Each film (or each chapter) grants every surviving PC some experience, composed as follows:

X points of base experience (usually 10 — a fixed value pays long campaigns less and short ones more), plus points for each of a PC's fine moments, plus a settlement award (paid once):

The PC attended every session and took basic actions: 1 point.

The PC roleplayed well and in character: 1–3 points by quality.

The PC showed sustained, coherent, standout roleplay and character development across the film: 3–10 points by excellence.

The PC escaped near-certain death, or cracked a seemingly impossible problem: 1–3 points.

The PC held to behavior true to their nature from start to finish, and didn't abandon it even under heavy blows, setbacks, and trials: 2–6 points by the resistance and losses borne.

The PC acted with particular valor: 1 point.

The PC learned something. Case by case, raise the related skill 1 level — generally only when what was learned clearly exceeds their former level.

For instance, a character who had never touched a car learns to drive during the film and takes the wheel on a highway: raise Drives (Car) from 0 to 1.

The PC defeated exceptionally strong, pivotal enemies: 1–3 points weighed by their number and strength —

the Alien Queen in *Aliens*, or the dark lord Darth Vader in *Star Wars*.

Under the Virtue/Sin system, each Virtue trigger in the film grants 3 points and each Sin trigger 1. Under the character-trait system, each triggered trait grants its performance score in points.

Scheme two: dynamic distribution — scaled to how freely the ST pays out.

Split experience into 5 parts; sum whichever apply to a PC's showing for their total.

Part one: the base value, set by head-count — each PC above 5 in the party adds 1 point of base value. With 6 PCs, each gets 1 point of base experience.

Part two: reward experience — total every point and Tier all PCs earned this game: each D grants 1 XP, each 500 points grants 1 XP; divide by the number of PCs, round up, and each PC receives that reward experience.

Part three: attendance — the PC attended each session and took basic actions: 1–2 XP per session attended. (As a rule, a single session shouldn't run past 5 battles.)

Part four: performance awards — extra XP the ST grants for showing within the campaign: carrying the fighting, standout RP, plot contributions. Because it is meant as recognition, pay it out on a scale of (surviving PCs − 1): with 4 PCs, the best gets 3, the middling 2, the weaker 1, the weakest none. With 6 PCs, first place means 5 XP.

Part five: adjustments — the special part: fine-tuning by the ST against the game's point hauls, positive or negative. E.g.: for each PC who died, every living PC gains 1 XP. E.g.: a designed branch mission lost, or an important NPC dead — all PCs lose 1 XP.
