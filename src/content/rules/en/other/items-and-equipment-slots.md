# Items and Equipment Slots

**Basic item rules:**

Every blade weapon has its own weapon classification — see the basic cold-weapon templates. Some classifications, like whips and nunchaku, sit outside the templates but exist all the same.

Every firearm has its classification too, its templates being pistol, rifle, machine gun, shotgun, cannon.

Special weapons are classified as special weapons, though finer classifications still apply.

If a weapon carries the "Longsword" classification, it gains everything "Longsword" holds in the basic cold-weapon template — classification, Profession, properties, and Size; duplicated properties are not gained twice.

If a resource takes a basic cold weapon as its template, it gains all of that weapon's properties, with only the conflicting parts replaced by the new.

In the example above, the weapon gains an extra 1 DP on all-out attacks. But if the weapon's other properties already grant 2 DP on all-out attacks, the final bonus is 2 DP, not 3.

And if such a template weapon is chosen as a "Barehand Weapon", then unless stated otherwise its weapon damage becomes "raise both hands' natural-weapon damage by X (generally L)" — X following No Tier 1, D 1, C 2, B 4, A 8, S 16.

Technique compatibility:

Every blade weapon carries the passive property of Technique compatibility: a blade weapon can serve Techniques up to its own Tier + 1. A D-Tier weapon can only deliver Techniques up to C-Tier; above S counts as S. Deliver a Technique above the weapon's compatibility, and after one use the weapon shatters — lost for good.

All artificial Brawl weapons (fist wraps and such) carry Technique compatibility too, as above; without an artificial Brawl weapon, the limit doesn't apply.

Natural weapons have no Technique compatibility; a natural weapon's Tier is the higher of its own and any artificial Brawl weapon (fist wraps).

Weapon damage caps:
Every non-natural weapon has a damage cap of (its original weapon damage × 2) — Techniques, abilities, and properties can at most double a weapon's damage. With the 【Divine Arms】 property, the cap becomes (original weapon damage × 3).
Unless stated otherwise, weapon damage added by enchantment or modification counts into the weapon's original damage.

For weapons that can upgrade, unseal, awaken, and the like, pay the points named in their descriptions.

Launch-type ranged weapons compute their attack's Tier as the higher of the weapon's and the ammunition's.

Launch-type ranged weapons using special ammunition stack the ammunition's effects with the weapon's; conflicts resolve in the ammunition's favor.

Weapon size and hiding: Size 1 = hides under clothes; Size 2 = hides under an overcoat; Size 3 or more = cannot be hidden on the body. Each 2 points of your build above 5 raises these thresholds by 1.

Unless stated otherwise, enchantments and reinforcements on an item do not change its original Tier.

Attacks with a weapon, unless stated otherwise, inherit all the weapon's property effects — area damage included.

**Broad weapon classes:**

Melee weapons: everything that isn't ranged — blade weapons and natural weapons included.

Ranged weapons: everything with range — bows, crossbows, firearms, cannon: weapons without 【Throwing Weapon】 among their special properties and with a base range among their basic properties.

Solid-round weapons: ranged weapons without 【Energy Weapon】 among their special properties, or whose ammunition isn't made of energy (the Inscribed Bow, say).

Firearm weapons: everything classified pistol, rifle, machine gun, shotgun, or cannon.

Artificial weapons: everything but natural weapons. Only artificial weapons can be enchanted.

Blade weapons: in essence any weapon can make Blade attacks; weapons that strike with their own body are blade weapons.

Barehand weapons: weapons with 【Barehand Weapon】 among their special properties.

Most weapons carry usage prerequisites — generally Stat, skill, and Profession.

Classification:

Classification governs which techniques a weapon serves. A weapon with two or more classifications matches more kinds of weapon skills.

Prerequisites:

Items carry STR requirements; unstated, the requirement equals the item's Size.

For Stat and skill: each point short imposes a 2 DP Machinery Penalty on use; 3 or more short and the weapon is unusable — STR 3 cannot use a STR-7 weapon; STR 6 uses it at a 6 DP Machinery Penalty.

Missing-Profession penalty: lacking a required Profession imposes a 3 DP Machinery Penalty; a weapon using several Professions costs 3 DP per missing one, and with none of them the weapon is unusable.

Amulets, necklaces, cloaks, and other magical items and equipment must spend more than 1 Scene with their wearer before their effects work.

Armor Defense, Shield Defense, or Born Defense currently in effect still applies, unless stated otherwise, when the wearer faces area-damage attacks: if the area damage allows a Reflex save, the wearer gains an equal Machinery Bonus on that save.

If the area damage carries Breach, the Breach applies as normal — reducing the Machinery Bonus that armor, shield, and Born Defense lent the Reflex save.

Consumables: no matter what (unless specially stated), items classified as consumables cannot be projected, copied, or similarly duplicated.

No matter what, when time is rewound, consumables already spent do not return.

A consumable item, unless stated otherwise, is lost after one use — once used, it generally cannot be used again.

All potions and elixirs among consumables, unless stated otherwise, take a Movement Action to drink.

No matter what (unless specially stated), consumables cannot gain the 【Bound】 property.

Items produced by copying, projection, and the like cannot use abilities with limited uses.

The Breach and Dispelling in weapons' basic properties are all of the "obtain" wording.

No Eldritch-Category item can be projected or manufactured unless specially stated.

For projected items or items with durations: unless stated otherwise, placing them in "spatial storage devices" or similar time-stopped storage does not stretch their time — the duration instead counts as ending the instant they go in.

※ Item Hardness and HP rules: note this too is optional. If your players insist on it, remind them your story characters may equally well smash the equipment they toiled to buy.

An object's Hardness marks how… "beating-resistant" it is. Each attack an item takes is offset by its Hardness (whether the damage is physical or energy) — a Hardness-2 object hit for 3 takes 1. An item's HP is also called its Structure, and Blunt, Lethal, and Aggravated damage all reduce Structure directly. Hardness by material:

| Hardness | Material |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Cork, hard rubber, thin glass |
| 3 | Wood |
| 5 | Stone, aluminum |
| 7 | Steel, iron |
| 13 | Adamantine |
| +1 | Per enchantment level on the item, or per layer of reinforcement (composite armor) |

If an item states no Structure or Hardness (and you mean to break it), its Structure generally equals its **Size + Hardness**. An item judged fine and fragile in construction (technological artifacts) loses 1–5 Structure, ST's call. Also, each D Tier in an item's price grants it 5 HP and 2 Hardness — a C-Tier item gets +15 HP and +6 Hardness.

Note that an item's Hardness does nothing for the wearer's defense:

armor's own Hardness wins the character no advantage on defense — it matters only when someone tries to sunder the armor.

The same goes for vehicles — though for fully sealed vehicles, the driver cannot be attacked without special abilities.

**General rules for divine artifacts:**

Some items have left undying names in their own worlds — every one a symbol of miracle, a summit of glory. Famed or infamous, each carries countless hopes or curses, and that weight is exactly what sets them apart from mass-produced gear.

Divine artifacts come in two kinds: Divine Arms, and divine artifacts proper.

Every divine artifact is a Divine Arm; the converse does not hold.

Many weapons and armors are glorious purely for their power and legend — some mightier than any artifact — but these can only be 【Divine Arms】. What deserves the name of divine artifact must be equipment housing an artifact spirit.

【Divine artifacts】

1. A divine artifact cannot be destroyed.

2. Every divine artifact has two parts: the body and the artifact spirit.

3. The spirit's manifestation is a projection of the body it inhabits, so unless the artifact's body is damaged, the manifested spirit cannot be wholly destroyed by any means — though it can be dispelled or driven off.

【Divine Arms】

1. A Divine Arm touches spirits and incorporeals as though present in every domain: Divine Arm weapons damage spirits normally, and the defense of Divine Arm armor and shields turns spirits' attacks.

2. A Divine Arm can guard against lightsabers and their kind.

3. A Divine Arm breaks the damage reductions "/Divine Arms", "/magic", and any specific material except "/adamantine" and "/silver".

4. When a Divine Arm strikes a non-Divine-Arm object, the object's Hardness halves.

The body

A divine artifact's body is generally an item, with a normal item classification.

The artifact spirit

Every divine artifact houses its spirit somewhere, with three attributes bearing directly on the weapon's performance:

1. Awareness — the spirit's power to touch the real world.

2. Mind — the spirit's power to think and understand.

3. Affinity — the spirit's closeness to its bearer and to the natural world.

Spirit advancement: some Divine Arms earn XP equal to their bearer's while held — evolving Divine Arms. In story terms, you fight alongside the weapon, grow into step with it, or grow up together.

Spirit manifestation: some spirits can manifest incorporeally. They have no HP, take no damage, and exert no influence on the world. They cannot stray from the weapon (radius: 10 meters per point of Affinity), and converse with the bearer by telepathy or plain speech.

Spirit alignment: in the hands of an incompatible alignment, the spirit's Affinity counts as 0 and does nothing at all — only the body's effects remain.

**Equipment and equipment-slot rules**

The Lord God Space sells conveniences and powers — indestructibility, a thousand li a day, riding the clouds. But nobody can wear every good thing at once. What one body can use is limited — sometimes by common sense, sometimes by the items' own nature.

**Magic items and tech items**

Among items with special functions, some run on high technology — machinery and power plants — and others on mysterious forces: magic, divine power, the spirit-qi of heaven and earth. Tech items obey universal scientific principle and don't interfere with one another; supernatural power is mysterious, and must knit its energy field to the user's life and soul before it works.

Specially, some magic items are made to be worn at the bodily position tied to a slot without themselves occupying it: a gem bearing "Gem Bomb" might be set in a ring and worn on a finger (touching the ring slot), yet not being a magic ring, it occupies no ring slot.

**Slots and their bodily positions**

Some items work merely carried; others must sit on the body in a particular way. Only the latter kind has anything to do with equipment slots.

**Equipment slots:**

A slot expresses the essential mutual exclusion of certain item classes — their inability to work together.

A slot ties to a bodily position, but doesn't mean the position itself; it means the position's limit on items that must be worn there. Of a slot-limited item class, only one works (rings may allow two or more) — sometimes because the item's supernatural field must interact with the body's at that spot, sometimes because the spot bears special weight in magical or religious symbolism. Either way, if an item must be worn at a slot's position without itself occupying the slot, the slot counts as free. (Though the item worn there may still keep slot-occupying items from being equipped properly.)

Multiple items in one slot: sometimes several items ride one slot — several rings on the hands, or a magic headscarf, hat, and helmet all at once. Only one item (or the slot's allowed number) works; unless stated otherwise, the highest-Tier item wins.

**Continuous, triggered, and activated items**

Continuous items work constantly once properly worn and enabled. Continuous magic items that must be worn at a slot's position count as occupying the slot.

Triggered items, once properly worn and enabled, fire automatically when a condition is met — the life-warding jade pendant, say. Triggered items count as a special kind of continuous item, so triggered magic items worn at a slot's position count as occupying it.

Activated items produce an instantaneous effect — or a very short-lived one — each time they are switched on. Even worn at a slot's position, such a magic item occupies no slot while dormant or when its effect is instantaneous. If activation produces a brief lasting effect, the item occupies the slot for the duration, handled under "multiple items in one slot".

**Donning and doffing**

Donning or doffing equipment usually takes a Standard Action — about 6 seconds out of combat. Armor is the exception: light armor takes a full Turn (about 1 minute out of combat); heavy armor takes 1 minute (about 5 out of combat).

**Bodily positions and slots**

Note these slots are described on the human template. If your creature changes into something else — beast, insect — the matching slots are lost unless stated otherwise:

a horse can neither wield a weapon nor wear a ring.

And gaining extra limbs still leaves only one of each matching slot working — except the "held" position:

an octopus cannot wear 8 rings, but it can hold 8 weapons.

Head slots:

Helmet slot

The helmet slot spans the whole head, or its upper half including the back of the skull — home to magic hats, arcane helms, magic headscarves, ornaments, masks. One item works here.

Body slots:

Amulet slot

The amulet slot ties to the neck — the great arteries, the cervical spine, everything bound tightly to life and function. Amulets, necklaces, collars and the like occupy it. One item works here.

Cloak slot

The cloak slot is peculiar: it ties to the body's whole outer surface, so cloaks and capes worn over all clothing and armor occupy it. With several items here, energy grade counts for nothing — only the outermost works.

Belt slot

The belt slot holds gear worn at the waist — the joint between above and below; some special belts carry special effects. One belt per character.

Armor slot:

Generally a character wears one suit of armor, which guards the body from harm.

Layered armor rules:

Layering generally lets you wear one suit of light armor inside a suit of heavy armor.

Classical armor tends to be layered already, so you cannot wear a mail shirt inside knight's plate (the plate already carries mail as its lining). But you might wear a bulletproof vest inside knight's plate, or a mail shirt inside powered armor.

Wearing several suits, your Armor Defense comes only from the best of them, and armor special properties and traits come only from the innermost suit — even where their workings differ. (The outer armor is 【Bulletproof】 and grants STR +1; the inner is 【Energyproof】 and grants AGI +1 — you get 【Energyproof】 and AGI +1 only.)

An enemy weapon's Breach must chew through your armor layer by layer: only Breach fully exceeding the first layer carries its remainder to the second. Breach against the second layer = the weapon's Breach − the first layer's Armor Defense.

If Breach wholly defeats the first layer while the first layer was the higher — and effective — Armor Defense, you lose your Armor Defense, but the second layer's special properties survive so long as it stands unbroken.

If Breach wholly defeats the first layer while the effective Armor Defense was the second layer's, you keep the second layer's Armor Defense, computing post-Breach defense as normal.

Multiple suits hinder movement: their Armor Penalties stack, plus an extra (number of suits worn × 2).

Specially, worn liquid-metal armor doesn't count as armor for layering purposes, though it still occupies the light-armor slot.

Limb slots:

Glove slot

The glove slot ties to both hands — gloves, gauntlets, bracers and their kin occupy it. One pair of gloves, gauntlets, or bracers works here, and most such items must be worn on both hands.

Ring slot

Rings tie to the fingers. The finger's veins run to the heart, touching the essence of life, and the ring itself is symbolic of standing and fate. Ordinarily one ring works per hand.

Footwear slot

The footwear slot ties to the feet — magic boots, leggings, and the like. Most such items work only as a pair.

Related matters:

Held:

"Held" is not a slot, but most held items' special properties demand proper use before they work.

Note that only beings with the concept of a "hand" — humans, humanoids, primates — can hold things.

Hooves (cattle, horses), claws (lions, leopards), fins (fish, lizards), and webbed feet (frogs, ducks) can never hold, unless specially stated.

Concept armaments:

Concept armaments take no fixed form and usually carry some attached concept; their item classification must contain words defining them as 【Concept Armament】.

Some concept armaments are attachments: fixed to an object, they work only while that object is used.

Some hold a slot of their own while also being concept armaments — occupying both at once.

Some lie dormant, activated per their descriptions — but if the matching slot is occupied, they cannot take effect. Unless stated otherwise, ending a concept armament's effect is a Free Action.

Some switch on when gained and never switch off.

You may equip only one concept armament at a time — carry as many as you like, but all except one lie dead.

Slotless items:

Some items need no special position — worn or carried anywhere on the body, they work. Carry any sensible number, but same-named effects don't stack: only the best applies. And even carry-to-work gear must ride directly on the body — stowed in extradimensional carriers like hyperspace armlets, storage rings, or transformation capsules, it does nothing.

Having no slots:

Some shape-changing and similar effects cost a character certain slots — transformation, limb disability, and more:

lose both hands and you wield nothing; lose both legs and you wear no shoes.

**Carrying items:**

Characters carry sundries in limited number: each point of your Size (or STR, if lower) lets you carry 100 rounds of ammunition or the like, or 20 arrows / 10 gold bars / 10 grenades or the like (containers included — bandoliers, quivers, ammo boxes). Each point of Legendary STR counts your Size 1 higher. You may overload: each unit over counts your STR and AGI 1 lower, until either hits 0.

You may carry items freely in any sensible fashion. An ordinary Size-5 person can hide Size-0 props anywhere on their body, tuck Size-1 props into sleeves or pockets, and hide Size-2 props under an overcoat; Size 3 and up cannot be hidden by ordinary means. Total carried Size cannot exceed your Size + STR.

This rule governs ordinary carrying of sundries, apart from encumbrance — a player may be able to lift very heavy things they cannot lug around daily.

Daily carried weight must stay within light load; at medium load STR and AGI halve, at heavy load they drop to 1/3, and beyond heavy load nothing can be carried.

**Exclusive items**

Some resources carry purchasable exclusive items. Unlocking their purchase is a prerequisite like opening a skill tree: only characters who have opened the exchange may buy and use them. Unless stated otherwise, exclusive items cannot be used — or have any property triggered — by anyone else.

**Carrying capacity**

Tracking weights in play is tedious, but deciding whether a character's STR can carry an object has its place; STR and Size adjustments set carrying capacity.
Ordinarily encumbrance serves only the ST's call on whether something can be carried; actual quantities follow the carrying rules above.

| STR | Light load | Medium load | Heavy load |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | up to 13 kg | 14–26 kg | 27–40 kg |
| 2 | up to 16 kg | 17–33 kg | 34–50 kg |
| 3 | up to 21 kg | 22–43 kg | 44–65 kg |
| 4 | up to 29 kg | 30–58 kg | 59–88 kg |
| 5 | up to 37 kg | 38–76 kg | 77–115 kg |
| 6 | up to 50 kg | 50–100 kg | 101–150 kg |
| 7 | up to 66 kg | 67–133 kg | 134–200 kg |
| 8 | up to 86 kg | 87–173 kg | 174–260 kg |
| 9 | up to 126 kg | 127–233 kg | 234–350 kg |
| 10 | up to 153 kg | 154–306 kg | 307–460 kg |

For creatures whose STR exceeds the table, find the load from the STR's ones digit, then multiply by 12 for each full 10 in the tens: 21–30 multiplies by 144, 31–40 by 1,728, and so on.

For example, a STR-26 creature: ones digit 6, tens digit 2, so base load 50 × 12 × 12 = 7,200 kg. With 5 Legendary STR adding 8,000 kg under the Legendary rules, the total is 15,200 kg.

For example, a STR-30 creature: ones digit 0 (use 10), tens digit 3 (in fact take 2, 30 being 20 above 10) — base load 153 × 144 = 22,032 kg. With 5 Legendary STR adding 8,000 kg, the total is 30,032 kg.

A character can lift their maximum load overhead, can briefly heave twice their maximum off the ground — creeping 2 meters per Turn as a Whole-round Action — and can push or drag five times their maximum.

Apply this rule wherever carrying capacity matters: hauling an object, shoving or dragging something heavy.
