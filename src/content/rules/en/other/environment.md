# Environment Rules

Environment:

Everything an environment brings counts as environmental influence.

Environmental influence covers 3 parts:

Environmental effects: certain environments produce particular special effects.

Environmental penalties: certain environmental effects penalize characters at certain actions.

Environmental damage: harsh environments damage characters outright.

All environmental influence is Natural-Category and No-Tier.

Matching immunities cover only their matching part — unless the immunity is to environmental influence itself, which covers everything the environment brings.

Most environments aren't single environments; some are composites — merge a landslide with lava and you have a volcanic eruption.

---

**Forest**

Forest terrain splits into three kinds: sparse woods, ordinary forest, and dense forest, appearing across different films.

Assign terrain to match the film in question.

Generally one forest may hold all three — sparse woods at the fringes, dense forest at the heart.

Telling them apart:

Sparse woods: no towering trees; on average, a 50% chance of an ordinary tree every 2 meters.

Ordinary: a few towering trees; on average, a 70% chance of an ordinary tree — or 10% of a towering one — every 2 meters.

Dense: some towering trees; on average, an 80% chance of an ordinary tree — or 20% of a towering one — every 2 meters.

The forest's defining feature is its trees. Generally an ordinary tree grants one target slight cover; a towering tree grants one target partial cover.

Undergrowth — vines, roots, low shrubs and everything else strewn across a forest floor — carpets the ground. All forest floor counts as difficult terrain. Forests also hold thickets:

thickets appear only in ordinary and dense forest — a 20% chance per 2 meters in ordinary forest, 50% in dense.

A character may share a thicket's spot. In one, the thicket grants slight cover, Stealth (Hiding) checks gain a 5 DP Environmental Bonus, Stealth (Sneaking) and Athletics-related checks take a 5 DP Environmental Penalty, and movement costs quadruple movement (not the usual double of difficult terrain).

Environmental effects:

Because of the trees, sightlines differ by forest:

sparse woods: maximum sight range 6d10 meters;

ordinary forest: 5d10 meters;

dense forest: 4d10 meters.

Note this caps only maximum sight range — not other sensory or rough ranges, nor blindsense or blindsight.

A forest's noises hamper listening: on PCT + Investigation (Listening) checks, the DC rises 1 per 5 meters between you and the source.

Other forest features:

Fallen giant trunks: grant cover by Size under the cover rules.

Forest brooks: rarely over 4 meters wide, 1 meter deep.

Forest paths: travel along them does not count as difficult terrain.

Forest fires:

Most PCs will sooner or later start setting fires — or dry weather, lightning into a tree, or the film's own plot will — and then a forest fire shows the characters exactly what danger looks like.

A character may spot the fire with PCT + Investigation (Scouting): each success reveals fire up to 100 meters away, capped by their rough scouting range.

If everyone in the party fails, then the moment flames spread into a character's sightline (or into some special sense like blindsight), the fire is noticed automatically.

A character with no scouting means at all (blind, no special abilities) notices at 30 meters by the sheer heat.

Downwind, fire spreads terrifyingly fast — at 1.5 × the current wind speed.

Windless, it spreads 5 meters per round.

Upwind, fire doesn't spread against the wind — it runs downwind at the wind's speed.

Once alight, the region burns out within 8d10 minutes.

**Swamps:**

Swamp terrain comes in two kinds: wet mire and drier bogland.

Swamps generally form beside lakes or where lakes once lay — but however it looks, if the film calls the ground a swamp, the ST should furnish one.

The commonest swamp ground is wetland; beyond it lie water-holes and man-eating mires.

Wetland: all wetland is difficult terrain, and while on it, all Reflex checks take a 2 DP Environmental Penalty — an environmental effect.

Water-holes: filthy water about 1–2 meters deep. Creatures of Size 4–8 spend 4 meters of movement per meter here; creatures of Size 3 or below must "swim" to move at all. Any target of Size 8 or below in a water-hole gains 4 points of cover.

A water-hole is usually ringed by wetland.

And all Stealth (Sneaking)-related checks in wetland or water-holes take a 2 DP Environmental Penalty.

Man-eating mire: it usually looks perfectly solid — like ordinary ground or wetland. A target moving at normal speed may roll PCT + Survival (Wilderness) at DC 2 to spot the mire before stepping in; anyone running, charging, or the like gets no chance. Once in, the character rolls Athletics (Swimming) at DC 2 each round: success holds them where they are, failure sinks them 1 point of Size — and when the sunken Size equals their own, they're under, entering suffocation. A margin above 5 lets them shift 1 meter sideways.

If the sinking character can pull toward the bank (grabbing grass or branches), the check becomes STR + Athletics (Swimming). Helpers follow the assistance rules.

Swamps generally block neither sight nor listening.

Other swamp features:

Besides the ground itself, swamps may hold ordinary trees, thickets, and brambles.

Brambles: common in swampland, usually matted with stone and mud into something like a low wall. They grant cover by Size under the cover rules. Crossing them takes STR + Athletics (Climbing) at DC 3; on a failure, each point of margin deals 1 Blunt damage.

**Hills:**

Hill terrain can thread through any other terrain, or stand alone.

Insert it anywhere to compose a landscape — in some fantasy films, nearly everything that isn't forest is hills.

Hills sit between plain and mountain — an utterly common terrain.

Hill ground is intricate, all rise and fall, in 3 kinds:

Gentle slopes: normal terrain heading down, difficult terrain heading up — though this difficult terrain permits charges. An environmental effect.

Steep slopes: normal terrain heading down — but charging (or the like) downhill takes AGI + Athletics (Balance) at DC 2; on a failure, fall prone and slide 1d2 × (current speed / 6) meters or until the slope ends, then take Blunt damage of half the remaining slide. Heading up is difficult terrain. An environmental effect.

Cliffs: sheer rock faces, impassable by normal movement, typically 2d10 meters high.

Hills generally block neither sight nor listening.

Note, though: a character on higher ground gains cover against those below, by the difference in height:

over 10 meters — slight cover;

over 20 meters — partial cover;

over 40 meters — heavy cover.

Other hill features:

Hills carry the odd tree and thicket, and streams often run through the lowest hollows.

Ordinary trees and thickets: see "Forest".

Valley streams: rarely over 4 meters wide, 2 deep — and always running toward the lowest ground.

**Mountains**

Mountain terrain appears rather rarely in films. Mountain country is vast; a range generally builds in 3 stages — entering it, a character meets the gentler alpine ground, then the rugged peaks, and finally the summit.

Mountains hold plenty of tangled ground:

Gentle slopes: as in hills.

Steep slopes: as in hills.

Cliffs: as in hills, but loftier — alpine cliffs match the hills', peak-country cliffs reach 4d10 meters, and summit cliffs 8d10 meters.

Crevasses: torn open by geological upheaval — pits, in effect, and deadly ones. A character over a crevasse falls into the mountain's body. Crevasses run 4d10 meters deep, 3–5 long, 3–15 wide; climbing out rates as ordinary rock wall.

Different stages mix the ground differently:

Alpine: 50% gentle slopes, 40% steep, 10% cliffs — broadly mild.

Peaks: 25% gentle, 55% steep, 15% cliffs, 5% crevasses — markedly steeper and more dangerous.

Summit: gentle slopes just 15%, steep 55%, cliffs up to 20%, crevasses 10% — precipitous, perilous, wretched climbing.

Mountains generally don't block sight.

High mountains do carry sound farther: your auditory sensory range doubles up there.

And again, height grants cover against those below:

over 10 meters — slight cover;

over 20 meters — partial cover;

over 40 meters — heavy cover.

Other mountain features:

Thickets: see "Forest" — though scarce, and all but gone by the summit.

Scree: ground littered with small stones — difficult terrain. Athletics (Balance) and Stealth (Sneaking) checks on it take a 2 DP Environmental Penalty. An environmental effect.

Dense scree: stones of every size — difficult terrain. Athletics (Balance) checks take a 4 DP Environmental Penalty, Stealth (Sneaking) 2 DP. An environmental effect.

Mountain streams: rarely over 4 meters wide, 2 deep — always running toward the lowest ground.

Special mountain features:

Caves: one or more mouths, usually opening from rock — cliff or steep slope.

Altitude: moving at high altitude, for creatures unused to it, is grueling — even lethal. The cold and the thin air punish most living things.

Note: creatures that don't breathe — undead, constructs, elementals, spirits — ignore altitude.

Altitude bands:

Low altitude: below 3,000 meters — mostly alpine terrain, with no further environmental influence.

Middle altitude: 3,000–9,000 meters — the tops of lesser ranges, or the still-traveled belts of greater ones. Every creature unacclimated to mountains labors for breath: a Fortitude check each hour at DC 2, +1 per 6 hours. Each point of failure inflicts 1 fatigue point, stacking without limit and never recovering until the character returns to normal conditions, at which point saves resume as usual. An environmental effect.

High altitude: above 9,000 meters — an extreme no normal creature can acclimate to; every non-immune creature suffers. A Fortitude check each hour at DC 2, +1 per 6 hours: each point of failure inflicts 1 fatigue point, stacking without limit and never recovering. And every 6 hours a further Fortitude check at DC 2, +1 per 6 hours: each failure deals 1 point of Stats Damage to every Stat, unrecovering. Only on returning to normal conditions can the fatigue and Stats Damage be saved off. An environmental effect.

Special events:

Landslides:

A landslide sends masses of rock tumbling from above — the deadliest crisis in the mountains.

A character may spot one with PCT + Investigation (Scouting): each success reveals a slide up to 200 meters off, capped by their rough scouting range.

If the whole party fails, the slide is noticed automatically once it enters sightline (or a special sense like blindsight).

A character with no scouting means may roll PCT + Investigation (Listening): each success detects the slide at 50 meters.

A landslide has two zones:

Burial zone: a character here cannot escape harm — 20L of falling-object damage, Reflex save, every 3 successes upgrading the damage one grade toward Blunt (at best Blunt). They are then pinned beneath the rubble.

Slide zone: a character here can still get clear — 20L of falling-object damage, Reflex save, each 3 successes reducing the damage by 1; at 0 they walk away unscathed, otherwise they too are pinned beneath the rubble.

A pinned character takes 1 Lethal physical damage per minute, ignoring damage reduction.

Landslides run enormous — up to 3d10 × 10 meters across, the burial zone at the center. The rockfall descends at about 150 meters per round.

**Deserts**

Deserts are common everywhere — above all in anything Egyptian —

and plenty of Westerns borrow desert elements too.

Desert terrain also splits into three:

Ice desert: the cold-belt kind — Siberia. 25% ice sheet, 5% scree, 15% thickets.

Stone desert: the temperate kind — the badlands of the West fit here. 30% scree and dense scree, 5% thickets.

Ordinary desert: the tropical kind — the "pure" desert. 5% thickets, 10% scree, 5% dense scree, and 50% dunes.

Note: ice deserts sit under ice and snow, so water comes relatively easily; and in summer the ice may melt, turning ice desert into swamp — minus the water-holes.

Features unique to deserts:

Dunes: built by wind, and mobile — covering hundreds of square meters and creeping under the wind's steady push. A dune's windward face is a gentle slope, its lee a steep one.

Ice sheet: a skin of ice over the ground — difficult terrain. It can be ignored: to do so, moving across it takes AGI + Athletics (Balance) at DC 2 — success lets you ignore it for the round, failure drops you prone. Charging or the like raises the DC to 4. An environmental effect.

Deserts generally block neither sight nor listening.

Other desert features:

Thickets: see "Forest".

Scree: as in mountains — difficult terrain; Athletics (Balance) and Stealth (Sneaking) take a 2 DP Environmental Penalty. An environmental effect.

Dense scree: as in mountains — difficult terrain; Athletics (Balance) takes 4 DP, Stealth (Sneaking) 2 DP. An environmental effect.

Quicksand: see "man-eating mire" under "Swamps".

**Underwater combat**

Combat adjustments:

All ranged attacks made in water, or at underwater targets, abandon their normal ranges: lacking 【Penetrating】 or 【Super Penetrating】, their base range becomes the lower of 20 meters and their own base range.

Natural-Category flame cannot burn underwater.

Water pressure:

Pressure hampers action markedly and wounds outright.

Unprotected, past every 30 meters of depth a character makes a Fortitude check per minute at DC 3, +1 per check; each failure deals 1 unavoidable Lethal damage. An environmental effect.

Unprotected, each 10 meters of depth inflicts 1 unsaveable entanglement point. An environmental effect.

Underwater sight depends on clarity: clear water shows 4d10 × 5 meters, murky water 1d10 × 5. All rapids count as murky whatever their clarity; only still water and slow current can be clear. An environmental effect.

Special events:

Floods: many films bring floods. All rivers in flood rise 2d10 meters and widen 1–2 times over. Swimming-related checks lose 2 further successes, and all current speeds rise 1.5× — still water that even so falls short of slow current becomes minimum-speed slow current.

**Weather:**

All weather effects are environmental effects.

Weather runs through every film; here are a few simple weathers — for anything richer, the ST can blend several:

rain + snow, say, merges the traits of "rain" and "snow" (taking the higher) into a new weather:

Sleet: snowfall cuts visibility sharply — sight range halves, and Investigation (Scouting) and Investigation (Search) take a 4 DP Environmental Penalty. Settled snow counts as difficult terrain.

Rain:

Rain cuts visibility sharply — sight range halves, and Investigation (Scouting) and Investigation (Search) take a 4 DP Environmental Penalty.

Snow:

Snowfall cuts visibility sharply — sight range halves, and Investigation (Scouting) and Investigation (Search) take a 4 DP Environmental Penalty. Settled snow counts as difficult terrain.

Hail:

Hail spares the eyes but batters the ears — Investigation (Listening) takes a 4 DP Environmental Penalty. And under hail, each round carries a 10% chance of 1 physical Lethal bludgeoning damage.

Storms:

Base effect: gale and precipitation together (snow, sand, or hail as the case may be) slash visibility — sight range drops to a quarter, and ranged attack checks, Investigation (Scouting), Investigation (Search), and Investigation (Listening) all take an 8 DP Environmental Penalty.

Sandstorm: beyond the base effect, anyone exposed takes 1 Lethal physical damage per minute, ignoring damage reduction — and with sand pouring into mouth and nose, enters suffocation. Covering mouth and nose with something delays the suffocation until (EDR × 5) rounds pass.

Blizzard: lays snow, and brings low temperatures with it.

Thunderstorm: each minute one great bolt falls for 40 Lethal lightning damage — a 1% chance of striking a person, rising to 5% in metal heavy armor.

Wind:

Calm: 0–52 meters per round — no effect.

High wind: 52–78 meters per round — Investigation (Listening) takes 2 DP; ranged attacks take 2 DP. Targets under Size 2 make a Fortitude check at DC 2 or fall prone.

Gale: 78–130 meters per round — Investigation (Listening) 4 DP; ranged attacks 4 DP. All targets under Size 7 make a Fortitude check at DC 4. Failing under Size 2: blown 1 meter per point of failure, 1 Lethal physical damage per meter. Failing under Size 4: prone. Failing under Size 7: unable to advance upwind.

Storm: 130–182 meters per round — Investigation (Listening) 8 DP; ranged attacks 8 DP. All targets under Size 14 make a Fortitude check at DC 6. Failing under Size 4: blown 1 meter per point of failure, 1 Lethal per meter. Under Size 7: prone. Under Size 14: unable to advance upwind.

Hurricane: 182–418 meters per round — Investigation (Listening) 16 DP; ranged attacks 16 DP, melee attacks 4 DP. All targets under Size 29 make a Fortitude check at DC 8. Failing under Size 7: blown 1 meter per point of failure, 1 Lethal per meter. Under Size 14: prone. Under Size 29: unable to advance upwind.

Tornado: over 418 meters per round — Investigation (Listening) is impossible, so are ranged attacks; melee attacks take 8 DP. All targets make a Fortitude check at DC 14. Failing under Size 14: blown 1 meter per point of failure, 1 Lethal per meter. Under Size 29: prone. Above Size 29: unable to advance upwind.

A character blown away is sucked into the tornado, tumbling inside for 1d10 rounds at 20 Lethal physical damage per round until the funnel flings them out (falling damage as normal).

Special: flying creatures count one Size class smaller — Size 9 counts as "under Size 7".

Special: a flying creature unable to advance is blown back an extra 1d10 meters. A flying creature "knocked prone" is blown back an extra 2d10 meters; a flying creature blown away doubles its margin of failure.

Fog: fog veils sight; sight range varies with the fog's thickness.

**Temperature:**

All temperature effects are environmental effects.

In hot and cold conditions a character consumes double water and food; in extreme heat and extreme cold, triple.

Falling short brings on hunger and thirst.

| Temperature | Grade |
| --- | --- |
| Above 60 °C | Extreme heat |
| 43–59 °C | Scorching |
| 32–42 °C | Hot |
| 4–31 °C | Normal |
| 5 to −17 °C | Cold |
| Below −18 °C | Extreme cold |

Heat:

Above 32 °C, all Physical checks take a 1 DP Environmental Penalty, and each hour demands a Fortitude check at DC 3, +1 per check; each failure deals 1 fire Blunt damage.

Above 43 °C, all Physical checks take 2 DP, and every 10 minutes demands a Fortitude check at DC 3, +1 per check; each failure deals 1 fire Blunt damage.

Extreme heat: above 60 °C, all Physical checks take 4 DP, and every minute demands a Fortitude check at DC 3, +1 per check; each failure deals 1 fire Lethal damage.

When a character's HP fills with Blunt damage (from this environment or any other cause) while in this environment, they enter exhaustion at once.

Specially, the Armor Penalty applies to these Fortitude checks, and damage from this environment does not heal naturally until the target leaves the extreme temperature.

Cold:

Below 5 °C without any protection against it, all Physical checks take a 1 DP Environmental Penalty, and each hour demands a Fortitude check at DC 3, +1 per check; each failure deals 1 cold Blunt damage.

Extreme cold: below −18 °C unprotected, all Physical checks take 1 DP, and each hour demands a Fortitude check at DC 3, +1 per check; each failure deals 1 cold Blunt damage plus 1 stacking freeze point.

Specially, damage and freeze points from this environment do not recover naturally until the target leaves the extreme temperature.

**Fires:**

A character caught in a fire faces three harms:

Scorching: within the fire's area, each round deals 9 DP of fire Lethal damage, no save. Also, every 5 rounds demands a Fortitude check at DC 2, +1 per minute; each failure deals 1 Blunt damage. Any Armor Penalty applies to these Fortitude checks. This damage is environmental damage.

Catching fire: deep in a blaze, a lapse of attention sets you alight. Each minute demands a Reflex check at DC 2; each failure deals 9 DP fire Lethal damage — environmental damage — plus an equal number of burn points.

Smoke: anyone inside a fire suffers the effects of "smoke".

---

**Sealed spaces:**

A creature of Size 4–6 in a sealed 3-cubic-meter space breathes normally for 6 hours. Past that, every 15 minutes brings an EDR check at DC equal to their own EDR, dealing Blunt damage equal to the margin; when Blunt damage fills their HP, the character falls unconscious.

At the next 15 minutes, all the Blunt converts to Lethal — the character is at death's door.

At the next 15 minutes, all the Lethal converts to Aggravated, and the character dies.

Each additional creature — or flame, or anything else that burns oxygen — shortens the time proportionally:

two Size-5 creatures breathe normally for 3 hours, then check every 7 minutes.

---

**Boiling water:**

Touching boiling water deals 3 Lethal fire damage per round —

walking across it counts as touching it.

Full immersion deals 20 Lethal fire damage per round, and a wholly submerged character enters suffocation.

---

**Lava:**

Touching lava or magma deals 6 Lethal fire damage per round —

walking across it counts as touching it.

Full immersion deals 60 Lethal fire damage per round, plus burn points equal to half the final damage — and a wholly submerged character enters suffocation.

---

**Smoke:**

A character breathing thick smoke makes a Fortitude check each round at DC 2, +1 per 30 seconds; each failure deals 1 Blunt damage. This is environmental damage and touches only creatures with respiratory systems. Thick smoke also veils sight: a character within it gains 4 points of cover defense against ranged attacks. An environmental effect.

---

**Dim / dark environments:**

Dim: in poor light (a moonlit night), creatures without blindsense, blindsight, darkvision, or true seeing halve the sensory and rough ranges of their sight.

Dark: with no light at all, creatures without blindsense, blindsight, darkvision, or true seeing cannot see — they enter blindness.

---

**Space:**

Space combines the three environmental effects "vacuum", "radiation", and "weightlessness", and the three stack.

---

**Radiation:**

Characters sometimes take radiation — a reactor leak, or exposure in space.

Radiation's effects are many and scale with intensity, but for simplicity we cite space's own: exposed to space's radiation, a character takes 1 EDR damage per round.

Radiation damage cannot heal naturally — only the Lord God can repair it.

---

**Vacuum:**

Vacuum is air pressure fallen far enough to injure living things.

Vacuum is quite deadly to creatures: each round deals 2 EDR damage and 1 INT damage,

and being in vacuum drops the character into suffocation.

---

**Weightlessness:**

Weightlessness is a character in an environment without gravity.

Weightless: the character loses base land speed, may clamber along at base speed, and takes no falling damage.

Attacking while weightless, the attacker drifts opposite the attack's direction by (attack successes) meters;

the defender, after defense AS are counted, drifts (remaining successes) meters.

---

**Difficult terrain:**

Difficult terrain is ground pocked, rutted, and uneven enough to hamper movement.

Each meter moved on difficult terrain costs 2 meters of movement, and no charging is possible on it.

---

**Bright-light environments:** under intense light a character continuously takes non-stacking dazzle points, saving each round. These dazzle points refresh each round rather than persisting; short of a ruinous consequence, they fade at 1 per round after leaving the light.

The dazzle points scale with luminance, measured in nits (candela per square meter, cd/m²):

Dark environment: under 1 nit

Dim environment: 1–200 nits

Bright environment: 200–10,000 nits

Intense light: above 10,000 nits — 1 dazzle point,

plus 1 more per tenfold rise in luminance.

Ambient luminance doesn't mean everything within shares that band.

Common luminances (nits):

red laser pointer: 20,000,000,000

the Sun's surface: 2,000,000,000

incandescent filament: 10,000,000

white paper in sunlight: 30,000

what the eye can adapt to: 3,000

the full Moon's face: 2,500

where the eye still tells colors well: 1

white paper under a full moon: 0.07

moonless night sky: 0.0001
