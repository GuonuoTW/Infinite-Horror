# The Core Rules

An Infinite Horror TRPG game is normally played by one host (hereafter **ST**, for storyteller) and several players (hereafter **PC**, for player character). The ST is responsible for creating the world and the starting point of the story for the PCs, and for responding to the PCs' actions from the world's point of view. As the world's spokesperson, the ST should hold neither goodwill nor malice toward anything that exists in their world. The PCs are responsible for carrying the story forward, and are expected to bear the consequences of their own actions.

When the rules conflict with the ST's common sense, the ST has the right to change the rules to fit that common sense — such changes are called house rules (hereafter **HR**). Naturally, when faced with an HR they cannot accept, PCs likewise have the right to flip the table.

**Checks, competitions, and contests**

The Infinite Horror tabletop roleplaying game uses ten-sided dice (hereafter **D10**) and success-counting rules. Whenever your character needs to attempt something where success or failure carries different consequences, you should make a **check** for them. This usually involves the following steps:

1. **Determine your DP.** DP means dice pool — it decides how many D10s you roll. A DP is usually one Stat value + one Skill value + (−) other modifiers, but depending on a specific action's description or the ST's judgment, a DP might equal a single Stat, the sum of two Stats, or some special fixed value.

2. **Determine your + Dice level.** If any die in a check shows a 10, you may roll one additional die for that check; if it scores a success, add that success to the total. Furthermore, if that new die also shows a 10, you may roll yet another, and so on. This rule is called "10+ Dice". Some abilities or items grant "9+ Dice", meaning a die showing 9 or 10 lets you roll again. The lowest possible + Dice level is 8. A simple analogy may help: in Ludo (Aeroplane Chess), you use a D6, and the game runs a "6+ Dice" rule — every time you roll a 6, you roll one more die.

3. **Roll the check.** Roll a number of D10s equal to your DP. Each die showing 8, 9, or 10 counts as 1 success. The total number of successful dice is your "successes".

4. **Add your Additional Successes (AS).** High Stats and high Skills grant Additional Successes. A Stat of 6 grants 1 Additional Success, plus one more for every further 5 points (so Stat 11 grants 2 Additional Successes). Skills follow a different scheme — see the "Skills Overview" page. You can also gain Additional Successes through other means. When making a check, if your final effective rolled successes are greater than 0, add these Additional Successes to the final total. (See the "using Additional Successes" rules for details.)

5. **Compare against the target.** The difficulty (hereafter **DC**, for difficulty class) is the target number of a check. When your successes equal or exceed the DC, the action succeeds — and the more your successes exceed the DC, the better the action goes; otherwise the action fails. Some checks don't care about a specific target number at all, only about your own successes, which determine how well the action goes — how much damage an attack deals, how high a desperate jump carries you, and so on. What the DC is depends on whether the check takes the form of a Competing Check or a Resisting Check.

**Competing Check**: a Competing Check is a check whose DC is a fixed, pre-set value — for example, lifting a sack of rice, jumping over a pit, or climbing a wall.

**Resisting Check**: a Resisting Check is a check whose DC is the number of successes rolled by an opponent's check — for example, beating someone in a footrace, or trying to knock someone several meters away.

For example, player A wants to backstab player B, and chooses to sneak up on them. Neither player has any device or ability for detection. Player A rolls AGI + Stealth, and the successes from that roll become the DC of player B's detection check. Only if player B's PCT + Investigation successes exceed that DC does player B notice player A. Otherwise, player B takes a knife in the back without ever knowing.

**The Opportunity Roll (Opp. Roll)**

Occasionally, for one reason or another, the number of D10s you could roll drops to 0 or below. You may still roll a single D10 — but this die only scores a success on a 10, though it gains "+ Dice" rerolls as normal. If this roll shows a 1, you suffer some adverse effect (set by the ST); this is called a critical failure. Such a roll is called an Opportunity Roll. Although an Opp. Roll is nominally a check, it is not actually a check: it is unaffected by any ability that increases a check's DP or its result, unless that ability says otherwise. Also, if the Opp. Roll has already produced a success, a 1 rolled on a bonus die does not count as a critical failure.

**Using Additional Successes**

Bonus successes are successes added on top of an action — they can make your action better, but they cannot turn a failed action into a success. In other words, if the successes you actually rolled are 0 or fewer, the action counts as a failure no matter how many Additional Successes you have.

If a check of yours rolls 1 success but you lose 1 success for some reason, your rolled successes become 0, and you cannot gain any Additional Successes.

The Additional Successes a character has on attacks are called attack Additional Successes (including attack Additional Successes from equipment, Attributes, Skills, enhancements, and so on).

Each attack action a character takes in a round may use their full pool of attack Additional Successes.

If an attack action delivers multiple attacks, then before making the attack, the attacker must declare how the action's total attack Additional Successes are divided among the individual attacks.

Special: when a character attacks multiple targets in one attack action — e.g. one attack against each target in an area — each of those attacks receives the full declared Additional Successes.

Special: when a character's single attack action both hits multiple targets and delivers multiple attacks against particular targets, every target receiving one attack gets the full attack Additional Successes, while targets receiving multiple attacks still require the normal division of attack Additional Successes.

Special: when a character attacks a single target multiple times in one attack action and gains attack Additional Successes during the attack (on hit, from a check, etc.), the character may use those attack Additional Successes immediately — but once this has triggered once, it does not trigger again within the same action.

Special: when using multiple weapons to attack a single target multiple times, or to attack multiple targets, attack Additional Successes are allocated separately per weapon used. (For example, if in one attack action you attack once with an ordinary longsword and once with a greataxe that grants +4 Additional Successes on attacks, the axe's 4 Additional Successes may only be allocated among the axe's own attacks — they cannot be moved to the sword's attacks.)

The Additional Successes a character has on defense are called defense Additional Successes.

To use defense Additional Successes, a character must declare how many they are using before the attacker makes the attack check. If the defender's Additional Successes (including skills that reduce the attacker's successes, but excluding damage absorption, reduction, negation, or similar abilities) cancel out all of the successes the attacker naturally rolled on the dice (before the attacker's Additional Successes are counted), the attack fails (misses) — the attacker's Additional Successes have no effect, and none of the attack's on-hit effects take effect either.

The character then loses the declared number of defense Additional Successes for the rest of the turn. The defense Additional Successes recover at the character's next action.

As a special case, when a character's Base Defense drops to 0 — from charging, multiple-attack penalties, High Speed, and so on — they lose the defense Additional Successes granted by Legendary Agility and Legendary Perception.

**Points of note:**

Defense Additional Successes must be declared, in quantity, before an attack is rolled. It is therefore possible for a defender to declare 5 Additional Successes against an attack that rolls only 1 success. In that case, 4 of the defender's Additional Successes are **unavoidably wasted**.

It may also happen that a defender forgets (or otherwise fails) to declare defense Additional Successes before the attack roll — in that case, they are treated as not using any.

But if the ST prefers a more relaxed game, they may allow defending players to allocate defense Additional Successes freely after seeing the attacker's successes.

**Rounding:**
Unless stated otherwise, whenever a calculation produces a fraction, drop everything after the decimal point unconditionally. This is called rounding down.

**On the meaning of "/" in the rules**

"/" normally means division — the value before it divided by the value after it. For instance "6 / Determination Additional Successes + 1": if your Determination Additional Successes are 1, the result is 3. There is one exception: notation like "1/minute", where what follows is only a unit with no number, means simply "1 per minute".

**Multiple multiplications, divisions, and additions**
Sometimes a rule multiplies a value by a number (or by several times a number, or a rolled value), or adds a number to it. With a single multiplier or addend, just multiply or add normally. When two or more multipliers apply to any abstract value (such as a modifier, a rolled value, or successes), or when multipliers and addends are both present, treat each multiplier as adding (multiplier − 1) times the value, and apply addends last.

Example: a character whose bloodline gives ×2 land speed uses an ability that temporarily makes land speed ×4, while also wearing shoes that add 50 to land speed. Their actual land speed counts as +1× then +3× then +50, i.e. land speed × (1 + 1 + 3) + 50.

Note that the rule above applies to computations on a single value. For a value derived from another value, finish computing the base value first, then compute the derived value — do not mix the two computations together.

Example: a character's base speed is doubled and increased by 10, and then their land speed becomes four times the original. Their actual land speed is (base speed × 2 + 10) × 4.

**Division:**

Some rules grant abilities that halve something — a cost, a rest time, and so on. If you have several, simply apply all multiplication and division first, then addition and subtraction.

Example: your bloodline halves your mana costs, you then use an ability that halves mana costs again, and you also carry a weapon that reduces mana costs by 1. Your actual mana cost is one quarter of the base, minus 1.

These computation rules do not apply to things outside the player's own statistics — for money, distances, and the like, use ordinary arithmetic.
