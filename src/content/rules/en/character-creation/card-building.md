# Card Building

To play the Infinite Horror TRPG, you first need to create a character card of your own that conforms to the game's rules.

An Infinite Horror TRPG character card has six major sections: the "Concept section", "Stats section", "Skills section", "Feats section", "Derived Stats section", and "Abilities section". Each section's data and its meaning are described in detail below.

**Concept section**

The character concept defines the character's image, background, personality, and other roleplay elements. As a reference, here is the concept section of an actual card:

*Name: Roland*

*Gender: male*

*Age: 20*

*Height/weight: 180cm / 56kg*

*Species/nationality: human / British*

*Languages:*

*Appearance/features: brilliant golden long hair*

*Personality: all in all, call him a hopeless do-gooder.*

*Summary: My parents named me after an ancient paladin.*

*For a very long time, I never understood what that name meant.*

*Paladins and all that — they're awfully far removed from lives like ours.*

*So far as to be… a little out of reach.*

*But a quiet life isn't so bad. I don't hate living like this — you could even say I rather like it. Maybe that's just what my life is: plain, but not without its little joys.*

*That day, though, I got a bit impulsive. (laughs)*

*I knew perfectly well that whoever moved that bomb off the bus was going to die.*

*But my body moved before I could stop it.*

*"Ahh, I really am hopeless," I sighed, and ran for the distance with everything I had.*

*"This isn't something an ordinary civilian like me should be doing!" I cried as I ran — but I didn't regret it. If sacrificing one of me could save that many more, then so be it.*

*At least in that final moment, I didn't disgrace my name.*

*Virtue/Sin (character traits): Justice / Wrath*

*Defects: none*

*Talents: none*

*Quirks: none*

*(This card is based on the legendary samsara traveler Roland.)*

Virtue and Sin are concepts originally inherited from WoD. Once per film, when a player's roleplay embodies their Virtue, they restore Willpower to its maximum (Willpower is detailed in the Derived Stats section below). Once per Scene, when a player's roleplay embodies their Sin, they restore 1 point of Willpower (for the definition of a Scene, see the "Scenes and Actions" page). Note that having a Virtue does not oblige you to display it constantly — the occasional flash of it counts just as well.

The seven Virtues and Sins available in WoD are:

Virtues:

Charity: triggered when the character takes risks to help others — restore all Willpower.

Faith: triggered when the character finds meaning out of chaos and sorrow — restore all Willpower.

Fortitude: triggered when the character successfully withstands pressure and temptation — restore all Willpower.

Hope: triggered when the character brings someone else back from despair — restore all Willpower.

Justice: triggered when the character takes risks for the sake of justice — restore all Willpower.

Prudence: triggered when the character rejects the shortcut and chooses the safe path — restore all Willpower.

Temperance: triggered when the character has a chance to indulge (for good or ill) and resists the temptation — restore all Willpower.

Sins:

Envy: triggered when the character takes something good from another, or makes another lose something good — restore 1 Willpower.

Gluttony: triggered when the character indulges their desires at the expense of everything else — restore 1 Willpower.

Greed: triggered when the character takes risks to get what they want — restore 1 Willpower.

Lust: triggered when the character vents their desires on another without regard for their feelings — restore 1 Willpower.

Pride: triggered when the character takes risks to make a show of their own ideas — restore 1 Willpower.

Sloth: triggered when the character dodges a task that needed doing — and the task gets done anyway — restore 1 Willpower.

Wrath: triggered when the character ignores danger to vent their anger — restore 1 Willpower.

Character traits: if both ST and PC feel that Virtue and Sin don't suit the character's roleplay style, character traits may be used in their place.

Under the character-trait rules, each PC sets 2–4 character traits for their character, and the ST assigns each trait a "performance score" based on how hard it is to portray. Once per Scene, when the player successfully portrays a character trait, the character restores Willpower equal to its performance score.

This system is compatible with the Virtue/Sin system — simply treat Virtues and Sins as character traits with a performance score of 1.

Traits that are too easy to portray may be ruled a performance score of 0 by the ST — "emotionless deadpan", for instance — while "tsundere" might be judged worth 1.

Defects, Talents, and Quirks are part of advanced play; the ST is free to decide whether to open them up. See the "Defects, Talents, and Quirks" page.

*Roland sees his goal as becoming a paladin who champions justice. He chooses Justice as his Virtue and Wrath as his Sin — he cannot see an injustice without roaring at it.*

*Roland believes a noble paladin should have no Defects or Quirks, so he takes no Defects, Talents, or Quirks at all.*

**Stats section**

A character's Stats define the most fundamental abilities they are born with. Unlike the original Infinite Horror novel's six attributes ("intelligence, psychic force, cell vitality, nerve reaction speed, muscle tissue strength, immunity"), in the Infinite TRPG rules a character has nine Stats — the three branches (Intensity, Flexibility, Durability) of the three Stat categories (Physical, Mental, Social).

|  | Intensity | Flexibility | Durability |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **PHYSICAL** | STR (Strength) | AGI (Agility) | EDR (Endurance) |
| **MENTAL** | INT (Intelligence) | PCT (Perception) | DTR (Determination) |
| **SOCIAL** | DMR (Demeanor) | CTR (Control) | CMT (Composure) |

Building a fresh card

First, set all of the character's base Stats to 1. Distribute 3/2/1 points freely among the three Stat categories — Physical, Mental, Social — then add those points to individual Stats. After that, add 3 free points to any Stats you wish to raise. Note that raising a Stat that has already reached 4 up to 5 costs 2 points.

Example 1: Passerby A assigns 3 points to Physical, 2 to Mental, and 1 to Social. In Physical he raises STR, AGI, and EDR to 2 each, spending 3 points; in Mental he raises INT and PCT to 2 each, spending 2 points; in Social he raises DMR to 2, spending 1 point.

His Stats now read: STR 2 / AGI 2 / EDR 2 / INT 2 / PCT 2 / DTR 1 / DMR 2 / CTR 1 / CMT 1.

Deciding a lopsided spread looks bad, he puts his remaining 3 points into DTR, CTR, and CMT, raising each to 2 — so in the end, all his Stats are 2.

Example 2: Passerby B assigns 3 points to Mental, 2 to Social, and 1 to Physical. In Physical he raises EDR to 2, spending 1 point; in Mental he raises INT, PCT, and DTR to 2 each, spending 3 points; in Social he raises CMT to 3, spending 2 points.

His Stats now read: STR 1 / AGI 1 / EDR 2 / INT 2 / PCT 2 / DTR 2 / DMR 1 / CTR 1 / CMT 3.

He decides to lean into Initiative and defense (explained in the Derived Stats section below), so he pours all 3 remaining points into AGI, raising it to 4.

His Stats become STR 1 / AGI 4 / EDR 2 / INT 2 / PCT 2 / DTR 2 / DMR 1 / CTR 1 / CMT 3.

The average person's Stats are 2; the ordinary human ceiling is 5.

For detailed descriptions of each Stat, transcendent Stats, and raising/lowering Stats, see the separate "Stats Overview" page.

*As a knight, physical fitness is non-negotiable. Roland assigns 3 Stat points to Physical, 2 to Mental, and 1 to Social. He puts 2 into STR and 1 into EDR; both Mental points go into DTR; and the Social point goes into CMT. His Stats become:*

*STR 3, AGI 1, EDR 2, INT 1, PCT 1, DTR 3, DMR 1, CTR 1, CMT 2*

*He then gives 2 of his free points to EDR and 1 to DTR, completing the Stats section. His Stats are now:*

*STR 3, AGI 1, EDR 4, INT 1, PCT 1, DTR 4, DMR 1, CTR 1, CMT 2*

**Skills section**

A character's Skills define abilities and techniques learned after birth — the capacity to do specific things.

Like Stats, Skills divide into three categories: Physical Skills, Mental Skills, and Social Skills.

Physical Skills: Athletics, Brawl, Drives, Firearms, Larceny, Stealth, Survival, Blade, Archery

Mental Skills: Academics, Computer, Crafts\*, Investigation, Medicine, Occult, Science

Social Skills: Animal Ken, Empathy, Expression\*\*, Intimidation, Socialize, Subterfuge

Beyond the Skills themselves, each Skill subdivides into countless "Professions", representing particular ways of using the Skill.

Professions are generally agreed between ST and PC; the Skill sub-pages list some common ones.

When a player has a Profession under a Skill and uses the Skill in a way that matches it, they gain a 1 DP Profession Bonus. Without a matching Profession, the "no-Profession penalty" applies — see the "Skills" chapter. Since the gain is a DP bonus, a Profession Bonus cannot benefit uses that involve no check.

\*Crafts is not a single skill but a family of skills. Crafts can have unlimited sub-skills — "Crafts–Cooking" and "Crafts–Smithing" are plainly two completely different skills. Its sub-skills, and each sub-skill's Professions, are agreed between ST and PC.

\*\*Likewise, Expression is not a single skill but a family. "Expression–Oratory" and "Expression–Performance" are obviously two different skills; its sub-skills and their Professions are agreed between ST and PC.

Building a fresh card

First set all the character's base Skills to 0. Distribute 6/5/4 points freely among the three Skill categories — Physical, Mental, Social — then add each category's points to its Skills. After that, add 5 free points to whichever Skills you need. Normally, the Skill-level cap on a starting card is 3. In addition, the character receives three free Professions, which may be placed under one or more Skills of at least level 1.

As with Stats, the 6/5/4 may go to Physical/Mental/Social, or Mental/Social/Physical, or Physical/Social/Mental — entirely as the player wishes.

For Skill advancement, gaining Additional Successes, XP-discount rewards, and how Professions are used, see the "Skills Overview" page and each Skill's detail page.

*What kind of knight can't handle sword and spear? Roland gives 6 Skill points to Physical Skills, 5 to Social, and 4 to Mental.*

*He raises Brawl and Blade to 3 each. He raises Socialize to 3 and Empathy to 1, picks the Poetry sub-skill under Expression and raises it to 1. He raises Investigation to 3 and Occult to 1.*

*He still has 5 free Skill points. He raises Athletics to 3 and Survival to 2.*

*Finally, he chooses his 3 Professions: "Swimming" and "Light Throwing Weapons" under Athletics, and the "Greatsword" Profession under Blade.*

*Roland's completed Skills section:*

*Athletics 3 (Swimming, Light Throwing Weapons), Brawl 3, Drives –, Firearms –, Larceny –, Stealth –, Survival 2, Blade 3 (Greatsword), Archery –*

*Academics –, Computer –, Crafts –, Investigation 3, Medicine –, Occult 1, Science –*

*Animal Ken –, Empathy 1, Expression–Poetry 1, Intimidation –, Socialize 3, Subterfuge –*

**Feats section**

Feats define special abilities that benefit the character in certain actions.

Building a fresh card

Assign 5 points to Feats; each point buys 1 Feat level. If a Feat has multiple levels, each level costs 1 point — except that raising a Feat from level 3 to level 4 costs 2 points.

For instance, if a Feat has levels 1–4, you can spend 5 points for all 4 levels, 3 points for level 3, or of course 1 point for just level 1.

Many advanced Feats may cost more points to acquire: if a Feat starts at level 2 when bought, it costs 2 Feat points.

For each point of INT the character has, they gain 2 language Feat points, spendable only on language Feats.

Some Feats carry purchase restrictions at the start.

See the "Feats Overview" page for the Feats themselves.

*Roland believes a knight should stand in harm's way — shielding his teammates and hammering his enemies. He decides the "Diehard" Feat perfectly captures a knight's toughness. Diehard requires EDR 3 and DTR 3; Roland has 4 in both, so he raises Diehard to level 3.*

*With his 2 remaining Feat points, Roland buys "Special Identity" at level 2. Its level-2 effect grants either 2 extra free Skill points or 4 extra Professions; Roland takes the Skill points and puts them into Medicine and Intimidation.*

*Special Identity level 1 also lets him bring some common items into films. After talking it over with his ST (mostly to confirm what the ST counts as "common"), Roland decides to carry a light ceremonial sword in.*

*Finally, since Roland's INT is 1, he gains 2 language Feat points, raising English to level 2.*

*At this point the basic card-building process is done. All that remains is for Roland to compute each derived stat per the Derived Stats section below — then he's ready to play.*

*Here is Roland's finished card (concept section as above), with:*

*Feats:*

*Diehard (3):*

*The character rules their mind with their body, shrugging off pain and exhaustion.*

*The character gains a bonus equal to this Feat's level on Fortitude Saves.*

*The character's HP +2.*

*The character ignores fatigue points up to this Feat's level. The character does not fall unconscious from their health track filling with Blunt damage; and when their health track fills with Lethal damage, they stay conscious as long as the portion filled with Aggravated damage has not reached half their EDR.*

*Special Identity (2):*

*Level 2: Roland is a youth who dreams of knighthood.*

*When building the card, gain 2 extra free Skill points. May carry a light ceremonial sword into films.*

You may notice the Feat text here doesn't exactly match the full entries under "Feats" — for example, Diehard's level 4 and 5 portions aren't written in. That's because Roland only took Diehard 3, so he can't enjoy what levels 4 and 5 grant. If he later raises Diehard, he'll need to copy in the missing portion.

The same method applies to Bloodlines, Alternations, Eye Techniques, items, and so on. Don't leave bare keywords on the card — copy in every part of the rulebook text that actually applies.

Writing cards this way makes it easy for ST and PC to look up what the character can do, and makes for a handsomer card besides.

Copying the applicable rulebook text onto your card is a basic competency for every PC.

*Stats section:*

*STR 3, AGI 1, EDR 4, INT 1, PCT 1, DTR 4, DMR 1, CTR 1, CMT 2*

*Skills section: as above (Medicine 1 and Intimidation 1 added via Special Identity)*

*Items:*

*Estoc — Category: Tech. Weapon base properties: weapon damage 3L, Breach 1.*

**Derived Stats section**

Derived stats are generally determined by the character's Stats, Feats, and so on. The values below are all starting values, and may change with the enhancements a player exchanges for.

Size: an ordinary adult is Size 5. With the right Feat or Defect you can raise it to 6 or lower it to 4. Size's full definition is on the "Miscellaneous Rules" page.

Whenever you change Size by any means, various aspects of the character are affected — this is called Size adjustment. The detailed rules are in "Miscellaneous Rules".

Some resources change your Size without following Size adjustment, or read "this Size change carries no Stat changes" — in that case the changed Size affects only HP and speed.

Speed:

Speed comes in three kinds:

Base speed: how far the character moves with one Movement Action; the starting value is **STR + AGI + Size**, in meters.

Land speed: the character's speed moving on the ground — initially equal to base speed, but some abilities may change it.

Flight speed: the character's speed moving through the air. A character needs a special ability to fly, and the granting ability states the flight speed.

Initiative: decides the order of action in battle; the starting value is **AGI + CMT**.

Base Defense: decides the character's defense value; the starting value is **the lower of AGI and PCT**. Base Defense stacks with Base Defense granted by other abilities.

HP: equal to **EDR + Size**.

Sensory range: the range within which the player perceives things distinctly — close enough to make out an opposing character's face, hear their conversation clearly, and so on. The starting value is **PCT × 10 meters**. In general, most creatures need sight to pinpoint; without a special ability, a creature that pinpoints by sight can only get a rough fix by hearing. Only creatures with "keen scent" can get a rough fix by smell.

Rough sensory range: within this range, the character only notices that a target is there, without making out its appearance. The starting value is **sensory range × 10 meters**. Generally, without special abilities, the rough range applies to sight only.

Because targets differ in Size (the bigger, the easier to spot), sensory range is dynamically adjusted per observed target, as follows:

For every point of the target's Size above 5, treat the character's PCT toward that target as 1 higher; for every point below 5, treat it as 1 lower.

Sensory range as used here doesn't care about the mode of perception (it covers sight, hearing, even intuition). Being outside sensory range doesn't mean the character can't perceive the target at all — in rules terms it means you cannot target specific body parts, nor make out ornaments, writing, and the like on the target. A target beyond the rough range, however, cannot be perceived at all, and thus cannot be established as a combat target.

Some abilities grant perception beyond the sensory and rough ranges; the ability will say so.

Likewise, some abilities extend a single sense's range — a telescope extends sight — and these enlarge only that sense's range, not sensory range itself.

In writing, sight (for instance) may be written simply as "vision" or as "sensory range with sight as its sense".

In such cases, only the matching sense uses the larger range; other senses use the normal range. And abilities defined in terms of sensory or rough range are unaffected.

Will: the parameter symbolizing the firmness of the character's resolve; its base value is **DTR + CMT**.

Willpower: a character's Willpower represents their capacity to reverse a bad situation or bend reality through sheer determination. Willpower is a pool of points spent for benefits; its starting maximum equals **Will**, and a fresh card starts with full Willpower.

Willpower's basic use: when the character takes an action (inside or outside their turn) that is not an Extended Action and needs only one check (including but not limited to attacks, Skill checks, Resisting Checks, defense), they may spend 1 Willpower — no action required — to gain a 3 DP Perfect Bonus on that action, once per action. Alternatively, when attacked, the character may spend 1 Willpower to gain a 3-point Perfect Bonus on defense against that attack.

For more uses and recovery of Willpower, see the "HP, Willpower, and Energy Pools" page.

Will checks: some rules call for a Will check or Will Save — this is that value, commonly used to resist mental influence or Mental damage. Note that "Will check" and "Will Save" are not the same thing. A Will Save's starting DP equals **DTR**.
Because Will is composed of DTR and CMT, CMT also contributes bonuses to Will checks and Saves — but effects that apply to CMT checks do not carry over to Will checks and Saves. See "HP, Willpower, and Energy Pools" for details.

Reflex: some rules call for a Reflex check — commonly used to avoid area attacks. Its starting DP equals **AGI + Athletics (Self-Preservation)**.

Fortitude: some rules call for a Fortitude check — commonly used to resist physiological effects. Its starting DP equals **EDR + Survival (Self-Preservation)**.

The three above are collectively known as the three Saves.

**Abilities section**

Covers items, special abilities, and the like.

When building a fresh card, negotiate with the ST which items you bring into the film — generally you cannot carry things your background could not plausibly obtain.

**Ways to enhance a character card**

The Infinite Horror TRPG has three main currencies:

Tiers: Tiers come in 5 grades, from low to high D, C, B, A, S. In most cases an ability priced at C is almost certainly stronger than one priced D, and a B ability stronger again than a C. Owning a full suite of S-Tier abilities and chaining them into one glorious combo is every samsara traveler's dream. Tiers split and combine freely: 3 D make a C, 3 C make a B, and so on — and the reverse works too, so a C can be broken up to buy 3 different D-Tier resources.

Reward points: also called points, the commonest currency in the Infinite Horror setting. Resources generally cost some Tier **and** some reward points: a high-energy particle pulse rifle priced D+500 means paying one D Tier and 500 reward points for that expensive future-tech weapon.

XP: experience. XP represents strength gained through tempering in the films. Obviously, one person's XP cannot be transferred to another. Nor can XP conjure an ability from nothing — it must build on an existing foundation through "study" or "training". See the "Experience System" and "Self-Taught Abilities" pages.

Ordinarily, a samsara traveler earns at least D+1000 as a completion reward for each film. The figure may vary by ST.

In the Resources section you'll find abilities and items of every description, each with its own price, purchasable and learnable with the matching Tiers, reward points, and XP. Raising a character's Stats and Skills also costs reward points or XP.
