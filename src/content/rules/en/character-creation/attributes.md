# Stats Overview

Stats overview:

Stats represent the abilities a character inherently possesses. These abilities are innate, can be trained later in life, or can be reinforced with the various resources of the Lord God Space. Every normal person has at least 1 in every Stat.

The great majority of a character's checks are based on their Stats, so Stats amount to the character's active capabilities and the range of what they might do — and sometimes also their ability to keep themselves from being disturbed or harmed. If a character has some supernatural Category or trait, Stats may operate in supernatural ways as well.

A Stat's value shows how capable the character is in that respect. Broadly, a Stat of 2 is the modern human average, and 5 is about the limit a normal human can reach. From 6 upward, the character has gained power beyond mortals, on the level of the heroes of legend; past 15, they step into the realm of demigods; and past 25, in mortal eyes they are as gods.

The nine Stats fall into the 3 categories of Physical, Mental, and Social:

| Physical Stats | STR | AGI | EDR |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Mental Stats | INT | PCT | DTR |
| Social Stats | DMR | CTR | CMT |

**Stat concepts:**

**STR (Strength):**

The Stat for pushing and pulling objects, lifting weights, and the like — covering explosive power and carrying capacity, and therefore also affecting how fast you run or swing an object, and how much damage your blows deal.

Anything that can exert physical force on an object has a STR value.

Since most items have no defined weight statistic, the rules do not recommend limiting what players can carry by encumbrance; if needed, see the carrying-capacity section under Items and Equipment Slots.

**AGI (Agility):**

Whole-body coordination, precision and nimbleness of movement, reflexes — the ability to move the body fast and effectively.

Anything that can act and move has an AGI value.

**EDR (Endurance):**

Bodily health and resistance, metabolic vigor — the ability to withstand injury and illness.

Anything alive has an EDR value.

**INT (Intelligence):**

Logical thinking, computation, analysis, and memory; sometimes also the character's allocation and control of supernatural energies.

Anything that can understand language and perform analysis and reasoning has an INT value.

**PCT (Perception):**

Noticing one's surroundings and detecting change — insight, intuition, quick discernment of detail. This Stat represents attentiveness and the ability to sort information, so being near-sighted doesn't necessarily mean low PCT.

Anything that can perceive the outside world has a PCT value.

**DTR (Determination):**

The character's decisiveness and the firmness of their decisions — also how stubborn they are. In the supernatural sense, the strength of the character's spirit.

Anything that can act with purpose has a DTR value.

**DMR (Demeanor):**

The character's bearing, style, and presence — the ability to draw others' interest without trying. In the supernatural sense, it also represents how far the character transcends the Natural category. Though related to looks, a high value doesn't mean handsome or beautiful, and a low one doesn't mean ugly.

Anything that can distinguish itself from its surroundings has a DMR value.

**CTR (Control):**

The ability to influence others — the persuasive force of word and deed, the capacity to incite people and bend them to your intent.

Anything that can express its own will has a CTR value. Any individual with CTR 2 or higher should possess some definite, active, deliberate means of expressing intent, such as language.

**CMT (Composure):**

The ability to stay unruffled — quickness of mental reaction, keeping cool under pressure or in emergencies. In the supernatural sense, the vitality of the character's mind.

Anything with the will to respond to outside stimuli has a CMT value.

**Stats at 0**

A lowered Stat means the character has been damaged in some respect — bodily, mental, or spiritual. If a Stat drops to 0, the character loses the ability to act autonomously — in play, the player loses control of the character.

A character at STR 0 cannot move: unable to bear their own weight or move their limbs, they collapse.

A character at AGI 0 cannot act, joints locked. Positioned just right they may stand or crouch, rigid and helpless; otherwise they fall.

A character at EDR 0 simply dies.

A character at INT 0 loses autonomous action, running on raw instinct — remembering nothing and able to use no skills (beyond moving and fleeing danger).

A character at PCT 0 loses touch with reality, sunk in a dream they cannot wake from.

A character at DTR 0 loses the capacity to act, sitting or wandering aimlessly, unable to use any skill and unresponsive to reality (dangers included).

A character at DMR 0 loses consciousness.

A character at CTR 0 goes utterly insane, incapable of any comprehensible or meaningful behavior.

A character at CMT 0 loses agency and self-control entirely — completely deranged, taking no actions and unresponsive to reality.

**Null Stats**

Some beings innately lack certain Stats; these are called null Stats. A being with a null Stat cannot make the related checks, is unaffected by effects that lower that Stat, and is outright immune to Saves made against that Stat.

Null-Stat immunity always counts as maximum Tier — permanent immunity — and holds even against unavoidable or always-effective abilities.

Beneficial effects keyed to the null Stat are likewise void: a target with null STR cannot gain STR-related buffs, and a creature without Willpower cannot gain Temporary Willpower, and so on.

Some examples of null Stats:

Ordinary objects have no Stats at all. Mechanical devices have STR; their AGI depends on the operator's AGI or skill. Pre-programmed and self-operating computers and similar devices have INT.

Machines have no EDR, so machines cannot be poisoned and are outright immune to Fortitude Saves.

Most zombies in Resident Evil have no INT — no capacity for language or logic — so they are immune to Mental assaults.

Ordinary plants have only EDR. They also have Hardness, with HP determined by EDR. Certain supernatural plants may have other Stats.

Spirits and incorporeal beings that cannot affect the physical world have no STR.

**Legendary Stats**

In worlds where supernatural power lurks beneath the surface, a Stat of 5 is usually the limit for ordinary folk; for the mighty of the hidden world, 5 is only a beginning. And in worlds where dragons soar and demons battle angels across the land, even common soldiers may exceed 5, while the apex powers reach unthinkable heights — to mortal eyes, hardly different from gods.

Each time your power breaks through its limit, you gain something transcendent: a Legendary Stat.

When a character's Stat (permanent plus temporary bonuses included) reaches 6, they automatically gain 1 Legendary point in it; every further 5 points (11, 16, 21, 26, 31, and so on) grants another.

Each Legendary point adds Additional Successes to checks with the related Stat — it does not add to your check DP. The AS granted are listed below. Note: unless stated otherwise, each level of a listed progression replaces the previous one rather than stacking.

Each Legendary point also brings extra effects.

**Remember: Legendary Stat n = (Stat − 1) / 5, rounded down.**

**Legendary STR**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on STR-related checks; gain an extra 500 × 2^(n−1) kg of carrying capacity; base throwing distance ×(n+1); gain an n DP bonus on STR-related checks; when attacking with STR, your damage cap rises by n; Fortitude Saves gain an n-point bonus.

Example — 1 point: 1 AS on STR-related checks, +500 kg carrying capacity, throwing distance ×2, 1 DP Perfect Bonus on STR-related checks, damage cap +1 on STR attacks, +1 Intrinsic Bonus on Fortitude Saves. 2 points: 2 AS, +1000 kg, ×3 throwing, 2 DP, damage cap +2, +2. 3 points: 3 AS, +2000 kg, ×4, 3 DP, +3, +3. 4 points: 4 AS, +4000 kg, ×5, 4 DP, +4, +4. 5 points: 5 AS, +8000 kg, ×6, 5 DP, +5, +5. And so on.

**Legendary AGI**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on AGI-related checks; gain n defense AS; Evade Defense +n; base speed +3n meters (applied before any base-speed doubling).

Example — 1 point: 1 AS on AGI-related checks, 1 defense AS, Evade Defense +1, base speed +3 m (before doubling). 2 points: 2 / 2 / +2 / +6 m. 3 points: 3 / 3 / +3 / +9 m. 4 points: 4 / 4 / +4 / +12 m. 5 points: 5 / 5 / +5 / +15 m. And so on.

**Legendary EDR**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on EDR-related checks; HP increases by an extra n(n+1)/2 total; the character can go 2^(n−1) weeks without food, drink, or sleep; breath-holding time ×2^n.

Example — 1 point: 1 AS, +1 HP, 1 week without food/drink/sleep, ×2 breath. 2 points: 2 AS, +2 more HP (total +3), 2 weeks, ×4. 3 points: 3 AS, +3 more (total +6), 4 weeks, ×8. 4 points: 4 AS, +4 more (total +10), 8 weeks, ×16. 5 points: 5 AS, +5 more (total +15), 16 weeks, ×32. And so on.

**Legendary INT**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on INT-related checks.

On gaining your nth Legendary INT, you immediately gain n mind points. By committing 1/3/9/27/81 mind points you may designate any one D/C/B/A/S skill within a skill tree you have opened. You may reallocate mind points freely, but designation and reallocation happen only in the Lord God Space. Skills designated via mind points cannot gain purchasable extras such as research upgrades, and remain subject to their usage limits.

When judging the Tier of the original skill being swapped out, only its original Tier counts — spell uptiering and upgrade options do not make it count as higher.

As a Standard Action you may perform a mind reshuffle: swap any skill you own for a designated skill of the same Tier in the same skill tree. A skill swapped out via reshuffle is unusable until restored. Skills with limited uses cannot be swapped or reset until their uses recover; if a swapped or restored skill has an ongoing effect, that effect ends immediately. All swapped skills restore after one Long Rest, or you may restore them with a 10-minute meditation.

Example — 1 point: 1 AS on INT-related checks; 1 mind point, enough to designate one D-Tier skill in a D-Tier skill tree you own — so inside a film, one Standard Action swaps your owned D skill for the designated one. 2 points: 2 AS; 2 mind points (3 committed designate three D skills to swap among). 3 points: 3 AS; 3 mind points — commit 6 to designate six D skills in one tree, or two C skills in a C-Tier Bloodline tree, and so on.

You must fix an order for all designated skills when entering a film. If a temporary Stat change costs you a level of Legendary INT, you immediately lose its mind points; if those points were allocated, you lose designations in reverse order, and any currently swapped-in skill is lost immediately (your original skill stays unusable until restored). On regaining that Legendary level, the mind points return, unallocated.

**Legendary PCT**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on PCT-related checks; gain n defense AS; gain a stackable 2n Insight Defense that stacks with Insight bonuses from other sources; base sensory range +20n meters.

Example — 1 point: 1 AS, 1 defense AS, +2 stackable Insight Defense, +20 m sensory range. 2 points: 2 / 2 / +4 / +40 m. 3 points: 3 / 3 / +6 / +60 m. 4 points: 4 / 4 / +8 / +80 m. 5 points: 5 / 5 / +10 / +100 m. And so on.

**Legendary DTR**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on DTR-related checks; Will +n; Will checks gain an n-point bonus.

Example — 1 point: 1 AS, Will +1, +1 Perfect Bonus on Will checks. 2 points: 2 / +2 / +2 … up to 5 points: 5 / +5 / +5. And so on.

**Legendary DMR**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on DMR-related checks; on Gene Lock unlocking checks and any Opportunity Roll, add +n to the result (this does not grant + Dice on the Opp. Roll); and you may voluntarily open your Gene Lock n extra times per film (each rank opened counts as one time).

Example — 1 point: 1 AS; +1 on Gene Lock and Opp. Roll results (no + Dice); 1 extra voluntary Gene Lock opening per film. 2 points: 2 / +2 / 2 times … 5 points: 5 / +5 / 5 times. And so on.

**Legendary CTR**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on CTR-related checks; Reflex Saves gain a 2n-point Intrinsic Bonus; the character gains n rerolls per Scene, usable after seeing an unsatisfying roll, keeping the better result. (Note this rerolls the dice, not the check — it works even on abilities that are not checks, but cannot work on things that cannot be rerolled.)

Example — 1 point: 1 AS; +2 Perfect Bonus on Reflex Saves; 1 reroll per Scene, usable after the roll, take the better result, at most once per single roll. 2 points: 2 / +4 / 2 rerolls … 5 points: 5 / +10 / 5 rerolls. And so on.

**Legendary CMT**

General effect at n points: gain n AS on CMT-related checks; Will +n; Initiative +n.

Example — 1 point: 1 AS, Will +1, Initiative +1 … 5 points: 5 AS, Will +5, Initiative +5. And so on.

**Derived stats**

Generally, some Stats cannot by themselves fully express the capability they stand for; derived stats assist the base Stats in expressing it.

**Raising and lowering Stats**

**Permanently raising a Stat**

Broadly, there are three ways to permanently raise a Stat:

1. Exchange for an ability that grants a permanent Stat bonus.

2. Buy Stats with reward points: raising a Stat by 1 costs (current value × 200) points — so raising STR from 1 to 5 costs 200+400+600+800 = 2000 points. Stats raised this way are called base Stats.

3. Buy Stats with XP: raising a Stat by 1 costs (current value × 2) XP — so STR 1 to 5 costs 2+4+6+8 = 20 XP. These are likewise base Stats.

4. Buying a base Stat from 0 to 1 costs the same as from 1 to 2.

Note: when buying Stats with points or XP, purchased abilities and Stats Damage are not counted — only the base Stat value sets the price.

Example 1: with base STR 4 and a +2 STR Intrinsic Bonus, buying the next point costs 800, not 1200. After buying, you have base STR 5 plus the +2 Intrinsic Bonus — 7 STR total.

Example 2: with base CMT 4 and a −2 CMT Intrinsic Penalty, buying still costs 800, not 400. After buying, you have base CMT 5 minus 2 — 3 CMT total.

**Temporarily raising a Stat**

There are many ways to raise a Stat temporarily.

Unless stated otherwise, temporarily raised Stats work like the base Stat in use and calculation, but cannot serve as prerequisites for exchanging abilities (items excepted).

Similarly, if a temporary Stat qualifies you for some ability and you later lose the Stat, the ability immediately stops working; regain the Stat and it works again — except that limited-use abilities still count their prior uses.

Example 1: a character with STR 5 wears a belt of +2 Reinforcement Bonus STR, reaching 7 and gaining Legendary STR. Take the belt off, and that Legendary point stops working until STR is bought back up to 6.

Example 2: a character with CTR 7 uses the Legendary CTR reroll; afterward their CTR falls to 5, losing the Legendary point. When the Stat returns to 7, they regain the Legendary point — but the reroll has been spent and cannot be used again.

**Judging temporary vs. permanent Stats**

Permanent Stats: you buy something and it simply gives you the Stat.
Example 1: template Stats from a Bloodline or Alternation — INT +2, PCT +2.
Example 2: Perseverance: your STR gains a further +3 Practicing Bonus.

Temporary Stats: you buy something but must satisfy a condition to receive the Stat — that makes it temporary, even if the duration is "n/a".
Example 1: Inhuman Charm: for each point of Legendary CTR, your DMR gains a +1 Intrinsic Bonus; for each point of Legendary DMR, your CTR gains a +1 Intrinsic Bonus, stacking with this Bloodline's own Intrinsic Bonus.
Example 2: Power of the Source: while you stand on solid ground, each point of Legendary STR grants +1 Intrinsic Bonus STR.

When Stats change, compute all permanent Stats first, then temporary bonuses. If computation order would change an ability's result, rule the worst outcome; if there is no worst outcome, pick one.

Also, derived Stats cannot derive further: Stats gained from a trait like "Inhuman Charm" above cannot beget new Stats through similar abilities.

**Lowering a Stat**

There are many ways to lower a Stat.

Stat-lowering effects can reduce a character's Stat to 0 at most. If a lowered Stat means you no longer meet an owned ability's prerequisites, you keep the ability but it temporarily stops working — Legendary Stats included.

Broadly, Stats drop in only four ways:

1. Exchanging for an ability that permanently lowers a Stat.

Such an exchange requires the lowered Stat to remain above 1; if it would drop to 0, the exchange cannot proceed.

2. Gaining a condition that lowers a Stat for a duration.

These generally come from one of your own abilities or another character's; the ability in question spells out the details.

3. Stats Damage.

Stats Damage is a milder, temporary effect; unless stated otherwise all Stats Damage is temporary. Each point makes the Stat count as 1 lower for the duration, immediately affecting everything tied to it. Unless stated otherwise, each 24 hours spent in Long-Rest-style inactivity (sleep and the like) recovers 1 point. If caused by special abilities (such as negative points), recovery can begin only after the matching points are removed.

4. Stats Drain (Stat absorption).

Stats Drain is permanent damage to a Stat, applied to the base Stat, and can go negative; if the permanent Stat hits 0 but temporary bonuses keep the total positive, the character avoids the effects of a 0 Stat.

When drained, the Stat permanently drops by the drained amount, immediately affecting everything tied to it, and cannot be recovered by rest or repair — treat it as though never owned.

If buying or using an ability would push your base Stat below 0, you cannot buy or use it.

When later raising the Stat with XP or points (or similar), pay costs as from the reduced current base value.

If the lowered Stat costs you prerequisites for owned abilities, you keep them but they temporarily stop working — Stat Powers included. Note that those abilities' negative effects keep applying regardless.
