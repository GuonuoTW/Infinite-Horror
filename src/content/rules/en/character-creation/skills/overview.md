# Skills Overview

**Skills overview**

Player A can slip quietly into the room, press an ear to the door, and eavesdrop on the gangsters next door. If player B tries the same thing, his lack of finesse will likely produce a racket and draw the gang's attention — but should a fight break out, he can simply draw his pistol and put the opposition down. All of these actions relate to the Skills a character has.

Specifically, the skills needed in that example are Stealth (Sneaking), Investigation (Listening), and Firearms (Pistol).

Skills represent the varied practical capabilities you have mastered through your own learning.

These abilities are all acquired through training, so the minimum is 0. Compared with Stats, Skills are more specialized and narrower in use — but they are what make you a true expert. In many cases a Stat alone cannot carry a check; the matching Skill is required.

Skill levels 1–5 represent the mortal domain — 5 is an expert among mortals. Levels beyond 5 draw more on supernatural abilities or unfathomable power, and are not separately described here.

**Skill categories**

Like Stats, Skills fall into the three Physical, Mental, and Social categories.

Physical Skills: Athletics, Brawl, Drives, Firearms, Larceny, Stealth, Survival, Blade, Archery

Mental Skills: Academics, Computer, Crafts (has sub-skills), Investigation, Medicine, Occult, Science

Social Skills: Animal Ken, Empathy, Expression (has sub-skills), Intimidation, Socialize, Subterfuge

Owing to version differences, a few terms need clarifying:

Where rules and resources say "social-category skills" (社交系), that means Social Skills (互动系).

Where they say the "Medical" (医疗) skill, that is the Medicine (医学) skill.

Where they say the "Beast-taming" (驯兽) skill, that is the Animal Ken (动物沟通) skill.

Where they say the "Menace" (威吓) skill, that is the Intimidation (胁迫) skill.

If you spot usages like these, consider taking a commemorative screenshot — they will soon be unified into fixed wording.

**Skills at level 0:**

Physical Skills: physical skills are not purely technique — many can be attempted on raw fitness alone, without knowledge. At level 0 you may still attempt the check, but you lose one success.

Mental Skills: mental skills are pure knowledge and technique. At level 0 you cannot attempt the check at all — it automatically fails.

Social Skills: social skills tend to blend technique and knowledge, similar to physical ones. At level 0 you may still attempt the check but lose two successes — and for some skills, or some uses of some skills, the ST may rule the attempt impossible at 0 and an automatic failure, e.g. Expression–Piano.

**Gaining Skill levels**

When creating a character, players will find the skills they can learn — and their levels — limited. Characters aren't as multi-talented as real people. But the skills on your card don't truly represent everything the character can do.

A character may be familiar with things like cooking, hopping fences, riding a bike, or driving a car — yet doing them doesn't mean the character actually has levels in Crafts–Cooking, Athletics (Cycling), Drives (Car), or climbing. Skill levels represent professional-grade capability:

think world cooking championships, competitive rock climbing, the Tour de France, or drifting.

So how do we handle actions without skill levels?

First, establish that not every action calls for a skill check. Ordinary daily life and work are easy — easy enough to need no check at all: picking up a cup and drinking from it requires nothing.

In mundane life, ordinary actions have negligible DCs; anything slightly tricky is at most DC 1, never mind higher. In mundane days you aren't fighting for your life, soaring through the sky, or racing a strict clock — difficulty drops so far that you can succeed without skill levels.

Nevertheless, only skills bought with XP are what a character can truly rely on to protect themselves and save others in a crisis.

Skill level represents mastery. Every skill has a level from 0 to 15. When making a skill check, add the skill level to the check — each level means one more die. The higher the level, the better the result; levels reflect proficiency and training.

Generally, a character with skill 10 is more practiced and professional in applying that skill than one with skill 9.

Accordingly, at certain levels we reward the character with Additional Successes:

**Skill levels 5, 10, 11, 13, and 15 each grant 1 Additional Success on that skill's checks — up to 5 in total. In certain special cases your skill level may exceed 15, but unless stated otherwise, no further skill-level AS are gained beyond 15.**

Skill levels are raised by spending the "XP" earned in play.

**Skills cap at level 15. Raising a skill via exchange with the Lord God costs (current level − 1) × 2 XP, minimum 1. As a special case, raising a skill from 0 to 1 costs 3 XP.**

XP discounts: as skill levels rise, players pick up abilities and tricks without a teacher. In game terms: **when a skill reaches level 6, 9, 12, and 15, choose one Feat whose prerequisites include this skill, or one self-created skill based on it, and reduce that purchase's XP cost by 12 / 28 / 44 / 66 respectively, to a minimum cost of 0.**

The discount cannot be split across several abilities (different levels of the same Feat or Title count as different abilities), but discounts may be stacked onto a single ability, and you need not learn the chosen ability the moment you pick it. So if ability A costs 70 XP, a character can, by skill level 12, accumulate 84 XP of discounts toward A and get it free.

Only skills gained through XP or other permanent advancement enjoy XP discounts — copies, temporary boosts, and the like do not.

An XP discount works only once per character. In inheritance cases, count the pre-inheritance state: a used discount stays used after inheritance. In merging cases, count the pre-merge state — only permanent merges can use XP discounts, judged from the inherited skill levels; if the original character used theirs, it counts as used. Other cases are the ST's call by analogy.

Some abilities raise skill levels automatically. If you lose such an ability (e.g. a respec), tally the effective XP you invested in the skill and re-purchase from scratch; surplus XP must stay invested in this skill until it fills out a normal level-up. (E.g.: skill level 2 auto-raised to 3, then the player spends 4 XP to reach 4. On respec the skill returns to 2; the 4 invested XP raises it to 3 with 2 XP banked — once 2 more XP are added, it rises to 4.)

**Professions**

Using skills involves Professions.
A skill is itself a huge category — one skill can span careers and specializations in different fields — so we subdivide.
Generally, **a Profession under a skill costs 1 XP. Once gained, checks matching the Profession gain the Profession Bonus, normally 1 DP.**
Note that **if you lack the matching Profession for a skill check, your skill level counts as halved, rounded down — but you do not count as lacking the skill.**
You may be a track athlete, but the long jump still finds you unprofessional.
And **when a skill reaches 3 and 5, you immediately get one free Profession under it — however it was raised. You may also defer the choice and pick it when needed.**
In the Resources section, Professions are written in parentheses after the skill: Blade (Axe), Medicine (First Aid), Subterfuge (Disguise), and so on.

Some skills have special sub-categories, written "Skill–Sub-skill".
For these, the skill's Professions become sub-skills: buying the skill means choosing one matching Profession under it and leveling the sub-skill that Profession represents, which grants the Profession Bonus as usual.
For example "Crafts–Tech Armorsmithing" has its own level — you might hold both "Crafts–Tech Armorsmithing 3" and "Crafts–Tech Weaponsmithing 5", each applying to its own situations, and each leveled with its own XP.

These sub-skills also earn the XP-discount rewards above, but only the first sub-item of each skill to reach a given level gets it: if "Crafts–Tech Armorsmithing" is the first Crafts sub-item to hit level 6, it takes the discount; when "Crafts–Tech Weaponsmithing" later hits 6, no second discount.
Sub-items still count as Professions: a "Crafts–Tech Weaponsmithing" check gains the Profession Bonus for holding that (in effect) Profession.
However, these skills no longer receive the free-Profession reward when a sub-item first reaches 3 and 5.

When an action matches several Professions at once, Profession Bonuses do not stack.

**Using skills**

Using a skill requires a skill check. The higher the result, the better the character performs. The result must meet or beat some number to succeed (usually a difficulty class — DC — or an opponent's result). The harder the task, the higher the bar.

Circumstances can affect skill use. In general, free of outside interference, a character can focus and avoid simple mistakes. With ample time, they can try again and again for the best result. With help, they may finish work impossible alone.

Broadly, most checks for any action roll **key Stat + key skill level (matching Profession)** —

the character's gift (Stat) + training (skill level) + specialization in the field (Profession) + luck (the dice).

Other abilities and situations can, of course, affect the check.

To make a skill check, the player rolls the dice from the sum above, factors in whatever else affects the check to reach the final dice count, rolls, and compares successes against the DC.

Besides DCs there are also contests: the character's result is compared against another character's (or several characters') — a Resisting Check, resolved by its rules.

Which key skill and key Stat a given action uses, we detail below.

Anything not detailed largely follows the ST's common sense; several Stat + skill pairings may fit, in which case the ST picks the most suitable. The rules fix a number of common check formats.

**Retries**

In general, when a skill check fails, the character may retry without limit — but the consequences of failure must be considered. Some tasks allow only one attempt; after failing, the skill simply won't work. For most skills, once a check has succeeded, further successes don't change the result.

Example: player A attempts a climb check and fails right at the start — he drops, but from so low that nothing happens, and he can try again.

When he fails at 10 meters up, he takes falling damage as the price of failure — but he can still get up and try again.

**Time and skill checks**

Skill uses take different amounts of time — some none, some 1 round, some several rounds or longer. Most skill uses classify as Standard Actions, Movement Actions, or Whole-round Actions. These action types define the time cost within battle rounds (1 round = 6 seconds) and how the action interacts with movement (see "Scenes and Actions").

A skill check that is an instant reaction, or folded into another action, is not a separate action. And some skill checks include movement — jumping, climbing, swimming.

When a character undertakes something very long, we use periodic checks.

A periodic check applies when a task takes a long time and progresses over time —

forging armor or a weapon, operating on a patient, writing a stretch of code.

A periodic check consists of a check period and a total DC.

The period is the unit of time per check. Each check's successes are recorded and accumulated; when the total meets or exceeds the total DC, the task succeeds. Meanwhile, if the number of 1s rolled over the course of the task ever exceeds the total DC, the long-term check automatically fails — all accumulated successes are lost, any invested resources with them, possibly with dire consequences.

Example: player A operates on another player — a DC 10, per-hour INT + Medicine (Surgery) periodic check. His four checks score 3, 2, 2, and 5 successes: past 10 in total, so the surgery succeeds after 4 hours.

Had player A's accumulated 1s exceeded 10 along the way, the surgery fails, the invested time is lost, and the patient's wounds may worsen (ST's call).

Where the Resources section says "extended check", that means a periodic check.

**Impossible tasks**

Characters occasionally attempt the practically impossible. DCs may then be 40, 60, or higher.

"Impossible" resists advance definition — mostly senseless, pure-luck feats: swimming up a waterfall, digging a 10-meter pit barehanded, stepping off a 30-story roof unhurt. The ST decides what is utterly impossible and what is merely nearly so.

But our characters are transcendent beings who accumulate countless superpowers over play — so characters with special abilities or towering skill levels can still pull off the astonishing.

**Several people using skills together**

When several people use skills toward the same goal, the effects may stack —

distinguish whether the effect targets a shared objective or each individual.

For example: characters can build something together — everyone applies their skill to one goal, and effects stack.

For example: characters must swim across a stretch of sea — no shared target here; each swims for themselves.

Even then, one can still help another:

a character may forgo their action for the turn and make a check for the matching action; the target then gains an Environmental Bonus equal to the successes when performing that action.

Example: player A is making swim checks. Player B decides to help, forgoing his action on his turn to roll STR + Athletics (Swimming), scoring 3 successes. When player A's turn comes, her STR + Athletics (Swimming) check gains a 3 DP Environmental Bonus.

Note that with this kind of help, if the helped character does not perform the matching action next turn, the bonus is lost.

**Stat checks**

If the task has no appropriate skill check to call on, make a Stat check —

that is, the check changes from **key Stat + key skill level (matching Profession)** to **key Stat** alone.

For example: arm-wrestling, tug-of-war, and the like are pure STR contests with no skill.
