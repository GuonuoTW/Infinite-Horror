# Scenes and Actions

The Infinite Horror game rules use three classification standards for time.

**Real standards:**

Real standards mean seconds, minutes, hours, days, weeks, months, years, and so on.

Wherever the rules mention an ability with a real-standard concept of time, it points to the real standard.

Example: if an effect lasts "one day", that usually means 24 Earth hours. Activate the effect in a world where 1 day = 48 hours, and it lasts only "half a day" there.

Special case: if a resource reads "the effect lasts until the next sunrise", follow the sunrise and sunset times of the world in question. And PCs may reasonably conclude that in a land of eternal night, the effect lasts forever.

**Abstract standards:**

Abstract standards mean Turns, Rounds, Scenes (Encounters), films — time concepts with no real-world existence.

Turn: describes the battle itself. When every combat unit in a battle has acted once in action order, one Turn has passed. A Turn is reckoned as 6 seconds of the real world.

Round: describes a character's action-time within battle. Unlike Turns, each unit's Round runs on its own clock: a Round is the span from one unit's action starting to the next time their action starts. A lasting effect's duration is timed by the character who produced it: the clock starts when the character finishes producing the effect and it takes hold, and when that character's next action begins, the effect has "lasted one Round".

Scene (Encounter): the Scene is a time-like concept controlled by the ST, describing the story's divisions. A complete stretch of plot consists of several Scenes and interludes; typically each interlude counts as a Scene change, and a battle is itself one Scene.

For example, a stretch of plot begins with the players in conference at the duke's residence and ends with an audience before the king: the duke's residence is one Scene. Barring incident, everything up to the duke presenting the players to the king is interlude. The royal audience is another Scene — and if battle breaks out with the king's guards after the audience, that battle is yet another Scene, not lumped with the audience itself.

When a stretch of plot concludes, the plot pivots, the venue changes, and so on, the ST may declare a Scene change — commonly applied at:

1) Battle starting is a Scene change, and battle ending is a Scene change. The battle must be one the ST recognizes as genuine: teammates declaring a "battle" and Scene-cycling to refresh skills is against the rules.

2) The conclusion of a plot beat may count as a Scene change — after the Sith Emperor throws Master Windu from the tower, or after the Red Queen's mainframe is shut down in *Resident Evil*: though place and cast are unchanged, the previous beat has closed, so a Scene change may be ruled.

3) A change of area, where the change can steer the plot's next step — entering the sanctum in *Raiders* can count as a Scene change. In no case may anyone shuttle in and out of an area to cycle Scenes and refresh skills; that is against the rules.

4) A change of time, where the change can steer the plot's next step — the countdown expiring in *The Grave Robbers' Chronicles*.

Note: final say on Scene changes belongs to the ST. Nobody may exploit one pivot to flip Scenes repeatedly, whether to maliciously scramble the plot or to farm skill refreshes.

Film: the division particular to the Infinite Horror setting — from entering a film to leaving it counts as one film. The same applies to returning to a film world.

**Battle standards:**

Battle standards are time concepts used only in combat, recording only the action-time of battle actions, never durations.

Each Turn, when a combat unit's action comes up, it can take the following five kinds of "battle action".

Only a unit receiving a full Round gains the complete set below. If a player somehow gains one isolated action, they gain only that action — no others — though conversion works as usual.

Free Actions: unlimited in number when your action comes up, but apart from speech, most Free Actions cannot be taken outside your own action. And for any one kind of act, only one Free Action may be used. The ST may also cap the number of Free Actions in a Turn.

Example 1: player A holds two weapons and drops both with one Free Action. He may spend a Movement Action to draw a new weapon — but he cannot spend another Free Action to drop it.

Example 2: player A casts at B with a Free Action, then casts at C with another Free Action — not allowed, even for a different spell, because it's the same kind of action: casting. The same holds for melee attacks, ranged attacks, and so on.

Swift Actions: one per Round when your action comes up.

Movement Actions: one per Round. A Movement Action may convert into a Swift Action, but not the reverse.

Standard Actions: one per Round. A Standard Action may convert into a Movement Action, but not the reverse.

Whole-round Actions: a Whole-round Action combines one Movement Action and one Standard Action — using one this Turn costs you both. Abilities produced with a Whole-round Action take effect immediately upon completion.

Full-turn Actions: a Full-turn Action combines one Swift, one Movement, and one Standard Action — using one this Turn costs all three. Abilities produced with a Full-turn Action take effect immediately upon completion.

If, after using a Whole-round or Full-turn Action, a character somehow still retains or gains a spare Standard or Movement Action, it may be used as normal.

There is one more special case, called Anytime, or "no action".

Anytime actions can be taken at any moment, including during the opponent's Round; each character has unlimited Anytime actions per Turn, and they always come from some ability.

Beyond the actions above, some special actions differ from the standard battle actions:

Reflex Actions: only particular resources or abilities allow Reflex Actions — otherwise players cannot take them, dropping prone excepted. A Reflex Action can generally be taken at any moment; unless stated otherwise, the unit taking one loses all of next Round's Swift Actions. Normally, one Reflex Action per Round, unless a special ability grants more. A Reflex Action can interrupt another's ongoing action; when the Reflex Action ends, if the interrupted action can still proceed, it must — otherwise the interrupted action automatically fails.

The forfeited Swift Action is not the Reflex Action's price: a character with several Reflex Actions loses all of next Round's Swift Actions after the first one used, but that doesn't stop them using the rest.

(If an attacker's target departs via a Reflex Action, the attack must still proceed, since the action can still be made — but if the target's Reflex Action puts the attacker into a state where they cannot act, killing them for instance, the attack fails.) When using a Reflex Action to leave an attack's range, your Evade Defense (Base Defense included) doubles against that attack; if the attack is an area attack that permits evasion, the successes on the check to offset the area damage or effect double.

Prepare Actions: a Prepare Action lets you hold an action for later; the delay must fall between the end of your Round and the start of your next. On your Round, spend a Standard Action to declare a Prepare Action ("when they attack me, I fall back at once"). You may prepare a Standard, Movement, Swift, or even Free Action. To do so, specify what you will do and under what condition. Then, any time before your next action, you may answer the trigger with the prepared action, which resolves just before the triggering act. If the trigger is part of someone else's action, you interrupt them; if they can still continue afterward, they do. In effect you gain a Reflex Action keyed to their particular act. The declared action type (Free, Swift, Movement, Standard) is skipped in your next Round — as though borrowed in advance.

Attached to / combined into an action: certain resources or abilities attach effects to an action the character performs. Such an ability costs no action itself, but only one such ability may be attached to any single action.

E.g.: ability A attaches to a Blade attack; ability B attaches to an all-out attack. Making an all-out attack with a Blade attack, the character picks just one of A and B to attach.

But if a Technique involves several actions, each qualifying action can carry its own attachment.

E.g.: a Technique moves, then attacks; ability A attaches to a move, ability B to an attack. Using the Technique, A and B may each ride their matching action.

E.g.: a Technique makes three Blade attacks, the last an all-out attack; ability A attaches to a Blade attack, B to an all-out attack. The first and second attacks may each carry A; the third may carry either A or B.

Focusing: some actions, once produced, demand continued attention to maintain or prolong them — this is "Focusing". The focus each ability costs may differ: a bearer of the "Illusion-Sight Pupil" needs a Standard Action per Turn to focus on watching for oncoming dangers, while a psychic must spend all their actions each Turn to read another's thoughts.

Focusing generally begins with an activating action; once activated, the effect lasts 1 Round by default, and extending it requires maintaining focus. Whether or not the character maintains it next Turn, once started, the character counts as being in focus.

Whether to maintain focus is decided at the start of the character's Round; choosing to maintain forfeits the matching action outright.
If the focusing action is unstated, it defaults to the activating action; if activation was a Reflex Action, focusing is a Swift Action.

A focusing creature may otherwise act normally with whatever actions it has.

In any case, one person can maintain only one focused effect at a time — even when maintaining costs no action. This means a character cannot start or use two focus-maintained acts in the same Round.

**Action conversion:**

As above, Standard Actions convert into Movement Actions, and Movement Actions into Swift Actions. A simple example:

Player A meets a mighty BOSS. Seeing its short arms, A decides to open distance and whittle it with ranged attacks. A first spends a Movement Action to pull away; with speed 10, he moves at most 10 meters. Deciding that isn't enough, he converts his unused Standard Action into a Movement Action and falls back another 10 meters. In sum, A used 1 Movement and 1 Standard Action for two moves. He still has an unused Swift Action, but decides to end his Round there.

**Action rules:**

In the Infinite Horror game, anything a character does can be called an "action". Taking an action means the player character did something, or reacted to something done by another.

Most actions contain one or more checks.

Sometimes what a character does takes very few steps, or they get only one chance; other times they must work slowly and carefully, step by step. This divides actions into two kinds:

**Single Actions:**

Single Actions are those resolved by one check. That a behavior is a Single Action means it completes in one or a few steps, and usually cannot — or need not — be repeated and reworked toward perfection.

Single Actions typically include, but aren't limited to: leaping a pit, shooting a basket, making an attack, scouting, opening a door.

The time a Single Action takes is not fixed — some need seconds, others minutes or longer.

Single Action difficulty: when you take an action, roll one check and count your successes (how many dice show 8, 9, or 10; with at least 1, add the AS from Stats, skills, or other sources). Your successes must equal or exceed a particular number for the action to succeed — the difficulty (DC). The further your successes exceed the DC, the more perfect the outcome (usually noted in the behavior's description). Many acts have DC 1 — one success completes them; others run higher. Generally, an act with no stated DC has DC 1.

Specially, some acts set no DC and read the outcome straight off your successes — attacking an enemy, say, where each success deals 1 damage.

**Extended Actions:**

Extended Actions need several checks, accumulating successes to finish. That a behavior is an Extended Action means it proceeds through repeated steps, refined layer by layer toward completion.

Extended Actions typically include, but aren't limited to: making something, finishing a work, completing a ritual, performing, repairing something, extricating yourself from a situation.

Extended Action difficulty:

An Extended Action's DC states how much work the task requires. Make repeated checks, recording each one's successes; when the running total meets or exceeds the DC, the work is done.

Unlike Single Actions, the work's quality is folded into the DC. "A novel people can follow" might be DC 5; "a gripping novel" DC 7; "a novel that gets a standalone printing and earns royalties" perhaps DC 15. You cannot lower your standards midway to change the DC — there's no turning back on the hard road.

Extended Action periods:

The time one check of an Extended Action takes is its period. Writing a novel might check once per day — period: days. The rules give examples; the ST sets each behavior's period by reference to them.

For some behaviors, a character may rest or go adventuring between periods, shelving the work; others must be finished in one sitting. Depending on how long the work sits, accumulated successes may erode — leaving an oil painting overnight, say, dries the paint too far to blend new color into the base. The ST's judgment governs.

Extended Action deadlines:

Some Extended Actions must finish within a time limit or fail. A vampire making a ward of blood, for instance, must complete the rite before blood fresh from the body loses its vitality. In such cases the character must accumulate enough successes before the deadline.

Optional rule: mishaps in Extended Actions

The rules above assume an Extended Action moves the work steadily toward the good. But effort doesn't always improve things — sometimes it leaves a flaw, and the flaw can't be papered over: a careless heavy stroke of black in a watercolor; a part snapped while assembling a model. This optional rule covers that.

Being optional, the ST need not enable it — and if they do, they should say so to their players.

While a player performs an Extended Action, track the 1s in every check. If the count of 1s meets or exceeds the DC before the successes do, the effort miscarries: time and materials are wasted, and worse may follow — the stir-fry was the plan, but the pan catches fire. To finish the task, the character must start over from the beginning. Note: if the act ultimately succeeds, the 1s do not mar the outcome's quality — mid-course flaws were mended or erased.

Before true failure or success, the character knows where they stand — the pan on fire, a circuit burnt through while soldering: such flaws are perceptible. If the effort is at risk of failing, the game host should tell the player.

**Repeated Single Actions:**

Some acts must be repeated, yet in urgent moments we treat them not as one Extended Action but as a chain of Single Actions — because every step tilts the situation.

For instance, Yang Kang, separated from the protagonist in *Shaun of the Dead*, wants to climb to a rooftop to spot them (an Athletics check). But zombies crowd around him, trying to drag him down — so how high he climbs each Turn decides both the zombies' threat to him and his usefulness to the protagonist. Treat him as making an Athletics check each Turn with a Movement Action.

In no particular hurry, the same repeated act can become an Extended Action to streamline play — after the story ends, Yang Kang wants to drink and admire the moon on that rooftop, and his climb can resolve as one Extended Action.
