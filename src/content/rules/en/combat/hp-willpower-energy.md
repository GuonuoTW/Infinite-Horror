# HP, Willpower, and Energy Pools

**HP**

A character's Health Points (HP) represent their tenacity of life and current state of health. Each point of HP has one of four wound states: "Healthy", "Blunt damage (B)", "Lethal damage (L)", and "Aggravated damage (A)".

**Fainting from grave wounds:**

When a character no longer has any "Healthy" HP and the worst state among their HP is not "A", the character faints until 1 point of HP recovers to "Healthy".

This unconsciousness normally lasts until the character recovers 1 Healthy HP — or recovering 1 point of Willpower in this state also wakes them.

They may of course also be woken by some particular outside stimulus — a splash of cold water, say; the ST rules on such cases.

**Deterioration:**

When a character no longer has any "Healthy" HP and the worst state among their HP is "A", then along with fainting from grave wounds, their condition keeps deteriorating: at the end of each of the character's rounds, 1 non-"A" point of HP converts to "A" ("L" converting first). This continues until all the character's HP is "A", or until 1 point recovers to "Healthy".

Additionally, if during this time the character receives any healing ability that recovers HP, or a successful "first aid", deterioration stops.

If a character takes damage again after deterioration has stopped, re-judge whether they faint from grave wounds, and re-judge whether deterioration begins.

**Life states:**

Some enhancements or abilities let a player directly read another creature's life state. Unless such an ability says otherwise, the feedback is one of the following, not exact HP numbers and states:

Unharmed: the creature is uninjured.

Minor injuries: converting all the creature's wounds into Blunt damage (1 Lethal = 2 Blunt, 1 Aggravated = 3 Blunt), the total does not exceed its HP.

Moderate injuries: the converted total exceeds its HP but not twice its HP.

Severe injuries: the converted total exceeds twice its HP but not three times.

Near death: all the creature's HP is Aggravated damage, but for some reason it is not yet dead.

Dead: the creature is dead.

Optional rule: while a character carries Lethal damage, all their capabilities decline. The character takes a wound penalty of ((3 − Healthy HP) × 1) DP on full checks, and a ((3 − Healthy HP) × 1)-point wound penalty on defense. Penalties from this rule bottom out at 0. This penalty type exists only through this rule.

**Taking damage:**

Damage likewise comes in three grades by severity: Blunt (B), Lethal (L), and Aggravated (A).

When you take DMG(B): for each point, if Healthy HP remains, convert 1 Healthy to B first; with no Healthy left, convert 1 B to L; with no B left, convert 1 L to A.

When you take DMG(L): for each point, if Healthy HP remains, convert 1 Healthy to L first; with no Healthy left, convert 1 B to L; with no B left, convert 1 L to A.

When you take DMG(A): for each point, if Healthy HP remains, convert 1 Healthy to A first; with no Healthy left, convert 1 B to A; with no B left, convert 1 L to A.

When several grades of damage land at once, resolve from the lowest grade up.

Note: for objects (vehicles included), B/L/A need not be distinguished — when damage fills the object's HP, the object is wrecked.

Example:

Character A has 20 HP; the first attack deals 3 Blunt damage.

— His HP reads 17 Healthy + 3 B.

He then steps on a trap and takes 4 Lethal damage.

— His HP reads 13 Healthy + 3 B + 4 L.

Then a BOSS's ultimate blasts him for 5 Aggravated damage.

— His HP reads 8 Healthy + 3 B + 4 L + 5 A.

Next he unleashes an ultimate of his own at the cost of 10 Blunt and 10 Lethal damage to himself.

Process the lighter 10 Blunt first: with only 8 Healthy remaining, only 8 of the DMG(B) can convert Healthy to B.

— After processing: 0 Healthy + 11 B + 4 L + 5 A.

He must still take 2 DMG(B) and 10 DMG(L).

Processing the 2 DMG(B) converts 2 B to L.

— Now: 0 Healthy + 9 B + 6 L + 5 A.

He must still take 10 DMG(L). His B is only 9, so 9 of the DMG(L) convert B to L.

— Now: 0 Healthy + 0 B + 15 L + 5 A.

He must still take 1 DMG(L), converting 1 L to A.

— Finally: 0 Healthy + 0 B + 14 L + 6 A. All HP is filled with damage and the worst state is A, so he faints, and his wounds keep deteriorating every round.

**Death:**

Absent special abilities, when all of a character's HP becomes A, the character dies.

Some abilities kill without filling all HP with A — "instant death", "maximum HP reduced to 0", and the like.

Some abilities trigger automatically on the character's death.

If a character holds several on-death effects, the player picks one to take effect at death; every other on-death ability fails to trigger. (E.g. Kratos's death-vengeance, the Killing Stone, the New-Horror transformation.)

**Natural recovery:**

Each Short Rest of at least 15 minutes (no other actions) recovers 1 B-state HP to Healthy.

Every two days (including at least two Long Rests of 8+ hours) recovers 1 L-state HP to Healthy.

Every seven days (including at least seven Long Rests of 8+ hours) recovers 1 A-state HP to Healthy.

These recoveries do not coexist: rest 7 days and you may recover 1 Aggravated to Healthy — but not 1 Aggravated and 3 Lethal both.

You may, conversely, split an 8-hour Long Rest apart — into 32 Short Rests of 15 minutes, say — gaining the Short Rest benefits and forfeiting the Long Rest's.

Sleeping 24 hours straight for any reason counts, for HP recovery, as three 8-hour Long Rests.

Some drugs and supernatural abilities speed recovery or restore HP outright.

Beyond HP, rest also grants the following:

Each Short Rest of at least 15 minutes (no other actions): choose one type of Negative Status points you carry and gain one chance to save against it.

Each Long Rest of at least 8 hours (no actions — sleep and the like): recover 1 Willpower.

Each continuous Long Rest of at least 24 hours: recover 1 point of Stats Damage.

These stack with the HP-recovery effects.

**Accelerated natural recovery:**

Some abilities grant faster natural recovery. With double natural recovery speed, all the times above — required durations, number of rests, and single-rest length — halve (round up, in minutes).

**Healing through Medicine:**

See the "Medicine" skill rules.

**Recovering wounds through the Lord God:**

A character may pay a price in the Lord God Space to have the Lord God repair their wounds directly.

B- and L-state HP is restored free of charge.

A-state HP costs 200 points per point restored.

**Rapid Healing and Regeneration:**

In the resources, Rapid Healing and Regeneration help characters recover quickly.

With 1 point of Rapid Healing, recover 1 point of DMG(B) or DMG(L) at the start of each round.

With 4 points of Rapid Healing, recover a cumulative 4 points of Blunt or Lethal damage — or 1 point of DMG(A) — at the start of each round.

Rapid Healing does not stack, but multiple instances take effect simultaneously.

E.g.: a player gains Rapid Healing 3 and Rapid Healing 2 through different abilities — they recover a cumulative 5 Blunt or Lethal per round, but cannot recover Aggravated, since no single instance reached 4.

With 1 point of Regeneration, recover 1 point of DMG(B) or DMG(L) at the start of each round.

With 4 points of Regeneration, recover a cumulative 4 Blunt or Lethal — or 1 DMG(A) — at the start of each round.

Regeneration does not stack, nor with Rapid Healing, but they take effect simultaneously.

Beyond that, Regeneration regrows lost limbs. If a player loses a limb, judge its Size, multiply by 100, and divide by the Regeneration points: the result is the hours needed to regrow it.

Note: certain very small parts (eyes, single teeth…) count as at least Size 1 for this.

Note: with a limb-reattachment ability, if the lost limb exists and permits reattachment, read the hours above as minutes.

**Temporary HP:**

Temporary HP works like a shield: when damage lands — whatever the type (energy, physical, Field…) and whatever the grade (B, L, A) — subtract the damage from it directly, highest grade first. Temporary HP applies last, after damage reduction, negation, absorption, and the like. And if an attack carries rider effects, damage that fails to exceed the Temporary HP has no effect on the character at all; riders computed from damage dealt lose the portion soaked by Temporary HP.

Temporary HP does not work against "unavoidable XX damage" or "convert X of your (the target's) HP to XX" and the like.

Nor against effects that convert your own HP — "damage on the target upgrades one grade", say.

Temporary HP generally does not stack; unless stated otherwise, a given skill's Temporary HP effect works once per Scene.

Unless stated otherwise, Temporary HP lasts one Scene.

**Temporary maximum-HP changes:**

This covers temporarily gaining HP — from temporary EDR, say, whose EDR grants extra HP.

Such HP arrives entirely as Healthy HP.

When you lose these temporary HP, remove current HP states in the order Healthy → B → L → A. If after removal your HP is filled with Aggravated damage, then absent special abilities you die all the same.

E.g.: gaining 1 temporary EDR raises max HP by 1 and grants 1 Healthy HP.

Losing 1 EDR lowers max HP by 1 and costs 1 Healthy HP; with no Healthy remaining, lose in Blunt–Lethal–Aggravated order, and if the loss leaves your HP filled with Aggravated, without special abilities you are dead.

If any effect reduced your max HP during a temporary max-HP increase, you take the reduction as normal once the temporary-max ability lapses, if the reduction still holds.

E.g.: your HP is 8; you temporarily gain 3 max, reaching 11. During the duration you lose 1 max, reaching 10. When the duration ends, your HP is 7.

**Secondary HP:**

Certain special abilities let a player, when all their HP has turned to Aggravated damage, use Willpower, another Energy Pool, or some other value as a second HP bar and keep living.

When such an ability takes effect, a new HP bar is built from the maximum Willpower, maximum Energy Pool, or that value as of the moment of activation — the "Secondary HP".

Unless stated otherwise, Secondary HP still distinguishes Blunt, Lethal, and Aggravated damage; and spending Willpower or energy, fluctuations in the base value, or temporary changes to the Willpower or Energy Pool maximum do NOT affect the Secondary HP bar.

All healing abilities — potions, others' healing spells, your own Rapid Healing, and more — heal the Secondary HP first; only when the Secondary HP is entirely Healthy does the overflow heal the primary HP.

**Willpower**

Willpower's starting maximum equals your Will. Willpower is not an Energy Pool and does not count as one: Energy Pool enhancements and Energy Pool-targeting abilities in the Resources section, unless stated otherwise, have no effect on Willpower.

**Willpower's basic use:**

When a character takes an action (in or out of turn) that is not an Extended Action and needs only one check (including but not limited to attacks, skill checks, Resisting Checks, defense), they may pay 1 Willpower — no action required — for a 3 DP Perfect Bonus on that action, once per action. Or, when attacked, a character may pay 1 Willpower for a 3-point Perfect Bonus on defense against that attack.

Some abilities increase the bonus granted by Willpower's basic use.

**Other uses of Willpower:**

When a character faints from grave wounds, they may pay 1 Willpower to stay conscious for 1 round (in battle) or 1 hour (out of battle). This costs no action; when the time ends they choose whether to pay again or faint.

Example: player A has 5 HP and takes a 5L hit.

His HP becomes 5L — grave wounds, so he faints. He immediately pays 1 Willpower to stay up.

On his turn he acts normally, but from the next round on, he must pay 1 Willpower at the start of each round to take it.

When a character loses consciousness from hunger, thirst, or the like, they may pay 1 Willpower to stay conscious for 3 hours — once per Scene, no action. If you do this, when the time is up you faint immediately, even if the cause has been removed —

that is, even if you dine and drink your fill within those 3 hours, you will still "pass out from hunger" when the timer ends.

Also, if a character kept conscious by Willpower then triggers fainting again — through worsening wounds or otherwise — they faint all the same unless they pay Willpower again.

Example: player A has 5 HP and takes a 6L hit.

His HP becomes 4L 1A — he faints from grave wounds and immediately pays 1 Willpower to stay up.

On his turn, deterioration turns his HP to 3L 2A; he must pay 1 more Willpower to begin his turn.

After that, if he takes another attack and survives, he must pay 1 more Willpower once it resolves — or decline and faint.

Unless stated otherwise, characters cannot spend Willpower on extended checks.

Note: a character can use Willpower only while self-aware. Asleep, unconscious, or in similar states, Willpower cannot be used.

**Temporary Willpower**

Temporary Willpower is gained ignoring the Willpower maximum.

After gaining Temporary Willpower, the character's next Willpower expenditure must spend the temporary points first.

When an ability strips the character of Willpower, Temporary Willpower is lost first.

Temporary Willpower generally does not stack; unless stated otherwise, a skill's Temporary Willpower effect works once per Scene.

As long as a character holds Temporary Willpower, their Willpower does not count as 0.

Gaining Temporary Willpower is not recovering Willpower: it cannot trigger certain effects or abilities, nor wake a character fainted from grave wounds.

Unless stated otherwise, Temporary Willpower lasts one Scene.

Optional rule: if a character's remaining Willpower reaches 0 for any reason, they immediately lose consciousness and collapse on the spot, waking only after recovering at least 1 Willpower.

**Temporary changes to Will and to the Willpower maximum:**

Special cases can change a character's Will; since Willpower derives from Will, the Willpower pool changes with it.

Some abilities also affect the Willpower maximum directly.

If you temporarily gain Will — through 1 point of Stats, say — both Will and the Willpower maximum rise; but this grants no usable Willpower unless you have a means of recovering it.

E.g.: temporarily gain 1 DTR, and Will rises 1 and the Willpower maximum rises 1 — but this is 1 point of empty ceiling, not 1 usable Willpower.

If you suffer a Will decrease — losing 1 point of Stats, say — Will drops and the Willpower maximum with it; and if your current Willpower now exceeds the maximum, you lose the excess. If the maximum later recovers, the lost Willpower is not returned.

E.g.: lose 1 DTR, and Will drops 1 with the Willpower maximum; if current Willpower exceeds the new maximum, the excess is lost. When DTR recovers, Will and the maximum recover — but Willpower lost to the cap is not refunded.

Note: abilities that affect only Will checks or Will Saves do not affect Will, and so do not affect the Willpower maximum.

**Will checks**

Some rules call for Will checks or Will Saves. A Will check is made actively; a Will Save is passive, like Reflex and Fortitude Saves.

A Will check's key Stats are **DTR and CMT**; its base DP is **DTR + CMT + the Intrinsic Bonus from Legendary DTR**, gaining the AS on DTR and CMT.

A Will Save's key Stats are **DTR and CMT**; its base DP is **DTR + CMT**, gaining the AS on DTR and CMT.

**Energy Pools**

**Energy Pool broad categories**

Energy Pools in the Resources section divide into General Energy Pools and Special Energy Pools.

General Energy Pools usually further divide into Eastern and Western Energy Pools. Some General pools belong to both at once, called Energy Pools of both East and West.

Special Energy Pools are neither Eastern nor Western.

Every Energy Pool carries a note on its broad category. If one doesn't, report it to the rulebook publisher.

**Key Stats of an Energy Pool**

Energy Pools generally have their own key Stats, which set the pool's maximum.

Key Stats are usually 2 Stats; their sum is the pool's base maximum.

E.g.: a pool keyed to EDR + DMR, with EDR 3 and DMR 4, has a base maximum of 7.

All checks "of that pool's key Stats" are checks whose DP is the key Stats' sum.

**Energy Pool subcategories**

By key Stats, Energy Pools subdivide into Physical, Mental, and Social Energy Pools.

A pool whose two key Stats sit in 2 different Stat categories carries both natures: when meeting some ability's prerequisites you count as qualifying, and when facing some resisted effect, the opponent counts as qualifying too.

E.g.: your Inner Force pool is keyed to EDR + PCT, so for rulings it is both a Physical and a Mental Energy Pool.

E.g. 2: learning Infinite Punch, Inner Force qualifies as a Physical Energy Pool. But when your opponent is a great dragon that ignores Mental Energy Pool effects not grounded in Physical Stats, Inner Force — being both Mental and Physical — still works on the dragon as normal.

**Obtaining Energy Pools**

Three routes, generally:

Direct purchase: some pools can be bought outright; their pages carry prices.

Bundled with a resource: some pools carry no price and come only through other resources — the Mana pool cannot be bought directly, only gained through certain Bloodlines, Titles, or Alternations.

Advancement: some pools — True Origin Force, say — cannot be gained by anything purchase-like, only by advancing other pools into them through special means, detailed on each pool's page.

**Using Energy Pools**

Each pool's page states how its energy is used. Beyond that, certain Titles, Alternations, Bloodlines, etc., may add uses for particular pools after purchase.

Some abilities let the player choose a pool; unless stated otherwise, only General Energy Pools may be chosen.

E.g.: Infinite Punch asks for a Physical Energy Pool, but the Ripple cannot be chosen — the Ripple is a Special pool.

E.g. 2: Nail Punch costs 1 point of energy, so Calories cannot pay it — Calories is a Special pool. When Nail Punch can burn Calories, kindly press the rulebook publisher to delete this example.

**Recovering Energy Pools**

Each pool's page states its recovery means. Beyond that, certain Titles, Alternations, Bloodlines, etc., may add recovery methods for particular pools.

**Substitute spending between pools**

Some pools may substitute for one another in spending.

In that case, when the target has immunity matching a pool, judge by the new pool.

E.g.: against an effect immune to Mental energy, substituting Source Energy for Inner Force — the substituted effect is not immunized.

E.g.: against an effect immune to Mental energy, substituting Noble Qi for Demon Energy — the substituted Noble Qi effect is immunized.

**Energy Pool compatibility**

Some pools cannot coexist. When a player gains conflicting Energy Pools, they burst apart and die — no save, unavoidably.

Generally, absent a note, most pools are mutually compatible.

Compatibility modes fall roughly into:

Non-exclusive: the pool rejects no others, though others may reject it.

Compatible only with XX pools: the pool tolerates only pools of the XX nature or class and rejects all others. E.g.: only Eastern pools; only Western pools; only Special pools; only Physical pools.

Incompatible with XX pools: the pool rejects all pools of the XX nature or class and tolerates all others. E.g.: not Eastern; not Western; not Special; not Mental.

Rejects XX gauges: the pool rejects gauges of the XX nature.

A pool may carry one or several of these modes.

Clarification: plainly, under "only Eastern pools", a pool of both East and West is rejected, since it can be classed Western; and under "incompatible with Eastern pools", a pool of both East and West is rejected too, since it can be classed Eastern.

**Being unable to gain a pool again**

"Cannot again gain pools of a given nature" is an effect anchored to a point in time: from that moment on, the player is incompatible with pools of that nature.

E.g.: after gaining True Origin Force, a player cannot again gain Eastern General Energy Pools — meaning from the moment the first point of True Origin Force is condensed, gaining any Eastern General pool other than those participating in the condensation kills them.

E.g. 1: player Jia condenses his A-Tier Inner Force into True Origin Force, Inner Force being the only participating pool. Halfway through, Jia acquires Inner Force again by another route, hoping to stack the pool's cap. He bursts apart and dies.

E.g. 2: player Yi condenses his A-Tier Inner Force into True Origin Force, Inner Force alone participating. Halfway through, Yi buys Buddhist Force, hoping to fold it into the condensation. He bursts apart and dies.

**Attacking Energy Pools**

Any ability that inflicts negative effects on another character's Energy Pool use is defined as attacking the pool —

sealing the pool, lowering its cap, draining its energy, and so on.

**Reduced-cost spending**

Some resources reduce part of a pool's costs.

If a cost is lowered, the effect is unchanged — compute the original effect as normal.

If an ability spends all your energy, no cost-reduction ability triggers.

E.g.: an ability lasts (energy spent × 1) rounds; you spend 5 energy and hold an ability reducing energy costs by 2 — the ability lasts 7 rounds.

**Opening the same pool twice**

When a character opens an Energy Pool from any source while already owning the same pool, they receive a measure of improvement in compensation.

The sources must be entirely different — a Bloodline, a Title, a skill. The first duplicate grants +5 to the cap; the second +3; the third and fourth +1; further duplicates of the same pool grant nothing.

For example, player Ouyang takes the Gem-Person Bloodline and gains the Mana pool. He then takes the Magician Title, gaining Mana again — his Mana cap rises 5. Later he buys a *Necronomicon* and gains Mana a third time; having already taken one bonus, his cap now rises only 3.

**Expanding Energy Pools**

Beyond duplicate opening, every General Energy Pool's cap can be raised through "Energy Pool Reinforcement" among the Arts, or the samsara-team Feat of the same name.

**Temporary pool-cap changes**

If you temporarily gain pool cap — via a Stat point, say — only the cap rises, not the energy, unless you can recover energy.

E.g.: temporarily gain 1 INT and the Mana cap rises 1 — but it is an empty point of cap, granting no spendable mana.

If you suffer cap loss — losing a Stat point, say — the matching cap drops 1; if your current energy exceeds the cap, the excess is lost, and a later cap recovery does not refund it.

E.g.: lose 1 INT and the Mana cap drops 1; if current mana exceeds the cap, the excess is lost. When INT recovers, the cap recovers — but mana lost to the cap is not returned.

**Variant Energy Pools**

Some resources provide variants of Energy Pools.

Unless stated otherwise, a variant pool is treated as its archetype for all rulings.

Unless stated otherwise, the variant may substitute for the archetype in spending, but not the reverse. For example, Heavenly Demon Force, a variant of Inner Force, may pay in place of Inner Force to drive generic Inner Force martial arts — but Inner Force cannot pay in place of Heavenly Demon Force to drive the Heavenly Demon Art's techniques.

Unless stated otherwise, variant and archetype exist independently: gaining the variant does not merge with the archetype, and thus grants no duplicate-pool cap bonus — and vice versa.

**"Not counted as an Energy Pool"**

Some pools carry the trait "not counted as an Energy Pool", and differ from other pools as follows:

Unless stated otherwise, such a pool's cap cannot be expanded through "Energy Pool Reinforcement".

Unless stated otherwise, it cannot be chosen to pay for abilities that offer a free choice of pool.

Unless stated otherwise, it cannot be bound by enhancements that require binding an Energy Pool.

Unless stated otherwise, it is unaffected by pool-attacking effects.

Unless stated otherwise, it is not rejected by other pools when judging compatibility.

In all other respects it follows the ordinary Energy Pool rules.

**Legacy resource wording**

Old resources not yet repaired or corrected contain wording of every stripe:

some miswrite Energy Pool as "energy slot" or "slot";

some forget to give a General pool its broad category;

some assign East/West categories to Special pools;

and other assorted problems may lurk besides.

If play forces the use of such a legacy resource, report it to the rulebook publisher; until the resource is refurbished, the ST interprets or amends it.

**Gauges**

A gauge is another kind of measure of a character's capabilities. A gauge is not an Energy Pool.

**Gaining gauges**

Gauges come only bundled with resources. Certain Bloodlines, Alternations, or Titles provide gauges all their own.

**Gauge caps**

Some gauges have caps — based on Stats or skills, or fixed values.

Some gauges have no cap at all.

Each is detailed on its resource's page; if not, report it to the rulebook publisher.

**Using and recovering gauges**

Some gauges arrive full to their cap; others arrive at 0 spendable, or some other figure.

A gauge-granting resource always states the gauge's use or influence, and its recovery or reduction —

each detailed on its page; if not, report it to the rulebook publisher.

**Compatibility between gauges**

Ordinarily, gauges are mutually compatible. In vanishingly rare cases, some gauge may reject others. Either way, when a player's gauges conflict, they burst apart and die — no save, unavoidably.

**Compatibility of gauges and Energy Pools**

Ordinarily, holding gauges does not impede gaining pools, nor the reverse. In vanishingly rare cases a pool may reject gauges, or a gauge reject certain pool types. Either way, when a player's gauge and pool conflict, they burst apart and die — no save, unavoidably.

**Temporary gauge-cap changes:**

Gauge caps rarely shift. Gauges keyed to Stats or skills (even Legendary Stats and skill AS) can see their caps move with temporary gains or losses of Stats and skill levels.

If you temporarily gain gauge cap — a point of Stat or Legendary Stat, say — only the cap rises, not the gauge value, unless you can recover the gauge.

E.g.: a gauge capped by Legendary AGI. A character temporarily gains 1 AGI, going from 30 to 31 — Legendary AGI rises 1, and the gauge's cap +1; but it is an empty point of cap, granting no spendable gauge.

If you suffer cap loss — losing a point of Stat or Legendary Stat — the cap drops 1, and if the current gauge value exceeds the cap, the excess is lost; a later cap recovery does not refund it.

E.g.: a gauge capped by Legendary DMR. A character temporarily loses 1 DMR, going from 16 to 15 — Legendary DMR falls 1 and the gauge cap −1; if the current gauge exceeds the cap, the excess is lost. When DMR recovers, the cap recovers — but gauge lost to the cap is not returned.

**Same-named gauges**

In vanishingly rare cases, several different enhancements may grant gauges of the same name. Unless stated otherwise, these same-named gauges exist independently, and each enhancement can only power its abilities from the gauge it itself granted. A character cannot pool same-named gauge values across enhancements.
