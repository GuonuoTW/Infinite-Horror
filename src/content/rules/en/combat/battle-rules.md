# Battle Rules

**Battle Scenes**

A battle Scene is any stretch of the story where fighting or fierce contention breaks out. In a battle Scene, characters interact in short, intense, adversarial exchanges where every action affects someone else. This usually covers battles, pursuits, ambushes, and escapes — sometimes even a heated negotiation where every second counts.

Because battle Scenes are special, we replace ordinary free-form narration with "Rounds" and "acting in order".

==================================

**Before battle begins**

A battle always involves more than one side, so the first task before battle starts is identifying opponents.

Establish each character's sensory range. A character whose greater sensory range notices the target sooner can strike at those who haven't yet noticed them — this counts as a display of hostility and means they have opened a surprise round. In the surprise round, characters who haven't noticed the target still cannot act, though a target who was hostile from the start may be able to act in the surprise round.

==================================

**Deliberate hiding**

Without special abilities, deliberate hiding has three requirements:

First, to hide deliberately you must choose targets and declare the trigger moment (choosing targets doesn't require them to be within sensory range — a character who spots a sentry 500 meters off can already choose to hide deliberately from that sentry; multiple targets are allowed).

Second, what you deliberately hide may be either the hostile act you're preparing to spring, or your own presence and movements.

Finally, for hiding intent, see the "Subterfuge skill" details; for hiding your presence, see the "Stealth skill" details.

For hiding via special abilities, see the "Detection and Locating rules".

==================================

**Sensing hostility**

Sensing hostility specifically means perceiving a target's hostile intent toward you before battle begins.

Even with an unobstructed view of the target, judging whether they are hostile — and so whether to enter battle — is difficult.

The moment either side displays hostility, both sides enter battle.

But unless side A enters side B's sensory range while already displaying hostility, side B usually cannot enter battle at the same moment they perceive side A. (B can, of course, actively declare hostility first — in which case A, who hasn't yet shown hostility, gets caught in the surprise attack instead, even if A had meant to close in and spring one on B.)

Drawing a weapon, tensing into readiness, launching an obvious attack, and so on all count as displays of hostility.

An unobvious attack may still be read as your hostility from context (with only two parties present, someone suddenly struck by an invisible fist will mostly assume the other party did it).

A character cannot sense hostility from a target they cannot locate, but can sense it from a target under a rough fix or a pinpoint.

==================================

**The surprise round**

Because of "sensory range", "deliberate hiding", and "sensing hostility", not every combatant realizes the fight has started when it starts. All units aware that battle has begun gain a small window of action over those who aren't — the surprise round. A surprise round always has an initiator.

Once the surprise round begins, battle is joined: all targets able to act in the surprise round roll Initiative (the roll carries over into the normal battle Turns), and the surprise round's action order follows the results. Specially, the initiator always acts first regardless of Initiative, that action must be the hostile act itself, and the initiator cannot use Prepare Actions or delays.

In a surprise round, a unit able to act may take only one Movement Action, or one Standard Action, or one Swift Action; Free Actions remain unlimited.

If a character is immune to surprise, they enter the surprise round regardless of whether they were successfully surprised.

Note: once battle has begun, absent special abilities, even a target who sneaks successfully again — or becomes untraceable — cannot open a new surprise round, because everyone is already on guard, unless the ST declares the battle over.

==================================

**An example of triggering a surprise round**

Character A, flanked by two minions, negotiates with character B; ninja C guards B from concealment.

A and B begin talking. C palms a shuriken and hides deliberately with his abilities; though inside the sensory range of A and both minions, none of the four notice him. He takes a Prepare Action — and because of how Prepare Actions work, he counts as having done nothing, so still nobody notices.

Mid-negotiation, A turns on B, reaching for a pistol.

A, as initiator of the surprise round, springs it by displaying hostility, spending his surprise-round action on the draw. If his two minions knew in advance that A would turn, they too may act in the surprise round; otherwise they, like B, cannot.

Suppose the minions can act.

Before A's pistol clears its holster, C's shuriken kills him — only now do the minions notice ninja C, and they carry on with their surprise-round actions.

**A sample surprise-round sequence**

*ST: You spot a Shadowfang dragon sprawled on the grass. What do you do?*

*Zimu (player — melee): My hands are suddenly itching to hunt a dragon.*

*ST: Then you enter a surprise round. One action each.*

*(Surprise round: one action per character; Free Actions uncounted.)*

*Zimu: Movement Action — I run to the dragon's north side, 2 meters out.*

*Suzi (player — support): I move to the east side, 15 meters.*

*Baiye (player — melee): West side, 2 meters.*

*Shiyue (player — ranged): South side, 10 meters.*

*(Everyone spends their action; the surprise round ends with an encirclement and no attacks.)*

*ST: As you close the ring, the dragon catches your scent. Battle begins — roll Initiative.*

*ST: Dragon Initiative: 1d10+12 = 2+12 = 14*

*Zimu: 1d10+9 = 6+9 = 15*

*Suzi: 1d10+10 = 10+10 = 20*

*Baiye: 1d10+5 = 1+5 = 6*

*Shiyue: 1d10+16 = 3+16 = 19*

*(If the surprise round didn't finish every enemy, roll Initiative and enter standard battle Turns.)*

*ST: Order of action — Suzi, Shiyue, Zimu, the dragon, Baiye.*

==================================

**Initiative**

As battle begins, every unit that hasn't rolled Initiative in a surprise round makes an Initiative roll; all combatants are ranked from highest to lowest and act in that order.

Though called a check, Initiative is not actually a check: no ability that raises check DP or results affects it, unless the ability says so specially.

The Initiative roll is 1d10 + Initiative modifier; the result is your Initiative rank.

The Initiative modifier is AGI + CMT ± relevant adjustments.

Absent special abilities, a character keeps the same Initiative rank every Turn.

If two characters tie, the higher CMT acts first; if CMT ties too, higher AGI; if still tied, roll 1d10. Two characters of the same side may simply decide between themselves.

**Changing the Initiative order**

Special circumstances can rearrange the Initiative order, handled as follows:

Changes come in two kinds — changing the Initiative value, and changing position.

For value changes, record the new Initiative:

If Initiative rises (acting earlier): if the new value falls after the current acting position in this Turn's order, the unit acts normally when reached. If it falls before the current acting position, the unit is skipped for a Turn and acts normally next Turn.

(E.g.: five combatants A/B/C/D/E at Initiative 25/20/15/10/5. C gains 6, reaching 21 — the order becomes A, C, B, D, E. If A holds the acting position, then after A comes C, who acts normally. But if the change lands when B already holds the acting position, then since C's new slot lies before B, it takes effect only next Turn — C skips a full Round.)

If Initiative falls (acting later): note whether the unit has acted this Turn. If not, they act normally when their new slot comes up; if they already acted, they are simply skipped.

(E.g.: A/B/C/D/E at 25/20/15/10/5. C drops 6 to 9 — the order becomes A, B, D, C, E. If B holds the acting position: after B comes D, then C, who hasn't acted this Turn and so acts normally. But if the change lands when D holds the acting position, then after D comes C — who has already acted this Turn, so is skipped, passing straight to E.)

Changing position follows the same rules as changing values.

**Delaying Initiative**

At the start of your own Round, you may resolve and do nothing and voluntarily lower your Initiative to let others go first. Declare an Initiative number no greater than your current rank, then reslot yourself by the new rank. You count as not yet having acted this Turn. This is called delaying.

**Joining a battle already underway**

Sometimes units join mid-battle, as a third party or as reinforcements for one side.

All units joining mid-battle roll Initiative and, from the next Turn (after the last unit in the current order finishes its Round), slot themselves into the order by their results and fight.

Joining mid-battle produces no surprise round — everyone on the field already knows the battle is on.

==================================

**Actions in battle Scenes**

All the action notes below assume two defaults:

1. No special abilities (some abilities temporarily or permanently change an act's required action).

2. Items already sit in handy places (a sheathed weapon at the waist or back, a quiver slung on, throwing knives racked neatly in a belt pouch, and so on).

**Free Actions**

Talking, working a bolt, thumbing back a hammer — absent special significance, all Free Actions.

Shoving or kicking aside an effortless obstacle while moving (an empty carton in the way, weeds on the path) is a Free Action. What counts as effortless is the ST's call — something that looks kickable but is actually heavy may hurt the character (that empty-looking carton with a stake under it).

**Reflex Actions**

Dropping prone from standing.

**Swift Actions**

Working the bolt of a single-action rifle, and the like.

Drawing a small item stowed for quick access — a test tube in a shirt's breast pocket, a dart from a dart pouch.

**Movement Actions**

Making one move, up to a distance equal to your speed.

Controlling a vehicle or steering a mount under normal conditions.

Jumping or climbing a stretch. Depending on circumstances, a short jump or a low climb can ride inside a move — including the flashier stunts, like leaping for the chandelier and swinging across the table.

Drawing a weapon from its sheath. Unslinging a bow from your back. Taking something from a pocket.

Swapping an automatic pistol's magazine. Loading a single-shot rifle. Loading a revolver with a speedloader. Loading a light crossbow's bolt. Cocking a light crossbow. Loading one shell into a cannon.

Opening a closed door.

Drinking a mouthful of water (or other liquid). Eating a bite of easily chewed food or a small dose of medicine.

Guarding.

Picking up an item.

Rising from the ground.

**Standard Actions**

Making one attack. Any melee or ranged attack — unarmed, weapon, thrown, bow, gunfire — is a Standard Action.

Taking an item from a belt pouch.

Casting a spell or using a special ability — unless stated otherwise, a Standard Action.

Stowing a weapon. (Meaning putting it away; opening your hands and letting it fall is a Free Action.)

==================================

**Judging attack types in battle**

Some resources specify whether a skill is melee or ranged — and some specify nothing. Handle it so:

If the effect's wording says melee, it is a melee attack, and unless stated otherwise, reach and other melee-related abilities apply by default.

If the wording says ranged, it is a ranged attack, and unless stated otherwise, range and other ranged-related abilities apply by default.

If the wording says neither, it is neither a melee nor a ranged attack; such a resource's area, range, and reach are specified individually in the resource.

Generally, any act with the word "attack" in a resource is an attack, in these broad types: melee attack, ranged attack, casting attack. Finer splits include melee Brawl attacks, ranged Brawl attacks, melee Blade attacks, ranged Blade attacks, gunfire, archery, melee firearm attacks, ranged casting attacks, melee casting attacks, and so on.

In every case, effects keyed to "attacks" apply in full.

E.g.: "attack checks gain a 6 DP Reinforcement Bonus" — every act bearing the word "attack" gains it.

==================================

**Judging attacks**

When an ability or act contains the word "attack", the act is an attack.

When it works on 【one target】, subtract the target's defense from the DP; if it names no target and hits everything in an area, no defense is subtracted.

Special: checks like STR + Blade or AGI + Throwing per se do not subtract defense.

**Attacking everyone in an area separately**

Attacking every target in an area separately is not an AOE.

Attacking targets in an area separately means a separate attack check per target.

Using such abilities, you may still only attack targets you have located.

The attack remains, in essence, single-target — so it still cannot affect swarm creatures.

**Notes on area damage**

Generally, unless stated otherwise, Tech-category and Natural-category area damage strikes friend and foe alike, self included; supernatural-category area damage, unless stated otherwise, spares the user standing inside the area but affects everyone else.

Generally, saves against area damage can take certain bonuses, and Armor Defense, Shield Defense, and Born Defense may all be added to saves against area damage.

Certain properties of area damage also bite into those bonuses:

High Speed: if the area-damage ability has a High Speed effect, targets take a 1 DP penalty on their Reflex Saves against it — the penalty applies only to the Stat-and-skill portion of the save. Ignoring the High Speed advantage negates this as usual (likewise below).

Breach: if the area-damage ability has Breach and the target's Reflex Save carries armor, shield, or Born Defense bonuses, Breach breaks through those defenses as normal.

Dispelling: if it has Dispelling and the target's Reflex Save carries bonuses from supernatural abilities, Dispelling breaks through as normal.

Mighty: if it has Mighty and the target's Reflex Save carries guard-derived bonuses, Mighty breaks through that portion as normal.

Area damage based on an attack check or a weapon, unless stated otherwise, does not inherit the attack's or weapon's High Speed, Breach, Dispelling, or Mighty.

==================================

**Melee attacks**

Base reach: natural-weapon Size / 2 (at least 1) + blade-weapon Size / 3.

**Brawl attacks**

You make one ordinary melee physical attack with a natural weapon against one target within your reach.

A Standard Action.

The Brawl attack check is STR + Brawl skill level + natural-weapon damage − target's defense ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, with the damage's type and grade set by the natural weapon used.

The Brawl attack's damage cap is STR + Brawl skill level + natural-weapon damage ± relevant adjustments.

Unless stated otherwise, an ordinary creature's fist, foot, and claw natural weapons have Size equal to the creature's Size / 5; teeth, Size / 50; tail, Size / 3.

Unless stated otherwise, an ordinary humanoid's natural weapons are its two fists (claws, elbows included) and two legs (feet included).

For non-humanoids, a tail also counts among natural weapons (several tails count as one), as do teeth (several mouths or teeth count as one).

**Blade attacks**

You make one ordinary melee physical attack with a blade weapon against one target within your reach.

A Standard Action.

The Blade attack check is STR + Blade skill level + blade-weapon damage − target's defense ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, type and grade set by the blade weapon used.

The Blade attack's damage cap is STR + Blade skill level + blade-weapon damage ± relevant adjustments.

==================================

**Ranged attacks**

Note: a target whose position you cannot perceive (under total cover, beyond your sight, or otherwise) cannot be attacked even inside your range — you have no line of sight.

**Ranged throwing attacks**

You make one ordinary ranged physical attack with a throwing weapon against one target in range.

A Standard Action.

The light-throwing attack check is AGI + Athletics skill level + weapon damage − target's defense − distance penalty ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, type and grade set by the weapon thrown.

The heavy-throwing attack check is STR + Athletics skill level + weapon damage − target's defense − distance penalty ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, type and grade set by the weapon thrown.

The throwing attack's damage cap is STR + Athletics skill level + weapon damage ± relevant adjustments.

Every throwing weapon has a base range; if none (improvised weapons), the default base range is 10 meters.

A throw's maximum distance is base range × STR × 1.

The distance penalty: each full base range beyond the first costs the attack check 2 DP.

If you fall short of the weapon's minimum STR requirement, each missing point costs 2 DP Machinery Penalty on the attack; 3 or more short and the weapon is unusable. And each base range exceeded costs 2 DP more of distance penalty.

**Ranged bow attacks**

You make one ordinary ranged physical attack with bow and arrow against one target in range.

A Standard Action.

The bow attack check is AGI + Archery skill level + weapon damage − target's defense − distance penalty ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, type and grade set by the weapon used.

The bow attack's damage cap is Archery skill level + weapon damage × 2 + the weapon's STR requirement.

A bow's maximum range is 8 times base range.

The distance penalty: each full base range beyond the first costs the check 2 DP.

Falling short of the weapon's minimum STR: each missing point costs 2 DP Machinery Penalty on the attack and shortens the maximum distance by one range unit, and each base range exceeded costs 2 DP more. Short by 3 or more, the weapon is unusable — you may simply be unable to draw a stiff bow.

Bows are strictly two-handed; you cannot use a bow one-handed.

**Ranged firearm attacks**

You make one ordinary ranged physical attack with a firearm (or crossbow) not classified "Cannon" against one target in range.

A Standard Action.

The firearm attack check is AGI + Firearms skill level + weapon damage − target's defense − distance penalty ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, type and grade set by the weapon.

The firearm attack's damage cap is Firearms skill level + weapon damage × 2.

A firearm's maximum range is 5 times base range.

The distance penalty: each full base range beyond the first costs the check 2 DP.

**Ranged cannon attacks**

You make one ordinary ranged physical attack with a firearm classified "Cannon" against one target in range.

A Standard Action.

The cannon attack check is INT + Firearms skill level + weapon damage − target's defense − distance penalty ± relevant adjustments; each success deals 1 damage, type and grade set by the weapon.

The cannon attack's damage cap is Firearms skill level + weapon damage × 2.

A cannon's maximum range is 4 times base range.

Specially, cannon-class weapons generally carry the **[Bombardment]** property; to fire a bombardment, see the "Item Inscriptions" entry.

**Aiming at an enemy and attacking**

A Full-turn Action.

Ranged attacks may generally aim under this rule, but only at targets within 3 base ranges and inside your sensory range. Afterward you make one ranged attack at the target, gaining a 1 DP aiming bonus and +1 damage cap on it.

You may forgo attacking to keep aiming over several Rounds, attacking when the aim ends for greater gains: each Round aimed grants 1 DP aiming bonus and +1 damage cap, to a maximum of 5 DP and +5 damage cap.

If you take any other Standard or Movement Action before firing, or are struck by a melee attack, the benefit is lost.

==================================

**Defensive actions**

These rules describe actions that raise your defenses.

**Full defense**

A Standard Action.

Straining every nerve to evade your enemies, until you next take another Standard Action your Evade Defense gains an Evade bonus equal to your Base Defense, and your saves against area damage gain a DP Evade bonus equal to your Base Defense.

Additionally, if you can see an enemy attacking with firearms, you are unaffected by the **[High Speed]** their weapon brings to the attack.

Maintaining this effect requires Focusing.

**Brawl guard**

A Movement Action.

Until the start of your next Round, you gain a guard bonus on defense equal to your Brawl skill level.

If your natural weapon carries the **[Guard]** property, you may add the natural weapon's Size (see Brawl attacks for natural-weapon Size) to the guard bonus; that portion of defense is called Shield Defense.

**Blade guard**

A Movement Action.

Until the start of your next Round, you gain a guard bonus on defense equal to your Blade skill level. You must hold a blade weapon to guard.

If the weapon carries **[Guard]**, you may add its Size to the guard bonus; that portion of defense is called Shield Defense.

==================================

**Seeking cover**

Seeking cover is a sound defensive move. In battle, a character can prop movable objects before themselves, duck behind trees and walls, or take concealment from smoke and cloud — good for hiding on the one hand, and for avoiding attacks on the other, ranged attacks especially.

When a target is attacked, first judge whether they have cover: draw a line from any point of the attacker's position to any point of the defender's — if something covering lies on it, the target counts as covered. Cover is thus usually directional, applying against one direction only.

With cover, a character gains cover defense, set by their build and the cover's bulk: cover's Size − your Size + 4, capped at 8. Cover defense of 2 counts as slight cover; 4 as partial cover; 8 as heavy cover. When the covering object reaches twice your Size, it is total cover.

The same amount also applies as a Covered Bonus on Reflex Saves against area damage.

A character may also haul or pick up objects to prop before themselves as cover (picking up an item is a Movement Action). If they can carry the cover and can still move this Round (via some Swift-Action movement ability, or a Standard Action spent moving), they may move under cover, object in tow.

Different objects also cover differently. Broadly, an object must be able to (in the real-world sense) block the character to grant cover at all. Some objects — a Size-4 spear, say — cover far less efficiently than others, or not at all; likewise, cover may vary when the object cannot fully screen the character. The ST rules these cases.

Special: bracing an object with **[Guard]** is guarding, not cover — it grants Guard Defense and Shield Defense, not cover defense.

Special: when a target has the means to locate and attack you around total cover, you do not count as totally covered against it — area attacks, or radar locks paired with **[Penetrating]** fire that breaks your cover and carries through to wound you.

Special: cloud, smoke, and other passable screens cover you only by blocking sight, so apart from total cover, they grant no other cover benefit.

Total cover normally also keeps the target ignorant of your exact position — though given the cover's extent, enemies can still roughly bound where you might be.

If the total-cover effect comes from passable screens like cloud banks or smoke: a target with means of seeing through them (blindsight, say) locates and attacks you normally; or a target may simply attack the spot it guesses you occupy — and if you happen to be there, it affects you as under a rough fix.

**Shooting from behind cover**

Leaning out from cover, making a ranged attack, and ducking back behind it.

Your DP takes a penalty of half the cover defense you enjoy; under total cover, no attack can be made.

==================================

**Special attack actions**

Special attack actions replace a direct attack.

**Charge attacks**

A Whole-round Action.

You first spend a Movement Action to close on the enemy, then make one melee attack. Count the straight-line distance moved before reaching them: each meter grants 1 DP Reinforcement Bonus on the attack check and costs your Base Defense 1 point.

This cannot grant a bonus above your STR, nor push your Base Defense below 0. Your charge cannot exceed your speed.

A charge must run straight, along a clear, unobstructed path between you and the target — nothing may hinder the movement (difficult terrain, obstacles). "Clear, unobstructed path" means: first, you must be able to move into the nearest square from which you can strike the target (if that spot is occupied or blocked, you cannot charge); second, if any line from any point of your starting square to any point of your ending square crosses a square that blocks movement, slows movement, or is occupied by a creature, you cannot charge (helpless creatures do not obstruct a charge).

If no line of sight connects you and the target at the start of your Turn, you cannot charge that target.

Special: in a surprise round, a character may charge as a Standard Action, but the maximum distance drops to half their speed.

Special: if some ability lets your charge be limited only by your STR cap, you may exceed the Base Defense cap — the excess becomes an Unnamed Penalty on defense.

Special: if some ability spares the character the charge's penalties, the charge's maximum DP cap, unless stated otherwise, is still bounded by Base Defense and STR.

**All-out attack**

A Standard Action; once per Round, however many Standard Actions you hold.

You make one melee attack, gaining a Reinforcement Bonus of half your STR, capped by your Base Defense; you then lose an equal amount of Base Defense for 1 Round.

If, after an all-out attack, your Base Defense somehow drops to 0 or below, you lose Evade Defense, Insight Defense, and Guard Defense with it.

E.g.: Base Defense 5, Guard Defense 3, STR 7. Your all-out attack takes a 3 DP Reinforcement Bonus, leaving Base Defense 2. The target has High Speed 3, dropping your Base Defense to 0 — and because the all-out attack caused it, you lose your Guard Defense too. The target's High Speed, after consuming your remaining Base Defense, has 1 point left over for whatever abilities it may trigger.

**Trips**

A trip is an attack that costs someone their footing.

A Standard Action.

You make a Brawl attack, treated as a No-Tier touch attack dealing no damage. On a hit, the opponent contests with AGI + Athletics (Balance) or STR + Athletics (Balance); if you win, they fall prone. A flying target's Mobility drops 1 grade — from clumsy, it simply plummets; perfect Mobility or hovering is unaffected. The target also takes stall points equal to the margin; the Mobility drop and stall points last 1 Round. In this attack, each point of the target's Size above yours costs you a 1 DP Unnamed Penalty.

Certain artificial blade weapons permit trip attacks — their entries say so — in which case it becomes a Blade attack.

**Slams**

A slam is a violent collision you initiate — a shoulder-check, a smashing forearm.

A Standard Action; specially, it may also ride within a charge attack.

With one attack, declare "knock back" or "knock down". Each point of the target's Size above yours costs you a 1 DP Unnamed Penalty.

On a hit it deals no damage; instead roll STR + Brawl (Slam), which the opponent contests with STR + Athletics (Balance) to withstand you or AGI + Athletics (Balance) to slip aside (or at least bleed off the impact).

If you win and chose knock down, the target is floored, entering the prone state.

If you win and chose knock back, each point of margin drives them back one meter.

Also, if you chose knock back, you may push them along for the margin in meters — or until your speed runs out, whichever is less — when: you can still move with a Movement Action, or you slammed off a charge attack with movement to spare.

Specially, if your opponent withstands your slam, they may spend a Reflex Action to slam you back, and you contest at a 2 DP Unnamed Penalty.

Specially, certain natural or artificial weapons permit slam attacks; you may then add the weapon's damage to the active slam roll (even with an artificial weapon, the roll still uses Brawl).

**Overruns**

A Movement Action, or a combat move launched as part of a charge.

An overrun means barreling or slipping through a target by force or finesse.

Check with STR + Athletics (Overrun) or AGI + Athletics (Overrun); the target contests with the higher of STR and AGI. Win, and you pass through them and keep moving. Lose, and the overrun fails — you halt in front of the target and the Movement Action ends by force.

**Feints**

A feint is a false move to deceive an opponent.

A Standard Action.

Roll INT + Subterfuge (Feint), contested by the target's PCT + Empathy (Reading People). Win, and your next melee attack against them strips all their Base Defense and Evade Defense. The attack must come before the end of your next Turn.

If the opponent spends a Whole-round Action to reset before you act, the benefit is lost.

**Grapples**

A grapple is a "seize the opponent" move.

Make one melee Brawl attack; on a hit it deals no damage — instead roll STR + Brawl (Grapple), and your opponent contests with STR + Brawl (Grapple) to break loose or AGI + Athletics (Escape) to slip free (the grapple contest). If your natural weapon suits seizing (biting jaws, claws, tentacles, a barbed tail), add its weapon damage to the roll. Win, and you hold them fast: you are mutually grappling, and the target is grappled.

With several grapplers, all are mutually grappling and the target is grappled.

In grapple contests, the smaller party takes a penalty equal to the Size gap. Against an especially large target, you may cling to it instead of grappling; see the "Miscellaneous Rules" page.

Some flexible weapons can grapple; check with Blade instead of Brawl.

In this state, both you and your opponent lose Base Defense against third parties, and the opponent takes a penalty equal to your margin on attacks, guards, and special abilities needing limb movement. Any attack on either of you has even odds of striking either (roll 1d10: 1–5 you, 6–10 the grappled target), shifted by your Size gap — each point the target's Size exceeds yours adds 1, each 3 points adds 2, and so on. (You Size 5, target 6: 1d10, 1–4 you, 5–10 target. You Size 5, target 8: 1d10, 1–3 you, 4–10 target.)

In this state, third parties may still grapple either of you, checked under the normal grapple rules.

In a grapple, anyone except the grappled target may start moving; doing so automatically leaves the grapple, then move normally. To haul the others along: if any other grappling party objects, contest a grapple against each objector — lose, and you cannot; win, and you may move everyone in the grapple, at no more than half speed.

E.g.: A grapples B; C also grapples B; C means to hurl B away and A objects — C contests A. If C wins and A won't release B, C may hurl A and B together.

Note: a ranged grapple involves no crossfire risk, and you lose no Base Defense against third parties. Defense is computed separately per attack; in a ranged grapple, being bigger than the grappled target grants you no bonus, while a bigger target gains its bonus as usual.

If some ability extends your reach past 3 meters and you grapple at the extended reach successfully, check whether the reach persists: if it does, treat it as a ranged grapple; if it lapses, resolve as though you forcibly drew the target to your side, judged by the movement rules within the grapple rules.

The grappled target may spend a Standard Action on their own Round to contest and break free, the grappler contesting passively.

A grappler may release the grappled target at any time.

You may attack an opponent inside your grapple, but only with natural weapons or an artificial melee weapon of Size no greater than your Size − 3. To do so, first win a grapple contest. Then attack with STR + Brawl, each success dealing 1 Blunt damage — even with an artificial weapon, the roll uses Brawl, and the damage is Blunt no matter what. A Whole-round Action.

Starting a grapple is an attack check: if the target departs via Reflex Action, it still resolves as though the target left your attack range — and on a hit and successful grapple, the target's move fails and no movement happens. If the opponent knocks you back, your attack still resolves as though they left your range — and on a hit and successful grapple, you caught them before being driven back: the target follows you the knockback distance and remains grappled. Such forced movement needs no check under the grapple rules' movement clause.

**Pins**

A pin is a "keep the opponent from acting" move.

You and the target must be mutually grappling with the target grappled; you may then spend a Standard Action to start a pin check: STR + Brawl (Grapple), which the opponent contests with STR + Brawl (Grapple) to break loose or AGI + Athletics (Escape) to slip free (the pin contest). Win, and you pin them fast: mutually pinning, the target pinned.

With several pinners, all are mutually pinning and the target is pinned.

In pin contests, the smaller party takes a penalty equal to the Size gap.

If the mutual grapple came through a flexible weapon, check with Blade instead of Brawl.

A pinned target cannot attack or move. You may carry them at half speed; all else as above. If your margin exceeds the opponent's STR, you may use their body as active cover.

The pinned target may spend a Standard Action on their own Round to contest free of the pin, the pinner contesting passively; success downgrades the pin to a grapple.

While pinning, the pin's victor may attack their pinned target as normal.

**Binding**

Binding is a "truss up the opponent" move.

You may bind only helpless or willing targets, and you need materials — rope and the like.

Spend 1 minute on an INT + Larceny (Binding) check and record the successes: they set the DC for escaping the bonds.

You may reinforce: another minute and another INT + Larceny (Binding) check adds half its successes to the DC.

You may keep reinforcing, each pass adding half again fewer successes, until none remain.

To escape, the target makes AGI + Athletics (Escape) checks on 1-minute periods; when accumulated successes exceed the DC, they're free.

A bound target rendered unable to act (mummy-wrapped, or lashed whole-body to a chair) counts as helpless.

**Throwing a target**

Throwing is hurling a target you have grappled.

You must have the target grappled — pinning is not required.

As a Standard Action, hurl one grappled target: roll STR + Athletics (Heavy Throwing Weapons), the target rolling (higher of AGI/STR) + Athletics (Balance). Each success throws the target 1 meter; each point of the target's Size above yours costs you 1 DP in the contest.

If an obstacle stands behind the thrown target: hardness above 2, and the target takes the remaining meters as Lethal physical damage, treated as falling damage (fall-shortening abilities apply; targets immune to falling damage are immune to this too). Hardness 2 or below, and target and obstacle both take the remaining meters as Blunt physical damage, likewise treated as falling damage.

Note: throwing someone presupposes the strength for it — check carrying capacity before rolling. Either way, once this check is made, your grapple or pin ends immediately.

**Called shots**

A called shot targets a vital spot on the enemy's body.

Called shots are generally contained within an attack action, so they can ride inside skills and Techniques.

Depending on the spot, the attack takes some Unnamed Penalty; if the defender is aware of the attack and retains Base Defense (neither flat-footed nor held), they gain an Evade bonus on defense equal to your penalty.

You can only call shots against targets inside your sensory range (usually sight).

Different spots carry different effects. Some examples:

Head: −4 DP; every 2 damage inflicts 1 stun point; Fortitude save, each success saving 1 stun.

Eyes: −4 DP; every 2 damage inflicts 1 dazzle point; Fortitude save, each success saving 1 dazzle.

Abdomen: −4 DP; every 2 damage inflicts 1 nausea point; Fortitude save, each success saving 1 nausea.

Ribs or bridge of the nose: −4 DP; every 2 damage inflicts 1 agony point; Fortitude save, each success saving 1 agony.

Striking the weapon (disarm): −3 DP, no damage; the target contests with STR + Blade — on a loss, an artificial non-Brawl weapon flies loose and lands. Attacking with a hand or similar grasping natural weapon, you may seize the weapon instead. (Disarming is not a vital-spot attack.)

Sundering the weapon: −3 DP, no damage; the target contests with STR + Blade — +4 DP if wielding the weapon two-handed, −4 DP if you hold the "Weapon Finesse" Feat for it. The larger party gains a bonus equal to the Size gap. Each success deals 1 damage to the weapon or shield, which must breach the item's Hardness. (Sundering is not a vital-spot attack.)

Throat: −6 DP; the target Fortitude-saves the damage taken. Each point of your margin upgrades 1 point of this attack's damage one grade (Blunt — Lethal — Aggravated).

Temple: −12 DP; the target Fortitude-saves the damage taken. Each point of your margin upgrades 1 point of this attack's damage — or of damage already on the target — one grade (Blunt — Lethal — Aggravated).

Note: natural weapons are immune to sundering and disarming.

Note: against a target holding several weapons, sunder and disarm must pick one.

Note: no Negative Status points from called shots stack.

Note: if the target sits in your rough perception range, or you cannot pinpoint them, you cannot call shots against them.

**Coup de grâce**

When the target is unconscious, asleep, petrified, helpless, or the like, a character may declare an attack on them a coup de grâce. This is not an action — it is contained in the attack — so it can ride inside skills and Techniques.

It has no effect on creatures without vitals (slimes, constructs) or on objects; for some creatures you must first find the vitals with a scouting check (or another, ST's choice). For some finely built machines and robots, beheading or piercing the central processor kills just as well, so they count as having vitals and a coup de grâce may be declared.

In this attack, all attack DP counts as successes, with no + Dice.

A coup de grâce still subtracts the target's effective defense from the DP, and remains bound by the damage cap.

For the states involved, see the "Negative Statuses" page.

**Assorted other rules**

**Assisting**

Choose someone to aid, then take a Prepare Action whose content is: when they next take a specified action, assist.

When they take that action, the assist lands: you make the same kind of check, and they gain a DP Perfect Bonus equal to your successes on theirs.

If they never take the specified action, your Prepare Action never fires.

You can only assist skill checks — climbing, swimming, grappling, and the like. You cannot assist defense, attacks, Will, or similar checks.

**Multiple special effects**

A weapon with several special effects: in the ordinary case of dealing damage once, all of them apply.

Where damage comes from multiple mixed munitions or the like: if even one round among them lacks a special effect, the damage carries none; if all carry different ones, the ST picks one to apply.

**Damage overflow**

When a character takes more than their remaining HP can bear, the overflowing damage isn't tallied — but the fact of overflow is.

E.g.: a character with 2 Healthy HP takes 5 Lethal damage. Their HP fills with 2 Aggravated damage, the extra 1 uncounted — but the overflow is on record.

Because some special cases need the overflow judged — Diehard, say.

Some resources, where stated, do count overflow damage.

**Inner methods, movement arts, and protective auras**

Abilities spending the same action cannot be used together — spend a Standard Action on an 【all-out attack】 and you cannot also trigger Baji Quan's 【Inch Force】 (both cost a Standard Action).

Beyond that, abilities marked as inner methods, movement arts, or protective auras cannot be active simultaneously with one another even on different actions (Nine-Luminary Ultraspeed and Sensory Combat, say). But within one Turn you may separately use an inner method, a movement art, and a protective aura.

Movement arts: qinggong, the Nanaya body technique, and the like — ways of governing bodily movement; one at a time.

Protective auras: ordinarily combinable with any movement art, Technique, or skill.

Inner methods: usually passive, stacking with everything — subject still to the other action-stacking rules.

**Pulling your blow**

When you want to act, but gently:

declare before the check how many successes you forgo;

then roll as normal, subtracting the declared successes from the result.

**Waiving defense**

When an opponent means to strike you and you'd rather not resist:

you may waive any of Base Defense, Evade Defense, Insight Defense, and Guard Defense — and decline to use defense AS.

**Multiple-attack penalty**

The multiple-attack penalty means that each attack you take in the current Turn opens gaps in your defense, handing the next attacker an opening.

Generally, each attack you take within a Turn costs your Guard Defense 1 point of multiple-attack defense penalty. With Guard Defense at 0, it eats Evade Defense next; Evade exhausted, Base Defense; at most all three are drained. With Base Defense at zero, the defense AS from Legendary AGI and Legendary PCT vanish entirely.

The attack needn't deal damage — needn't even hit: merely being attacked distracts your defense enough.

**The standard flow of an attack**

Following an earlier example, a summary of one attack's standard flow. Bracketed portions replay the ST's and PCs' inner arithmetic.

*ST: Battle begins — roll Initiative.*

*ST: Shadowfang dragon: 1d10+12 = 2+12 = 14*

*Zimu (melee): 1d10+9 = 6+9 = 15*

*Suzi (support): 1d10+10 = 10+10 = 20*

*Baiye (melee): 1d10+5 = 1+5 = 6*

*Shiyue (ranged): 1d10+16 = 3+16 = 19*

*ST: Order — Suzi, Shiyue, Zimu, the dragon, Baiye.*

*Suzi: I cast Creature Enhancement. (Rolls) 20 dice +3 = 8+3 = 11 successes. I give Zimu +10 STR for 1 Round. Then I guard with my dagger. Round over.*

*(This player casts, following the casting rules; the Movement Action goes to guarding.)*

*Shiyue: I shoot the dragon with my pistol. My attack DP is 18, carrying High Speed 5. I'll pay 1 Willpower for 3 more DP — 21 total. I'm using 3 AS on this attack.*

*(The player declares attack mode, the starting pool, and the attack's special properties.)*

*ST: (This dragon's defense is Base 8 + Evade 1 + Insight 1 + Born 6 + Deflection 2 — 18 defense and 2 AS. High Speed 5 breaks 5 Base Defense, leaving 13; 21 DP minus 13 is 8.) Your attack pool is 8 DP. The dragon will use 2 defense AS.*

*(The ST derives the PC's attack pool from the enemy's defense; the defender declares defense AS before the roll.)*

*Shiyue: (Rolls) 8 dice = 6. Six base successes, minus 2 defense AS, plus my 3 attack AS — 7 final successes.*

*(Roll the attack dice, apply the AS both ways, settle the final successes.)*

*ST: (The dragon has 20 HP total and just took 7L — now 13 Healthy + 7L.) You squeeze the trigger and punch a finger-width hole of blood into the dragon. The jet-black scales do nothing to stop your bullet; bright red blood runs from the wound. The dragon bellows in pain, its movements barely troubled.*

*(The ST tallies the damage and narrates it.)*

*Shiyue: (From that description, the dragon likely lacks 【Bulletproof】. And if I really dealt 7L, "movements barely troubled" suggests my damage is still a modest share of its total.) Round over.*

*(The PC infers the damage dealt from the ST's narration and proceeds.)*

(Excerpted from the session log "Chronicle of the Lord God", edited and polished.)

Unless PCs obtain the relevant intelligence in the story or hold particular abilities, they are not entitled to know how the enemies they face compose their defenses.

Likewise, without matching abilities or intelligence, PCs shouldn't know how much maximum HP their enemies hold.

The ST may, of course, simply read out the enemy's defenses to lighten their own load — either way, the choice is the ST's.
